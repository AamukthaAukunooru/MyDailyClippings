## The Dawn of Nuclear Clocks: A New Era of Precision

For decades, physicists have pursued a seemingly impossible goal: to build a clock that uses an atom's nucleus as its timekeeper, rather than the orbiting electrons. This week, that aspiration became a reality, with researchers demonstrating the world's first functioning **nuclear clocks** based on the rare isotope **thorium-229**. This monumental achievement could usher in an era of unprecedented precision timekeeping and open new avenues for exploring fundamental physics, including the elusive nature of dark matter.

Two independent teams, one in Europe led by physicist Luca Toscani De Col of the Technical University of Vienna, and another in China, have successfully built these devices. Both teams constructed their clocks around thorium-229 nuclei embedded in calcium fluoride crystals, using vacuum-ultraviolet laser light for interrogation. The European team's device operated as a complete standalone clock, continuously stabilizing a laser frequency using the thorium nucleus. They further demonstrated its long-term operation and stability by comparing it against an established ytterbium-ion atomic clock. Crucially, these early nuclear clocks have already been used to place constraints on some dark matter models, with expectations for improved sensitivity as the technology matures.

Unlike many optical atomic clocks that require ultracold atoms suspended in a vacuum, these thorium nuclei remain stable within a solid material at room temperature. This inherent robustness could eventually lead to compact, practical clocks useful for applications like advanced navigation systems, telecommunication networks, and data synchronization. While it may take many years for thorium clocks to rival today's best optical atomic clocks in terms of absolute precision, the scientific insights gained on this pathway promise to be invaluable. Thorsten Schumm, a professor of quantum metrology at the Vienna University of Technology, expressed the profound significance, stating it's "beautiful how a very 'wild' idea such as manipulating an atomic nucleus with a laser has turned into reality".

## Quantum Leaps: New States, Qubits, and Orbital Labs

The quantum computing landscape is buzzing with activity, marked by several exciting developments across different fronts. Oxford physicists have recently created an entirely new type of **Schrödinger's cat-like quantum state**, leveraging components that are themselves highly quantum in nature. This advance, detailed in a study by researchers at the University of Oxford, could unlock possibilities for more resilient quantum computers and provide deeper insights into the fundamental rules of the quantum universe. Unlike previous cat states built from classical wave packets, these new superpositions are constructed from highly nonclassical quantum components, offering a higher degree of control and paving the way for quantum computing beyond traditional binary systems. Dr. Raghavendra Srinivas, who supervised the work, noted that the team believes they are "still scratching the surface of what's possible".

Adding to the momentum, quantum computing company EeroQ announced a world-first breakthrough in **electron qubits floating on helium**. Published in *Nature Physics*, their peer-reviewed results demonstrate the strong coupling of a microwave photon to the charge qubit state of a single electron on helium. This landmark achievement establishes the electron-on-helium (eHe) approach as a viable seventh candidate in the global race to build a practical quantum computer, an approach long recognized for its potential to combine exceptional qubit quality with rapid scalability due to its compatibility with CMOS technology.

Meanwhile, QuEra Computing has unveiled **Libra**, its first **fault-tolerant quantum computer**, slated to arrive on Amazon Braket in 2028. This system is projected to feature over 256 error-corrected logical qubits and a logical error rate of 10⁻⁶, offering cloud access to fault-tolerant quantum computing for early commercial and research workflows. This announcement, alongside an expanded collaboration with Amazon Web Services (AWS), underscores the accelerating push toward more reliable and powerful quantum systems.

Even in orbit, quantum science is advancing. Astronauts aboard the International Space Station (ISS) have switched on NASA's newly upgraded **Cold Atom Lab (CAL)**. This one-of-a-kind facility, roughly the size of a minifridge, chills atoms to temperatures just above absolute zero, forming **Bose-Einstein condensates** (BECs), a fifth state of matter. The microgravity environment of space allows CAL to achieve science impossible on Earth, enabling researchers to explore fundamental workings of matter and develop new quantum technologies. Jason Williams, project scientist for CAL at NASA's Jet Propulsion Laboratory, emphasized that at these extreme colds, "ultracold matter can behave in ways that are not only unexpected, but that also enable extremely precise measurements of time, gravity, and motion".

## Dark Energy's Reign Continues: Universe Still Accelerating

In cosmology, a major new analysis has definitively reaffirmed one of modern cosmology's most profound and perplexing discoveries: the universe is not only expanding, but its expansion is **accelerating**. This finding strengthens the case for **dark energy**, the mysterious force believed to be driving this cosmic acceleration, and effectively addresses recent claims that suggested this acceleration might be an observational illusion.

The debate emerged in late 2025 when some astronomers suggested that the evidence for dark energy was weakening, raising the possibility that the universe's expansion might no longer be speeding up due to flaws in analyzing supernova data. However, a new international analysis, published in the *Monthly Notices of the Royal Astronomical Society*, revisited these arguments. Researchers re-examined the data and found that proposed biases in stellar brightness were already accounted for in current cosmological models and did not alter the expansion rate derived from observations.

The study, which included Nobel Prize-winning astrophysicists Professor Adam Riess and Professor Brian Schmidt, concluded that the universe continues to behave exactly as current cosmological models predict. Lead author Dr. Phil Wiseman of the University of Southampton noted that the controversy stemmed from a misunderstanding of the data. This robust reaffirmation of cosmic acceleration, consistent across multiple independent datasets and correction methods, underscores the enduring presence and significance of dark energy in shaping the fate of our universe.
