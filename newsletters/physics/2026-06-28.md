## The LHC's Four-Year Transformation: A Deeper Dive into Dark Matter

In a momentous event for particle physics, the **Large Hadron Collider (LHC)**, the 27-kilometer proton-smashing ring at CERN near Geneva, began a four-year shutdown this Monday for an ambitious upgrade. This isn't merely a maintenance break, but a profound transformation designed to birth the **High-Luminosity LHC (HL-LHC)**. The goal is to dramatically boost its collision capacity by a factor of 10, enabling scientists to collect up to 100 times more data than before.

When the HL-LHC commences operations in June 2030, it will usher in a new era of discovery, primarily focused on unraveling the mysteries of **dark matter** and further scrutinizing the elusive **Higgs boson**. Scientists believe that ordinary matter constitutes only about five percent of the universe, with dark matter and dark energy making up the remaining 95 percent, components that have yet to be directly detected. The enhanced luminosity will allow for the observation of between 140 and 200 collisions each time two particle packets meet, a significant leap from the current 60. This increased collision rate is crucial for observing rare phenomena and making precision measurements of particle properties, potentially revealing cracks in the **Standard Model** of particle physics. As HL-LHC project chief Markus Zerlauth aptly stated, "We still have lots of physics questions without answers. There are still many discoveries to be made". The upgrade, costing an estimated 1.2 billion Swiss francs, involves replacing components in 1.2 kilometers of the tunnel and installing new superconducting magnets to concentrate particle beams more effectively.

## "Cotton Candy" Worlds: Astronomers Discover Ultralight Exoplanets

Shifting our gaze to the cosmos, astronomers have recently announced the discovery of two extraordinary **exoplanets**, designated **TOI-791 b** and **TOI-791 c**, that defy conventional planetary descriptions. These "super-puff" planets, found by an international team led by the University of Oxford, are roughly the size of Jupiter but possess an astonishingly low density, making them less dense than cotton candy.

Orbiting an F7-type dwarf star approximately 1,110 light-years away in the southern constellation Volans, TOI-791 b has a density of just 0.038 grams per cubic centimeter, while TOI-791 c measures 0.047 grams per cubic centimeter. To put this into perspective, Jupiter has an average density of 1.33 grams per cubic centimeter, making it 28 to 35 times denser than these newly discovered worlds. This extreme lightness suggests that these planets possess enormous, lightweight atmospheres. The researchers, whose findings were published in *Monthly Notices of the Royal Astronomical Society*, also noted their unusual orbital arrangement, a **5:3 mean-motion resonance**. For every five orbits completed by the inner planet, the outer planet finishes almost exactly three, with their gravitational interactions causing measurable changes in transit timing. This rare orbital relationship and their diffuse nature could provide invaluable clues about the formation mechanisms of some of the galaxy's most peculiar planets, challenging our current models of planetary evolution.

## Quantum Leaps: From Error Correction to Long-Lived Magnons

The realm of **quantum computing** continues its rapid ascent, with 2026 marking a pivotal year where the field transitions from a purely physics research endeavor to a scalable engineering discipline with tangible commercial implications. A key breakthrough highlighted this year is the demonstration of **error correction at scale**, showing that logical error rates decrease exponentially as quantum systems grow larger. This fundamental shift is exemplified by milestones such as Google's **Willow processor** and Microsoft's logical-qubit system, developed in collaboration with Atom Computing.

Further propelling this advancement, the U.S. Department of Energy (DOE) recently launched its ambitious **Quantum Genesis initiative**, aiming to develop and deploy the world's first scientifically relevant, **fault-tolerant quantum computing capability** for research and development by 2028. This initiative seeks to establish a unified HPC-AI-quantum computing ecosystem to accelerate scientific discovery across fields like chemistry, materials science, and high-energy physics. Meanwhile, the Yale-led **ERASE project** (Erasure Qubits and Dynamic Circuits for Quantum Advantage) received a substantial $4 million grant from the National Science Foundation, furthering its work on error-correcting quantum computers utilizing innovative "erasure flag" qubits that simplify error identification and correction.

Adding another layer to this exciting domain, physicists at the University of Vienna have achieved a remarkable feat by extending the lifespan of **magnons** by nearly two orders of magnitude, from a few hundred nanoseconds to an impressive 18 microseconds. Magnons, which are collective excitations of electron spins in a magnetic material, have long held promise for quantum technologies, but their ephemeral nature has been a significant hurdle. This breakthrough, published in *Science Advances*, indicates that the previous lifespan limit was largely due to material quality, opening a clear path toward even longer-lived magnons. With enhanced lifespans, magnons could serve as durable **quantum memories** and low-loss communication channels on a chip, potentially connecting hundreds of qubits to form a crucial "quantum bus" for scalable quantum computers no larger than a coin. These diverse advancements underscore the relentless progress in harnessing the perplexing rules of the quantum world for revolutionary technologies.
