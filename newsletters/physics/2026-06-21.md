## The Last Piece of the Doubly Charmed Baryon Puzzle

The world of particle physics is abuzz with a significant discovery from the **LHCb Collaboration** at CERN's Large Hadron Collider. Researchers announced on June 18, 2026, they have detected the elusive **Ωcc⁺ baryon**, a particle composed of one strange quark and two charm quarks. This finding is monumental because it completes the family of doubly charmed baryons, particles made up of two charm quarks and one other quark, a set first theorized over half a century ago.

Quarks, as we know, are the fundamental building blocks of matter, coming in six "flavors": up, down, charm, strange, top, and bottom. They combine into pairs (mesons) or triplets (baryons). The existence and properties of these doubly charmed baryons were predicted after the discovery of the charm quark in 1974, extending the theoretical models that classify composite particles. The experimental confirmation of the Ωcc⁺ baryon, which has a mass about four times heavier than a proton, closes a chapter spanning sixty years of particle physics research.

Paula Collins, incoming Deputy Spokesperson of the LHCb experiment, highlighted the historical significance, stating that "Out of the 85 composite particles discovered so far at the LHC, these three doubly charmed baryons are unique". Their relatively long lifespan, decaying via the **weak force**, allowed scientists to measure their flight distances in the detector, a feat made possible by the LHCb's upgraded tracking and identification capabilities. This discovery not only validates long-standing theoretical predictions but also provides invaluable data for understanding the strong force that binds quarks together.

## Dark Energy's Unchallenged Reign

In the vast expanse of cosmology, a recent "crisis" has been averted, reaffirming one of modern physics' most profound discoveries: the accelerating expansion of the universe. An international team of astrophysicists, including Nobel laureates Adam Riess and Brian Schmidt, have published new research that robustly refutes a 2025 study which had cast doubt on this acceleration.

The original groundbreaking discovery, for which Riess, Schmidt, and Saul Perlmutter shared the 2011 Nobel Prize in Physics, relied on observations of **Type Ia supernovae**. These incredibly luminous stellar explosions serve as "standard candles" for measuring cosmic distances. Their earlier work showed that more distant supernovae appeared to be moving away faster than expected, leading to the conclusion that a mysterious force, dubbed **dark energy**, was driving the universe's expansion to accelerate.

However, a study published last year by a South Korean team suggested that the observed acceleration might be an illusion, proposing that the maximum brightness of Type Ia supernovae could vary with the age of the universe. This threatened to undermine the entire premise of dark energy. The new research, led by the University of Southampton, meticulously re-examined the data and found critical flaws in the 2025 analysis. They identified errors in how the age of stars was estimated and noted the failure to account for the mass of host galaxies, a standard correction in modern cosmology.

As Riess emphasized, "Extraordinary claims require especially careful testing". The new findings confirm that when these supernovae are properly calibrated, accounting for different host environments and stellar populations, the evidence for cosmic acceleration remains remarkably consistent. This means our understanding of a universe dominated by dark energy, driving its ever-faster expansion, remains solid. The mystery of what dark energy actually *is*, however, continues to be one of physics' most profound open questions.

## Schrödinger's Cat Enters a New Quantum Dimension

Meanwhile, closer to home, physicists at the University of Oxford have introduced a fascinating new twist to the famous **Schrödinger's cat** thought experiment, creating an entirely novel type of quantum state. This advancement could pave the way for more resilient quantum computers and deepen our insights into the enigmatic rules governing the quantum universe.

The concept of Schrödinger's cat famously illustrates **quantum superposition**, where a quantum system can exist in multiple states simultaneously until observed. While the hypothetical cat is both alive and dead, scientists routinely create real quantum superpositions in laboratories using atoms, light, and even motion. A prime example is a **qubit** in quantum computing, which can be a combination of 0 and 1 at the same time.

Traditionally, "cat states" involve an oscillator existing as a superposition of two wave packets, known as coherent states, moving in opposite directions. These coherent states are considered the closest quantum equivalents to classical motion. What the Oxford team has achieved is a departure from this classical-like foundation. They have developed a technique that combines a broad range of quantum components that are *already* highly nonclassical.

By precisely adjusting experimental parameters, the researchers demonstrated programmable control over these exotic quantum states, modifying the relative size, orientation, and separation of the components within the superposition. Their measurements revealed intricate interference patterns and regions of **Wigner negativity**, definitive signs that these states cannot be described by classical physics. This breakthrough, published on June 15, 2026, pushes the boundaries of quantum control and could unlock new avenues for quantum computing beyond traditional binary systems, as well as enhancing quantum sensing technologies.
