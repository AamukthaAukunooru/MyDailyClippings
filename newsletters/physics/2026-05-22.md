## Classical Computing Challenges Quantum Supremacy

A significant development emerged from the realm of quantum computing, or rather, from its classical counterpart. Physicists at the Center for Computational Quantum Physics (CCQ) at the Simons Foundation's Flatiron Institute, in collaboration with Boston University, have cracked a daunting quantum physics problem using conventional computers and sophisticated mathematical tools. Their groundbreaking technique, which leverages **tensor networks**, allowed them to simulate a quantum system composed of hundreds of interacting **qubits**, a task previously believed to be exclusively solvable by quantum computers. Remarkably, the researchers were even able to perform this complex simulation on a personal laptop, demonstrating an extraordinary efficiency that could redefine the boundaries of classical computational power in quantum dynamics.

This work, published in *Science* on May 21st, challenges the notion of "quantum supremacy" in certain problem domains, suggesting that classical methods, when sufficiently advanced, can still offer potent solutions. While quantum computing continues its rapid progression, this breakthrough opens new avenues for research into quantum dynamics and offers a powerful protocol for optimizing solutions amidst a multitude of possibilities. The team is already pushing further, developing tools to tackle even more challenging problems involving electron movement in quantum materials, a frontier that promises to deepen our understanding of quantum systems without the need for a quantum computer itself.

## Illuminating the Future: New Ways to Control Light

The control and manipulation of light continue to be fertile ground for innovation, with two distinct yet equally fascinating stories emerging. First, physicists at Peking University have unveiled a revolutionary method to confine light far beyond conventional limits, introducing what they call **singulonics**. Their research, published on May 21st, describes the discovery of **narwhal-shaped wavefunctions** that can trap light in extremely tiny, deep-subwavelength volumes within purely dielectric materials, sidestepping the energy dissipation issues associated with metal-based approaches. This advance holds immense promise for ultra-efficient **photonic chips**, next-generation **quantum technologies**, and imaging tools with unprecedented resolution. The team has already demonstrated a **singular optical microscope** capable of achieving a spatial resolution of λ/1000, imaging patterns like "PKU" and "SFM" at deep-subwavelength scales.

Concurrently, an international team of researchers, including a scientist from Aston University, has finally demystified the enigmatic behavior of "breather" laser pulses. These unusual ultrafast lasers produce light bursts that rhythmically grow and shrink, rather than maintaining a steady state, a puzzle that has long perplexed laser physicists. On May 21st, the team reported a new mathematical framework that successfully explains these "breathing" dynamics, unifying two previously distinct types of laser behavior under a single model. This deeper understanding is crucial, as ultrafast lasers are indispensable in fields ranging from eye surgery and biomedical imaging to advanced manufacturing. Solving this long-standing puzzle promises to enhance the stability of these lasers and enable their more precise tailoring for specialized applications.

## A New Eye on the Universe: The Vera C. Rubin Observatory

In a monumental stride for observational astrophysics, the **Vera C. Rubin Observatory** in Chile has officially commenced operations, marking the dawn of a new era in our understanding of the cosmos. After more than a decade of construction, this observatory is now poised to deliver an unprecedented stream of images and data, transforming modern astronomy. As Monmouth College physics professor Mike Solontoi highlighted on May 21st, the Rubin Observatory will embark on a ten-year "digital movie" project, imaging the entire southern sky every three to four nights.

This continuous survey will enable astronomers to study billions of galaxies, meticulously map **dark matter** through weak gravitational lensing, and delve deeper into the mysteries of **dark energy**, which is driving the accelerating expansion of the universe. Beyond cosmology, the observatory is expected to dramatically expand our inventory of the Solar System, discovering millions of new asteroids, comets, and trans-Neptunian objects. It will also detect millions of transient events nightly, from exploding stars to variable stars, and catalog approximately 17 billion stars within the Milky Way, offering invaluable insights into our galaxy's structure and evolution. Early successes already include the detection of 3I/Atlas, the third interstellar comet, and two ultra-fast spinning asteroids, hinting at the wealth of discoveries to come.
