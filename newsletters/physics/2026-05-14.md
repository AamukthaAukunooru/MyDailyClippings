## The Standard Model Under Strain: New Hints from CERN and Muon g-2

For decades, the **Standard Model of particle physics** has been our remarkably successful blueprint for understanding the fundamental particles and forces that govern the universe. Yet, physicists have long suspected it's incomplete, unable to account for phenomena like dark matter or the observed mass of neutrinos. Recent findings from CERN's Large Hadron Collider (LHC) are adding fresh cracks to this foundational theory, alongside a recent prestigious recognition for a long-standing anomaly.

New research from the **LHCb experiment** at CERN, announced on May 12, 2026, suggests that certain **B meson decays** may not be behaving as the Standard Model predicts. The LHCb experiment meticulously analyzes the collisions of proton particles within the 27-kilometer-long tunnel, specifically studying the transformations, or decays, of subatomic particles like B mesons. The team investigated how these B mesons decay into other particles, finding a disagreement with theoretical predictions. While not yet a definitive discovery, these highly unusual patterns in rare B meson decays provide "fresh reason to suspect new physics may be hiding beyond the Standard Model". This ongoing investigation could challenge the theory that has guided particle physics for the past 50 years.

Further supporting the notion of physics beyond the Standard Model, the **2026 Breakthrough Prize in Fundamental Physics** was recently awarded to the **Muon g-2 Collaborations** at CERN, Brookhaven National Laboratory, and Fermilab. This award recognizes over five decades of work by hundreds of scientists and engineers to precisely measure the **anomalous magnetic moment of the muon**. The muon, a heavier cousin of the electron, behaves like a tiny magnet, and its magnetic strength is subtly influenced by a "foam" of virtual particles constantly appearing and disappearing around it. The precision of these measurements, reaching an astonishing 127 parts per billion, has revealed a persistent discrepancy with the Standard Model's predictions, a tantalizing hint that unknown particles or forces might be at play. As University of Mississippi professor Breese Quinn, a member of the Fermilab Muon g-2 experiment, noted, his group is now using this ultra-high precision data to search for "hints of breakdowns in the fundamental symmetries of the universe". These independent lines of evidence from B meson decays and the muon's magnetic moment are converging, strongly indicating that our current understanding of particle physics is incomplete and the door to "new physics" is inching open.

## Gravitational Waves: A New Window into Dark Matter

The universe's most enigmatic constituent, **dark matter**, continues to elude direct detection, despite making up an estimated 85 percent of all matter. However, a clever new approach, leveraging the ripples in spacetime known as **gravitational waves**, offers a promising fresh avenue in this profound quest.

Researchers from MIT and several European institutions have developed a novel method to identify the subtle "imprints" that dark matter might leave on gravitational waves produced by **merging black holes**. Dark matter interacts with its surroundings almost exclusively through gravity, meaning that if two colliding black holes spiral through a dense region of dark matter, the gravitational waves they emit could carry a unique signature of that interaction.

The team applied their new model to publicly available data from the **LIGO-Virgo-KAGRA (LVK) network**, the global array of observatories designed to detect gravitational waves. Out of 28 clear signals from black hole mergers, 27 were consistent with mergers occurring in a vacuum, as expected. However, one particular signal, **GW190728**, showed "possible signs of a dark matter imprint". While this is not a definitive detection of dark matter, it marks a significant step, offering a new way to screen gravitational-wave data for these crucial hints.

Josu Aurrekoetxea, a postdoc in the MIT Department of Physics, explained that black holes can enhance the density of dark matter, making its effects potentially observable through gravitational waves. Soumen Roy of Université Catholique de Louvain, who led the data analysis, is optimistic, stating, "We now have the potential to discover dark matter around black holes as the LVK detectors keep collecting data in the coming years". This innovative technique opens the door to probing dark matter at scales much smaller than previously possible, marking an exciting new chapter in our search for the universe's invisible scaffolding.
