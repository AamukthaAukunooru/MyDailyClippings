## Antimatter on the Move: CERN's Road Trip Revolution

In a stunning display of precision engineering and scientific ingenuity, CERN scientists have achieved a world first: the successful road transport of **antiprotons**. This unprecedented feat, carried out on Tuesday, March 25, 2026, signals a "new era" for antimatter research and opens the door to supplying research laboratories across Europe with these elusive particles.

The BASE experiment (Baryon Antibaryon Symmetry Experiment) at CERN was behind this groundbreaking achievement. A specialized, portable **cryogenic Penning trap** was used to capture and store a cloud of 92 antiprotons. To prevent annihilation upon contact with ordinary matter, these antiprotons were cooled to an astonishing 8.2 Kelvin, or -268 degrees Celsius, and held in a strong vacuum, ensuring they remained isolated.

CERN physicist Stefan Ulmer, a spokesperson for the BASE experiment, confirmed the success after a large truck completed a 10-kilometre drive around the main physics laboratory campus in Meyrin, Switzerland, with the antiprotons safely contained. While a 10-kilometre journey might seem modest, the challenge of moving antimatter, which annihilates instantly upon contact with matter, is immense. Project leader Christian Smorra noted that the ultimate goal is to transport these antiprotons to dedicated precision laboratories, such as Heinrich Heine University Düsseldorf in Germany, where the magnetic field fluctuations inherent to CERN's "antimatter factory" can be avoided, allowing for measurements 100 to 1,000 times more precise. This portable system, weighing 1,000 kilograms, represents a significant miniaturization effort compared to previous setups. The ability to transport antimatter safely and reliably promises to deepen our understanding of the fundamental asymmetry between matter and antimatter in the universe, a mystery that continues to puzzle physicists since the Big Bang should have created equal amounts of both.

## A Heavier Cousin: New Proton-like Particle Discovered at LHCb

CERN continues to be a hotbed of discovery, with the LHCb experiment announcing the identification of a new subatomic particle, the **Ξcc⁺ (Xi-cc-plus)**. This particle is a heavier relative of the familiar proton and represents the first new particle discovered using the upgraded LHCb experiment at the Large Hadron Collider.

The Ξcc⁺ is a **baryon**, meaning it is composed of three quarks, much like protons and neutrons. However, unlike a proton, which consists of two up quarks and one down quark, the newly observed Ξcc⁺ contains **two charm quarks and one down quark**. This composition makes it roughly four times heavier than a proton. The discovery, initially presented around March 17, 2026, and widely reported since, is crucial for physicists seeking to better understand the **strong force**, the fundamental interaction that binds quarks together to form protons, neutrons, and other composite particles.

The upgraded LHCb detector, completed in 2023, played a pivotal role, capable of recording much larger volumes of data and dramatically increasing the chances of spotting rare phenomena. Professor Tim Gershon of the University of Warwick, who is set to lead the LHCb collaboration from July 2026, highlighted that this breakthrough signals a new era for the experiment, enabling researchers to explore previously inaccessible areas of particle physics. This is only the second time a baryon with two heavy quarks has been observed, building on a similar discovery by LHCb nearly a decade ago. The data for this landmark measurement was recorded during proton-proton collisions in 2024, the first full year of the upgraded experiment's operation.

## Unveiling a 48-Dimensional World in Quantum Light

Shifting our gaze to the more esoteric realms of quantum mechanics, scientists at the University of the Witwatersrand in South Africa, in collaboration with Huzhou University, have uncovered a startling complexity within entangled photons. Their research reveals that standard methods for producing entangled light can contain previously unseen **topological structures**, reaching a record-breaking **48 dimensions** and encompassing more than 17,000 distinct topological signatures.

Published in *Nature Communications* on March 21, 2026, this discovery introduces a vast new "alphabet" for encoding stable quantum information. Traditionally, it was assumed that at least two properties of light, such as orbital angular momentum (OAM) and polarization, would be needed to create such topology. However, Professor Andrew Forbes from the Wits School of Physics emphasized that their work demonstrates topology can emerge from a single property: **orbital angular momentum (OAM)**.

The team found that measuring the OAM of two entangled photons reveals an intrinsic topology, a fundamental feature of the entanglement itself. Because OAM can take on an unlimited range of values, the associated topologies can extend to very high dimensions, offering a significantly richer structure than previously recognized. This hidden 48-dimensional structure could revolutionize how quantum information is stored and protected, potentially making future quantum systems more robust against noise and environmental interference. This discovery opens up exciting new avenues for quantum computing and communication, leveraging the intricate, high-dimensional properties of light itself.
