## Untangling the Black Hole Information Paradox and the Origin of Mass

For decades, physicists have grappled with the perplexing **black hole information paradox**, a conundrum born from Stephen Hawking's seminal work in the 1970s. Hawking's calculations suggested that black holes emit a faint form of radiation, slowly evaporating and eventually disappearing. The paradox arises because, according to quantum mechanics, information cannot be destroyed. If a black hole vanishes completely, the information about everything that fell into it would seemingly vanish too, violating a fundamental principle of quantum theory.

Now, a new theoretical study, published on July 4, 2026, by researchers from the Institute of Experimental Physics SAS, offers a compelling potential resolution. Led by Richard Pinčák, the team proposes that black holes do not fully evaporate. Instead, they stop shrinking at the very last moment, leaving behind tiny, stable **remnants**. These remnants are predicted to have an incredibly small mass, approximately 9 x 10<sup>-41</sup> kg, and, crucially, they are theorized to preserve all the information the black hole once contained. The proposed mechanism for this information storage involves a spectrum of **quasi-normal modes** associated with the remnant's structure, where quantum information becomes encoded within long-lived "vibrations" of a torsion field within the remnant's geometry. For instance, a remnant from a solar-mass black hole could store an astonishing 1.515 x 10<sup>77</sup> qubits of information, precisely enough to resolve the paradox.

What makes this research even more intriguing is its unexpected connection to another fundamental mystery: the origin of the mass of elementary particles. The researchers investigated a version of gravity known as **Einstein-Cartan theory**, formulated within a 7-dimensional geometry on a mathematical structure called a **G2-manifold with torsion**. They found that this higher-dimensional geometry naturally produces the **electroweak scale**, approximately 246 GeV, when reduced to the four dimensions we experience. This energy scale is intimately associated with the **Higgs field**, which is responsible for imbuing elementary particles with mass. Within their model, the vacuum expectation value (VEV) of the torsion field is dynamically identified with this electroweak scale, suggesting a deep, previously unappreciated link between spacetime geometry, black hole evaporation, and the fundamental masses of the particles that make up our universe. This work represents a significant step towards a more unified understanding of gravity and quantum mechanics.

## Reshaping the Arrow of Time in Quantum Systems

Our everyday experience dictates that time flows inexorably forward, a concept physicists refer to as the **arrow of time**. Yet, at the microscopic quantum level, many fundamental laws of physics are symmetrical, meaning they don't inherently distinguish between forward and backward movement in time. Now, scientists at Los Alamos National Laboratory have achieved a remarkable feat, developing new quantum control protocols that can make the behavior of quantum systems appear more consistent with time moving backward rather than forward.

Published on July 3, 2026, in *Physical Review X*, this research introduces techniques that can suppress the usual emergence of the arrow of time or even reverse its apparent direction in quantum processes. Los Alamos National Laboratory physicist Luis Pedro García-Pintos highlighted that unlike macroscopic phenomena, the fundamental laws governing the microscopic world often allow for both forward and backward time movement. By precisely managing quantum measurements, the researchers can effectively reshape a system's **arrow of time**.

Perhaps even more astonishing is a practical application stemming from this breakthrough: the ability to harvest energy directly from the act of making quantum measurements. The new control methods influence how energy moves into and out of a quantum system, allowing for the creation of a **measurement engine**. In this framework, quantum measurements themselves become a thermodynamic resource that can be tapped to perform work, such as driving another quantum process or storing energy in a **quantum battery**. This discovery could pave the way for more powerful quantum computers and other advanced technologies, fundamentally altering how we interact with and utilize energy at the quantum scale. The team plans to experimentally demonstrate these Hamiltonian-based measurement processes using superconducting qubits, bringing this theoretical marvel closer to tangible reality.
