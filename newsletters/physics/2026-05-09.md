My Daily Clippings, 2026-05-09

This week in physics has seen a trio of fascinating developments, spanning the quantum realm, the distant cosmos, and the materials that underpin our technology. Researchers at the University of Oxford have pushed the boundaries of quantum control by demonstrating a novel "quadsqueezing" interaction, opening new avenues for quantum technologies. Meanwhile, observations from the James Webb Space Telescope continue to challenge our understanding of galaxy formation, with the discovery of a massive, non-spinning galaxy in the early universe. Closer to home, a breakthrough at Oak Ridge National Laboratory promises more efficient electronics by demonstrating a new way to engineer ferroelectric properties in widely used semiconductor materials.

## Quantum Leap: Unveiling Quadsqueezing

A significant advancement in quantum physics comes from the University of Oxford, where researchers have achieved the first-ever demonstration of **quadsqueezing**, a fourth-order quantum interaction. Published on May 1 in *Nature Physics*, this breakthrough allows for the observation of quantum behaviors previously considered out of reach, potentially revolutionizing quantum simulation, sensing, and computing.

The team, including study co-author Dr. Raghavendra Srinivas from Oxford's Department of Physics, utilized a single trapped ion to meticulously produce and control increasingly complex forms of "squeezing". Squeezing is a well-established technique to redistribute quantum uncertainty, improving the precision of measurements in quantum harmonic oscillators, systems that describe everything from light waves to atomic motion. By moving beyond conventional second-order squeezing to trisqueezing and now, for the first time, quadsqueezing, the scientists have essentially unlocked a new type of interaction. Their experimental setup enabled them to switch between these different squeezing types, confirming their results by reconstructing the quantum motion of the trapped ion and revealing distinct patterns linked to each order of squeezing. This innovative method not only provides a deeper understanding of fundamental quantum mechanics but also offers a powerful new tool for designing and controlling quantum interactions, with profound implications for the development of future quantum technologies.

## Cosmic Anomaly: A Non-Spinning Galaxy in the Early Universe

Astronomers, leveraging the formidable capabilities of the James Webb Space Telescope (JWST), have made a perplexing discovery that challenges established models of galaxy formation. They have identified a massive galaxy, formed less than 2 billion years after the Big Bang, that appears to exhibit no rotation whatsoever. This finding, published on May 4 in *Nature Astronomy* and reported on May 7, defies current theories which posit that young galaxies should still be actively spinning from their formation.

Ben Forrest, a research scientist in the Department of Physics and Astronomy at the University of California, Davis, and lead author of the study, expressed surprise at the observation of galaxy XMM-VID1-2075. Current cosmological models suggest that as galaxies form, gas inflow and gravitational pull generate angular momentum, setting these systems into rotational motion. While older, more evolved galaxies closer to Earth can lose their spin through repeated collisions and mergers in dense clusters, observing such a lack of rotation in a galaxy from the universe's infancy, less than 2 billion years old, is highly unexpected. This discovery forces astronomers to re-evaluate the complex processes governing the early evolution and dynamics of galaxies, potentially necessitating refinements to our understanding of how these cosmic structures emerge and mature.

## Engineering the Future of Electronics with Ferroelectric Aluminum Nitride

In a development with significant implications for next-generation electronics, scientists at the Department of Energy's Oak Ridge National Laboratory (ORNL) have successfully demonstrated a novel method to induce **ferroelectricity** directly into aluminum nitride. This breakthrough, published in *Advanced Materials* and reported on May 7, paves the way for the creation of more reliable and lower-power devices, as ferroelectric materials can store data without requiring a constant power supply.

Aluminum nitride is a widely used semiconductor in modern microelectronics, particularly in 5G and Wi-Fi devices. Its ferroelectric potential was only recognized in 2019, but until now, a practical method for precisely controlling these properties was elusive. The ORNL team, led by postdoctoral research associate Bogdan Dryzhakov, utilized a tightly focused **helium ion beam** at the Center for Nanophase Materials Sciences (CNMS) to "write" ferroelectric regions into the material. This approach is particularly innovative because both aluminum nitride and helium ion beams are already standard in chip manufacturing, meaning chip makers could integrate this new processing method without adopting entirely new materials or manufacturing steps. The researchers found that the treated aluminum nitride required approximately 40 percent less energy to switch its polarization and exhibited a stronger piezoresponse. This finding not only offers a pathway to more energy-efficient devices but also suggests that other materials might exhibit unexpected switching behaviors when their defects are strategically controlled rather than avoided, potentially widening the search for new ferroelectrics.
