## The Amaterasu Particle: A Heavyweight Contender in Cosmic Rays

One of the most enduring mysteries in astrophysics is the origin of **ultra-high-energy cosmic rays**, particles that strike Earth with energies far exceeding anything we can generate in our most powerful accelerators. Among these enigmatic visitors, the **Amaterasu particle**, detected by the Telescope Array in Utah in 2021, stands out for its extraordinary energy, rivaling even the famous "Oh-My-God particle" from 1991. For years, scientists have grappled with its identity and source, given that its energy should have been significantly attenuated over intergalactic distances if it were a simple proton.

Now, new research from scientists at Penn State and their collaborators, published on June 9, 2026, in *Physical Review Letters*, offers a compelling solution: the Amaterasu particle may not be a proton at all. Instead, it could be an **ultraheavy atomic nucleus**, heavier than iron. This re-evaluation is crucial because ultraheavy nuclei are theorized to lose energy more slowly than lighter particles as they traverse the vast cosmic distances, allowing them to retain their extreme energies upon reaching Earth. According to the team's calculations, this property makes them far more likely candidates for such powerful events. This finding doesn't suggest that *all* ultra-high-energy cosmic rays are ultraheavy nuclei, but it significantly impacts how researchers will search for the sources of these cosmic behemoths, potentially pointing to powerful cosmic explosions capable of forging and accelerating such massive particles.

## Peering into the Cosmic Dawn: The Earliest Flickering Quasar

Shifting our gaze to the dawn of the universe, astronomers at the MIT Kavli Institute for Astrophysics and Space Research have announced the discovery of the earliest known **flickering quasar**, a voracious supermassive black hole that was already surprisingly mature when the universe was a mere 850 million years old. This remarkable observation, reported on June 8, 2026, by a team including postdoc Gene Leung and Anna-Christina Eilers, provides unprecedented insight into the early growth of these cosmic giants.

What makes this discovery particularly significant is the quasar's "flickering" behavior. Over a 14-year observational period using data from NASA's **Near-Earth Object Wide-field Infrared Survey Explorer (NEOWISE)** mission, the quasar's light was seen to fluctuate randomly, much like a candle flame. This variability allowed researchers to deduce that its **accretion disk**, the swirling whirlpool of gas and dust feeding the black hole, had a surprisingly flat, pancake-like shape, remarkably similar to those found around more modern-day quasars. The quasar itself is estimated to be as bright as **12 trillion suns**, with its brightness fluctuating by approximately 20%, or about 2 trillion suns. This unexpected maturity and structural similarity to later quasars challenge existing models of black hole formation and galaxy evolution in the very young universe, suggesting that these colossal structures might have established their fundamental characteristics far earlier than previously thought.

## Quantum Material Unveils New Superconducting Secrets

Closer to home, in the realm of condensed matter physics, researchers are continually uncovering the astonishing properties of quantum materials. On June 8, 2026, physicists at Florida State University (FSU), led by Assistant Professor Cyprian Lewandowski and postdoctoral researcher Phong Võ Tiến, revealed unusual **superconducting states** and the **quantum anomalous Hall effect** in **rhombohedral graphene**. Their findings, published in *Nature Physics*, represent a significant step forward in understanding and potentially harnessing these exotic quantum phenomena.

Rhombohedral graphene is a unique form of graphene where a few layers of carbon atoms are stacked in a specific chiral, staircase-like arrangement. The team discovered that superconductivity emerges directly from a "dual-surface configuration," where electron and hole carriers on opposite surfaces of the material collectively conspire to form a superconducting state. Adding to this, they also observed the **quantum anomalous Hall effect**, a topological state where an electrical current flows without resistance exclusively along the material's edges, even without an external magnetic field. Lewandowski notes that rhombohedral graphene seems to capture many intriguing electronic phenomena previously seen in more complex atomically thin systems, but in a form that is more amenable to technical applications and replicability. This research not only deepens our fundamental understanding of quantum phases of matter but also opens promising avenues for developing new **quantum technologies**, potentially impacting everything from energy-efficient electronics to advanced quantum computing.
