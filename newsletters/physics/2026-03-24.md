## A Heavier Cousin for the Proton Emerges from the LHC

The world of particle physics is abuzz with the recent announcement from CERN, where the **LHCb Collaboration** has unveiled a new, exotic particle, the Ξcc⁺ (Xi-cc-plus). This isn't just any new particle; it's a **doubly charmed baryon**, a heavy relative of our familiar proton, composed of two **charm quarks** and one **down quark**. The discovery, presented at the ongoing Moriond conference and widely reported between March 17-19, 2026, marks a significant milestone as the first new particle identified using the upgraded LHCb detector.

With a mass approximately four times that of a proton, at 3619.97 MeV/c², the Ξcc⁺ provides physicists with an unprecedented opportunity to delve deeper into the intricacies of the **strong force**, the fundamental interaction that binds quarks together to form composite particles like protons and neutrons. The observation of this particle, which was detected through its decay into three lighter particles (Λc⁺ K⁻ π⁺) during proton-proton collisions in 2024, resolves a long-standing question in particle physics that dates back over two decades to unconfirmed claims. According to LHCb Spokesperson Vincenzo Vagnoni, this finding will be instrumental for theorists to test and refine models of **quantum chromodynamics (QCD)**, the theory governing the strong force, and to better understand the formation of not only conventional baryons and mesons, but also more exotic hadrons like tetraquarks and pentaquarks. Scientists from the University of Manchester, for instance, played a key role in the discovery, having designed and constructed critical components of the detector's tracking system.

## Time Crystals: Breaking the Rules of Interaction

Shifting our gaze to the peculiar world of quantum mechanics, physicists at **New York University** have engineered a novel type of **time crystal** that appears to flout one of the most foundational principles of classical physics, Newton's Third Law of Motion. Published on March 23, 2026, in *Physical Review Letters*, this groundbreaking work involves levitating tiny beads using sound waves, where their interactions exhibit a fascinating one-sided, unbalanced nature.

Traditional time crystals are intriguing states of matter where particles "tick" with a steady, repeating cycle in time, even in their lowest energy state. What makes this new iteration particularly remarkable is the **non-reciprocal interaction** observed between the levitated particles. In essence, a larger particle exerts a stronger influence on a smaller one via scattered sound waves than the smaller particle does in return, creating an imbalance that deviates from the equal and opposite reactions mandated by Newton's Third Law. This phenomenon, explained by the different scattering properties of beads of varying sizes, opens up new avenues for exploring fundamental physics and could have profound implications for future technologies such as **quantum computing** and advanced data storage. The simplicity of the experimental setup belies the profound complexity of the physics it reveals.

## Cosmic Archaeology: Unearthing a Galaxy's 12-Billion-Year History

Finally, our journey takes us across vast cosmic distances, where astronomers have pioneered a revolutionary technique dubbed "**extragalactic archaeology**" to reconstruct the 12-billion-year evolutionary saga of a giant spiral galaxy, **NGC 1365**. A team led by the **Center for Astrophysics, Harvard and Smithsonian**, published their findings on March 23, 2026, in *Nature Astronomy*, marking the first time such a detailed chemical archaeology method has been applied to a galaxy beyond our Milky Way.

By meticulously analyzing the **chemical fingerprints**, specifically the distribution of oxygen, within NGC 1365's gas, and comparing these observations with sophisticated simulations, researchers were able to trace its growth from shortly after the Big Bang to the present day. The study revealed that the galaxy's central region formed early and became rich in oxygen, while its outer regions gradually accumulated matter over billions of years through collisions with smaller dwarf galaxies. This innovative approach, utilizing data from the TYPHOON survey, offers a powerful new lens through which to understand the formation and evolution of distant galaxies, ultimately helping us piece together the cosmic narrative that led to the existence of our own solar system and indeed, the oxygen we breathe.
