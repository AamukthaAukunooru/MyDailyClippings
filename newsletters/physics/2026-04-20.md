## The Muon g-2 Collaboration Honored with Breakthrough Prize

In a testament to the power of sustained scientific inquiry, the **Muon g-2 experiment** collaborations at CERN, Brookhaven National Laboratory, and Fermi National Accelerator Laboratory (Fermilab) have been awarded the prestigious 2026 Breakthrough Prize in Fundamental Physics. The prize, announced on Saturday, April 18, 2026, recognizes "multi-decade, groundbreaking contributions to the measurement of the muon's anomalous magnetic moment, pushing the boundaries of experimental precision and igniting a new era in the quest for physics beyond the Standard Model".

The **muon**, a heavier cousin of the electron, possesses an intrinsic property called its magnetic moment, which causes it to "wobble" in a magnetic field. The **Standard Model of particle physics** precisely predicts this wobble. However, the Muon g-2 experiment has consistently measured a value that deviates significantly from this theoretical prediction. This discrepancy, expressed as "g-2," suggests that unknown particles or forces, not accounted for in the Standard Model, might be briefly interacting with the muon, influencing its magnetic behavior.

The experiment's journey began at CERN in the 1970s, moved to Brookhaven in the 1990s, and culminated with the most precise measurements at Fermilab, with final results published in 2025. The $3 million prize will be shared among the living co-authors across these institutions, including scientists like William M. Morse of Brookhaven Lab, Bradley Lee Roberts of Boston University, Chris Polly of Fermilab, and David Hertzog of the University of Washington, who were instrumental in leading the various stages of the experiment. This award not only celebrates the incredible precision achieved by the collaboration, but also highlights the tantalizing possibility of discovering new physics that could redefine our understanding of the universe's fundamental constituents.

## Graphene's Electrons Defy Expectations, Flowing Like a Perfect Liquid

In an astounding development that challenges a fundamental law of physics, scientists have observed electrons in **graphene** flowing like a nearly frictionless liquid. This exotic quantum state, reported on April 15, 2026, by researchers at the Indian Institute of Science (IISc), in collaboration with the National Institute for Materials Science in Japan, reveals new fundamental behavior and could unlock powerful future technologies. Their findings were published in *Nature Physics*.

For decades, physicists have sought to observe electrons moving as a perfectly smooth, frictionless fluid, governed by a universal quantum value. This has been incredibly challenging due to imperfections in real materials, such as atomic defects and impurities, which typically disrupt these delicate quantum effects. However, by creating exceptionally clean graphene samples, a single layer of carbon atoms arranged in a flat sheet, the team was able to measure how the material conducts both electricity and heat.

What they found was truly unexpected. The electrons exhibited behavior characteristic of a nearly perfect liquid, a state previously thought to be almost impossible to detect in conventional materials. As Professor Arindam Ghosh from the Department of Physics at IISc noted, "It is amazing that there is so much to do on just a single layer of graphene even after 20 years of discovery". This breakthrough establishes graphene as an accessible and cost-effective platform for exploring phenomena usually associated with extreme environments, potentially opening new avenues for studying high-energy physics, astrophysics, and other complex quantum phenomena in a laboratory setting.

## A Magnetic-Free Path to Orbitronics: Chiral Phonons Control Electrons

The future of computing may lie in a surprising discovery: scientists have found a way to control electrons without the need for magnets. Reported on April 17, 2026, this breakthrough demonstrates that tiny atomic vibrations, known as **chiral phonons**, can directly transfer motion to electrons, allowing them to carry information. This opens the door to a new field called **orbitronics**, where data is processed using the orbital motion of electrons rather than their charge or spin.

Traditionally, controlling the orbital motion of electrons has required magnetic materials, which are often heavy, costly, and difficult to integrate into scalable devices. This new study, with contributions from researchers at the University of Utah, has introduced a far simpler approach. For the first time, researchers demonstrated that chiral phonons can directly impart **orbital angular momentum** to electrons in a non-magnetic material. This removes a significant limitation that has hindered the advancement of orbitronics.

The key material in this discovery is **quartz**, a lightweight and inexpensive crystal. Scientists at the University of Utah directly measured the internal magnetic effects generated by chiral phonons in quartz using specialized equipment at the National High Magnetic Field Lab in Florida. By observing how reflected laser light changed, they confirmed that these atomic vibrations produce a significant magnetic field, capable of influencing electron orbitals. This ability to manipulate electrons in non-magnetic materials through atomic vibrations could revolutionize data processing, paving the way for more efficient and powerful computing technologies.
