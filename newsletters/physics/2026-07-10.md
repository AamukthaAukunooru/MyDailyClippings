## Euclid Casts a Wider Net, Uncovering the Universe's Most Ancient Quasars

The European Space Agency's **Euclid space telescope**, designed to map the "dark universe," has made headlines with the discovery of 31 new quasars, including two that now hold the record for the most ancient ever observed. These dazzling cosmic beacons, powered by supermassive black holes, shone when the universe was a mere 670 million years old, approximately 5% of its current age.

The two record-breaking quasars, named **EUCL J172902.75+641018.1** and **EUCL J125308.55+705432.3**, boast redshifts of 7.77 and 7.69 respectively, surpassing the previous record of 7.64. This finding more than doubles the number of quasars known at such extreme distances, providing an invaluable dataset for astronomers. Prior to Euclid, identifying even a handful of quasars at a redshift of 7 or above took over a decade. Now, in just 1.5 years of its Wide Survey, Euclid has achieved this feat, thanks to its unprecedented survey area and depth, allowing it to capture much fainter light than previously possible.

Researchers, including PhD candidate Daming Yang from Leiden University, led the effort, using multiple machine-learning and probabilistic techniques to sift through vast amounts of data. Follow-up spectroscopic observations with ground-based facilities like Keck, Magellan, and the Large Binocular Telescope confirmed their findings. This comprehensive "census" of ancient quasars is a significant step toward understanding how supermassive black holes formed and grew so rapidly in the early universe, a mystery that continues to challenge current cosmological models. The data will also help scientists map the universe's large-scale structure and test the **Lambda-CDM model** more thoroughly.

## Macroscopic Quantum Entanglement: A Crystal Reveals Hidden Quantum Depths

For decades, the bizarre phenomena of **quantum mechanics**, such as **quantum entanglement**, have been largely confined to the realm of the microscopic, requiring extreme isolation and ultra-low temperatures. However, recent work from researchers at TU Wien in Austria is challenging this notion, demonstrating surprisingly strong quantum entanglement within a centimeter-sized crystal.

Published on July 8, this discovery shows that even macroscopic materials, specifically a type of material known as a **strange metal**, can exhibit profound quantum behavior. The team detected a high degree of entanglement using a technique from quantum information science called **quantum Fisher information**. This finding bridges the gap between quantum information science and condensed matter physics, suggesting that the quantum world isn't as far removed from our everyday experience as once thought.

Professor Silke Bühler-Paschen, a key researcher in the study, highlighted that this "unusual approach" of applying quantum information methods to solid-state physics is yielding "fundamentally new insight". The implications are considerable, offering new avenues for understanding the mysterious properties of strange metals and potentially paving the way for the development of ultra-precise **quantum sensors** and other advanced technologies. This work suggests that the boundaries of where quantum effects can be observed are far more expansive than previously imagined.

## Gravitational Waves Offer a "Direct Wave" View of a Black Hole's Edge

In a stunning display of observational prowess, physicists have detected a new kind of gravitational wave signal emanating from the very edge of a newly formed black hole. This groundbreaking observation, focusing on the **black hole merger event GW250114** detected on January 14, 2025, by the **Laser Interferometer Gravitational-Wave Observatory (LIGO)**, provides unprecedented measurements of the mysterious region around a black hole's **event horizon**.

The detected signal, termed a "**direct wave**," is a subtle feature within the total gravitational wave signal that had been predicted by theory but never before observed in real data. Crucially, this direct wave appears to carry information from extremely close to the event horizon, the point of no return beyond which nothing, not even light, can escape.

This discovery, published in *Nature* on June 24, suggests that gravitational wave observatories could offer an entirely new method for probing the immediate vicinity of black holes without directly observing them. The measurements are consistent with **spacetime** near the horizon being rapidly dragged around by the spinning black hole, while the signal itself fades due to the intense gravitational field, a phenomenon predicted by Albert Einstein's theory of **general relativity**. This opens up exciting possibilities for future investigations into the most extreme gravitational environments in the universe.
