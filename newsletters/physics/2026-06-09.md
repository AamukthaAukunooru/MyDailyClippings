## Peering into the Cosmic Dawn: The Earliest Flickering Quasar

Astronomers at MIT have made a remarkable discovery, detecting the earliest known **flickering quasar** from a mere 850 million years after the Big Bang. This ancient, voracious black hole, surprisingly mature for its age, challenges our current understanding of how supermassive black holes formed and grew in the early universe. The findings, published on June 8, 2026, illuminate a puzzling aspect of cosmic evolution.

The team, including former MIT postdoc Kishalay De, now a faculty member at Columbia University, and current MIT Kavli Institute for Astrophysics and Space Research postdoc Gene Leung, unearthed this signal by reprocessing 14 years of archival data from NASA's **Near-Earth Object Wide-field Infrared Survey Explorer (NEOWISE)** mission. What they observed was a quasar, a highly luminous active galactic nucleus, flickering randomly over the extended period, much like a candle flame. This particular quasar is estimated to be as bright as 12 trillion suns, with its brightness fluctuating by about 20 percent, or roughly 2 trillion suns.

Crucially, the nature of this flicker allowed researchers to determine that the quasar's **accretion disk**, the whirlpool of gas and dust spiraling into the black hole, was surprisingly thin and flat. This flat, pancake-like structure is typically associated with more mature, stable black holes found in the modern universe, not those expected to be just forming in the chaotic early cosmos. This observation deepens the mystery of how such massive black holes could have settled into such a stable configuration so quickly after the Big Bang, forcing cosmologists to re-evaluate their models of early black hole growth.

## Microsoft's Quantum Leap with Majorana 2

In a significant announcement that is sure to reverberate across the tech world, Microsoft CEO Satya Nadella unveiled the company's new quantum processor, **Majorana 2**, on June 8, 2026. This development represents a substantial breakthrough in the quest for stable and scalable quantum computing, addressing one of the most persistent challenges in the field: **qubit stability**.

The primary innovation of Majorana 2 lies in its ability to maintain qubit stability for an unprecedented 20 seconds, and in some instances, up to a full minute. For context, maintaining the fragile quantum states of qubits for extended periods is paramount for executing complex quantum algorithms and reducing error rates. This improved coherence time is a critical step towards building fault-tolerant quantum computers that can tackle problems currently intractable for even the most powerful classical supercomputers.

What makes this breakthrough even more intriguing is the role of **artificial intelligence** in its creation. Microsoft leveraged AI agents to assist in the design and optimization of the Majorana 2 chip, showcasing a powerful synergy between AI and scientific discovery. Nadella suggested that this acceleration in development could mean a commercially useful quantum computer might arrive by 2029, significantly sooner than previously anticipated. Such a machine holds immense potential for revolutionizing fields from drug discovery and materials science to logistics and finance, by enabling simulations and optimizations far beyond current capabilities.

## Graphene's Unexpected Quantum Properties Unveiled

Closer to home, in the realm of condensed matter physics, a collaborative effort led by physicists at Florida State University (FSU) has uncovered novel electronic properties in **rhombohedral graphene**. Published on June 8, 2026, in *Nature Physics*, this research reveals unusual **superconducting states** and a **quantum anomalous Hall effect** in this atomically thin material, paving the way for unexpected quantum technologies.

Assistant Professor Cyprian Lewandowski and postdoctoral researcher Phong Võ Tiến, part of an international team, investigated rhombohedral graphene, which consists of a few layers of carbon atoms stacked in a unique chiral, staircase-like configuration. They found that at low energies, electrons in this structure localize almost exclusively on the material's top and bottom surfaces, rather than being distributed throughout the bulk. This concentration of electrons on the surfaces leads to intriguing emergent quantum properties, as the charges interact and collectively "make choices" about their arrangement.

The team's most significant finding is that superconductivity emerges directly from this dual-surface electron configuration, where electron and hole carriers on opposite surfaces conspire to form a superconducting state. Furthermore, they observed a **quantum anomalous Hall effect**, a topological state where electrical current flows without resistance along the edges of the material, even without an external magnetic field. According to Lewandowski, this rhombohedral system effectively distills many complex electronic phenomena seen in other atomically thin systems into a more ideal and replicable form, offering a simpler platform to understand underlying quantum mechanisms and develop future energy-efficient electronics and quantum information technologies.
