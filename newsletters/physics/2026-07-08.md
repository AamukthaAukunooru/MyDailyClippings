Today, we delve into the cosmos's deepest reaches and the cutting edge of quantum technology. Astronomers using the Euclid space telescope have unveiled a trove of the universe's most ancient quasars, offering an unprecedented look into the cosmic dawn. Meanwhile, a significant leap in quantum sensing promises to unlock secrets of dark matter and primordial gravitational waves. Not to be outdone, astrophysicists have, for the first time, directly witnessed the birth of a magnetar, confirming a long-standing theory about the power source behind some of the universe's brightest explosions.

## Euclid Unveils the Universe's Earliest Beacons

The European Space Agency's **Euclid space telescope**, designed to explore the "dark universe" of dark matter and dark energy, has delivered a truly spectacular early result: the discovery of 31 new ancient **quasars**, including the most distant ever observed. Published on July 6, 2026, in *Astronomy & Astrophysics*, this finding more than doubles the number of known quasars from the universe's infancy.

Quasars are the extraordinarily luminous active galactic nuclei powered by supermassive black holes at the centers of galaxies. These newly identified cosmic elders shone with the light of trillions of Suns when the universe was only about 670 million years old, roughly 5% of its current age. The highest redshift quasar, named **EUCL J172902.75+641018.1**, boasts a redshift of 7.77, surpassing the previous record of 7.64. Another, **EUCL J125308.55+705432.3**, has a redshift of 7.69.

The team, led by Daming Yang, a PhD in astronomy at Leiden University in the Netherlands, utilized Euclid's wide survey data, covering approximately 3000 square degrees of the sky. This unprecedented survey area and depth have allowed researchers to capture not just the brightest outliers, but a more representative sample of the ancient quasar population, providing a crucial "census" for understanding these objects on a fundamental level. This breakthrough is vital for probing the epoch of reionization, a pivotal period when the universe transitioned from a cold, dark state to an ionized one, illuminated by energetic light from the first stars and quasars.

## Quantum Sensors Sharpen Their Focus for Cosmic Mysteries

In a significant advance for quantum technology, scientists have developed a novel noise-canceling technique for **quantum detectors** that could profoundly impact our search for **dark matter** and ancient **gravitational waves**. Researchers at Imperial College London, part of the UK-wide Atom Interferometer Observatory and Network (AION) collaboration, published their findings on July 7, 2026, demonstrating a crucial concept for future quantum sensors.

The prototype device employs two clouds of **ultracold atoms** in a tabletop experimental system within the Imperial Ultracold Strontium Laboratory. By comparing these two **long-baseline atom interferometers**, the team successfully eliminated experimental noise, allowing them to recover hidden signals even when individual measurements were seemingly overwhelmed by interference. This real-world demonstration shows that a concept previously confined to idealized laboratory conditions can work effectively in more challenging environments.

This advance is a critical step toward building next-generation quantum detectors, which could include instruments like the proposed **Atom Interferometry CERN Experiment (AICE)**. Such large-scale atom interferometers would investigate fundamental physics on an unprecedented scale, potentially becoming some of the largest quantum experiments ever constructed. The ability to cut through noise is paramount for detecting the incredibly faint signals expected from exotic forms of dark matter or gravitational waves originating from the universe's earliest moments, offering a new window into some of the cosmos's biggest unanswered questions.

## The Violent Birth of a Magnetar Unveiled

For the first time, astronomers have directly observed the birth of a **magnetar**, an exceptionally magnetic and rapidly spinning type of neutron star. This groundbreaking observation, published on July 6, 2026, in the journal *Nature*, confirms that these exotic objects are indeed the powerhouses behind some of the universe's most luminous stellar explosions, known as **superluminous supernovae**.

The discovery was made possible by detecting a unique "chirp" signal in the light from a distant supernova. This distinctive variation in brightness, which could only be explained using **Einstein's theory of general relativity**, provided direct evidence of a newborn magnetar. The observed "chirp" confirms a theory first proposed 16 years ago, in 2010, by UC Berkeley theoretical astrophysicist Dan Kasen, who suggested that a newborn magnetar could inject extra energy into a supernova's expanding debris, keeping it intensely bright for far longer than expected.

Magnetars, though only about 10 miles across, possess magnetic fields 100 to 1,000 times stronger than typical pulsars and can spin over 1,000 times per second when young. This direct observation not only validates a long-standing theoretical model but also marks the first time general relativity has been used to explain the mechanics of a supernova. This insight into these extreme cosmic objects deepens our understanding of stellar evolution and the most powerful events in the universe.
