## Quantum Twist: Atoms Spin Backward in a Crystal

In a stunning experimental demonstration, an international team of researchers has directly observed angular momentum moving through a crystal lattice, revealing a bizarre quantum effect where atomic rotations unexpectedly reverse direction. Led by scientists from the Helmholtz-Zentrum Dresden-Rossendorf (HZDR) and the Fritz Haber Institute of the Max Planck Society, this groundbreaking work, published in *Nature Physics*, utilized ultra-powerful **terahertz laser pulses** to trigger tiny atomic rotations inside a quantum material, specifically bismuth selenide.

What makes this discovery so compelling is the "1 + 1 = -1" effect observed. As angular momenta tied to lattice vibrations combined, they produced a new rotation at twice the frequency but in the *opposite* direction. This phenomenon, resembling an **Umklapp process** known in other areas of condensed matter physics, marks the first experimental demonstration involving lattice angular momentum. Olga Minakova, a doctoral researcher at the Fritz Haber Institute, described the elegance of nature's symmetries dictating these physical laws. The findings provide a fresh perspective on the fundamental origins of magnetism and could pave the way for better control over advanced quantum materials, potentially impacting future information technologies and next-generation memory devices.

## Unraveling High-Temperature Superconductivity

The long-standing enigma of **high-temperature superconductivity** has received a crucial clue, thanks to new measurements of nickelate superconductors. Chinese researchers, spearheaded by Junfeng He of the University of Science and Technology of China (USTC) and collaborators including Qikun Xue and Zhuoyu Chen of the Southern University of Science and Technology (SUSTech), have made significant progress in understanding these complex materials. Their findings, published in *Science* on May 21, 2026, address two central questions: **superconducting gap symmetry** and the **superconducting pairing mechanism**.

For the first time, the team identified a **nodeless superconducting gap** in Ruddlesden-Popper bilayer nickelate superconducting thin films, a result consistent with *s*-wave (s±) superconducting gap symmetry. Equally important was the detection of **electron-boson coupling**, evidenced by a dispersion kink approximately 70 meV below the Fermi level. This "fingerprint" of electron-boson coupling offers vital evidence for how electron pairs form in these materials. By meticulously growing thin films and employing angle-resolved photoemission spectroscopy (ARPES), this collaboration has provided critical insights that could accelerate the development of practical high-temperature superconductors, a technological holy grail.

## Classical Machines Challenge Quantum Supremacy

In a development that has sent ripples through the quantum computing community, physicists at the Center for Computational Quantum Physics (CCQ) at the Simons Foundation's Flatiron Institute, in collaboration with Boston University, have cracked a daunting quantum physics problem previously thought solvable only by quantum computers. Their work, published in *Science* on May 21, 2026, effectively overturns a claim of "quantum supremacy" made by another group in March 2025.

Utilizing conventional computers, cutting-edge mathematical tools, and sophisticated code based on **tensor networks**, the researchers demonstrated an astonishing efficiency. Remarkably, some calculations were even completed on a personal laptop. Joseph Tindall, an associate research scientist at the CCQ and first author of the new *Science* paper, expressed a healthy skepticism towards claims of quantum exclusivity. This breakthrough doesn't diminish the potential of quantum computing, but it highlights the often-underestimated power of classical algorithms and computational ingenuity. It opens new avenues for research on **quantum dynamics** and suggests that the frontier of true quantum advantage may be further out, or perhaps more nuanced, than some had assumed.
