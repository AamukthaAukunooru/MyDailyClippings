## Unveiling the Early Cosmos: Quasars and Magnetars

Our understanding of the universe's earliest moments has taken a significant leap forward, thanks to two remarkable astronomical announcements. First, an international team of astronomers, featuring researchers from the University of Arizona Steward Observatory and the Max-Planck-Institute for Astronomy (MPIA), has unveiled the discovery of **31 new quasars** from a period when the universe was only 600 to 800 million years old, a mere 5% of its current age. Published in *Astronomy & Astrophysics* on July 6, these observations include the two most distant quasars ever detected, with the new record-holder, **EUCL J172902.75+641018.1**, corresponding to a cosmic age of approximately 670 million years. This surpasses the previous record by 15 million years. These incredibly luminous objects, powered by supermassive black holes, act as cosmic beacons, illuminating the pivotal epoch of reionization when the first stars and galaxies began ionizing the neutral hydrogen fog. Dr. Xiaohui Fan, Regents Professor of Astronomy at the University of Arizona Steward Observatory, noted that these newly discovered quasars are "fainter, less luminous, and have somewhat lower-mass black holes" than previously found, offering a more typical glimpse into early black hole growth. The data, largely gathered by the European Space Agency's (ESA) **Euclid space telescope**, which launched in 2023, is already demonstrating its unparalleled capability in surveying the early universe.

Adding to this cosmic spectacle, astronomers have, for the first time, directly observed the birth of a **magnetar**. Reported on July 6 by the University of California, Berkeley, this groundbreaking discovery was made possible by detecting a strange "chirping" signal emanating from a distant supernova. This observation confirms a theory proposed 16 years ago by UC Berkeley theoretical astrophysicist Dan Kasen, which posited that these incredibly magnetic neutron stars are the powerhouses behind some of the universe's brightest stellar explosions, known as superluminous supernovae. Crucially, the unique "chirp" in the supernova's light could only be explained using **Einstein's theory of general relativity**, marking a novel application of this fundamental theory to supernova mechanics.

## Quantum Systems: Rewriting the Arrow of Time

In a development that sounds straight out of science fiction, researchers at Los Alamos National Laboratory have achieved a stunning feat in quantum physics: they've made a quantum system *appear* to run backward in time. Published in *Physical Review X* on July 3, the team developed sophisticated **quantum control techniques** that precisely manage quantum measurements to reshape a system's **arrow of time**. While this doesn't mean we're literally reversing causality on a macroscopic scale, it's a profound demonstration of how the rules governing quantum systems can diverge dramatically from our everyday classical experience. Physicist Luis Pedro García-Pintos highlighted that, unlike macroscopic phenomena, "at the microscopic level most fundamental laws of physics see forward and backward movement in time as physically possible". Beyond the conceptual implications, this breakthrough also led to the surprising discovery of a method to **harvest energy from the measurement process itself**. This advance holds significant promise for the development of more powerful quantum computers, novel quantum batteries, and other advanced quantum technologies.

## AI Illuminates Condensed Matter's Dark Corners

Artificial intelligence continues to prove its mettle in scientific discovery, with a recent application in condensed matter physics offering a powerful new tool for understanding complex material behaviors. Researchers at Nanjing University, led by Professor Hongtao Yuan, have utilized a single machine learning model to map the intricate "hidden" structure within the **anomalous Hall effect**. This phenomenon, which describes how electrons move through certain magnetic materials in ways that defy conventional expectations, has long been a challenge for physicists due to its complexity.

The AI model successfully sorted millions of magnetoresistance curves, which describe a material's electrical resistance in a magnetic field, into just **13 distinct categories**. It then created detailed **phase diagrams** and topological networks, illustrating how subtle changes in a material's electronic properties can shift its behavior from one category to another. This ability to "turn one of condensed matter physics' most persistent problems into a navigable map" is a significant step forward. The experimental data closely matched the AI-generated phase diagrams, providing a crucial real-world validation for the model. This novel framework, published in *National Science Review* on July 6, is expected to accelerate the discovery of new electronic states and could provide a shortcut to developing highly sought-after materials for modern electronics.
