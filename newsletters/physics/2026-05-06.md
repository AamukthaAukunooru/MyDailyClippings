## Manipulating Quantum Matter for Future Tech

The quest to harness quantum phenomena for practical applications, particularly in computing, continues to yield fascinating results. This week, we've seen two distinct yet complementary advancements in controlling and understanding exotic quantum states.

First, researchers at **California Polytechnic State University** have demonstrated a novel way to create entirely new forms of matter by simply varying a **magnetic field** over time. Led by lecturer Ian Powell and student researcher Louis Buchalter, whose work was published in *Physical Review B*, the team found that by carefully "driving" materials with timed magnetic shifts, they could induce quantum states that do not exist under normal, static conditions. These exotic states exhibit enhanced stability and resistance to errors, addressing one of the most formidable challenges in building robust quantum computers. The implications are profound, suggesting that the future of quantum technology may depend as much on the dynamic manipulation of materials as on their inherent composition.

In a related vein, scientists at **Aalto University's Department of Applied Physics** have achieved a significant milestone with **time crystals**, a peculiar state of matter that "ticks" endlessly without external energy input. First proposed by Nobel laureate Frank Wilczek in 2012 and experimentally confirmed in 2016, time crystals exhibit a repeating pattern in time, akin to a clock that never winds down. The Aalto team, led by Academy Research Fellow Jere Mäkinen, successfully linked a time crystal to an external mechanical oscillator for the first time. This breakthrough demonstrates the ability to control its behavior, opening avenues for developing highly precise sensors and potentially revolutionizing memory systems for future quantum computers, given their inherent stability that lasts "for orders of magnitude longer than the quantum systems currently used". Their findings were published in *Nature Communications*.

## Magnetic Fields "Revive" Superconductivity in Nickelates

In the world of condensed matter physics, a surprising discovery from the **City University of Hong Kong (CityUHK)** is challenging conventional wisdom about superconductivity. A research team led by Professor Denver Li Danfeng has uncovered a phenomenon they term **"re-entrant superconductivity"** in infinite-layer nickelate superconductors. Typically, strong magnetic fields are known to suppress superconductivity, but in these nickelates, superconductivity, once suppressed, astonishingly reappears at even higher magnetic field strengths.

This counterintuitive behavior, detailed in a recent publication in *Nature*, suggests that under specific conditions, magnetic interactions may not always compete with superconductivity but can, in fact, facilitate electron pairing and stabilize the superconducting state. Professor Li noted that this discovery creates a "bridge between high-temperature superconductivity and magnetism-driven quantum phenomena," offering new insights into the microscopic mechanisms governing these complex interactions. Since their initial discovery in 2019, infinite-layer nickelates have garnered considerable attention due to their structural and electronic similarities to high-temperature cuprate superconductors. This latest finding provides a crucial foundation for the development of next-generation superconducting materials, potentially paving the way for innovations in energy transmission and advanced electronics.

## A Subtle "Flaw" in Time Itself?

Finally, let's turn to a captivating theoretical development that touches upon the very foundations of physics. An international group of physicists, supported by the **Foundational Questions Institute (FQxI)**, is proposing that the enigmatic process of **quantum collapse**, where fuzzy quantum possibilities solidify into definite realities, might subtly blur time itself.

Quantum mechanics famously describes particles existing in multiple states simultaneously, a concept known as **superposition**. However, when a quantum system is observed or measured, its **wavefunction** "collapses" into a single outcome, reconciling the quantum world with our everyday experience. While the exact mechanism of this collapse remains one of quantum mechanics' deepest mysteries, new research suggests that these spontaneous collapse processes, potentially linked to gravity, could introduce tiny fluctuations in time.

These minute temporal blurs would be imperceptible to our macroscopic clocks but reveal a hidden limit to the ultimate precision with which time can ever be measured. Published on May 3, 2026, this work offers a tantalizing new path toward unifying quantum physics with gravity, two pillars of modern physics that have famously resisted integration. It's a profound thought: the very act of a quantum possibility becoming real might leave an indelible, if infinitesimal, mark on the flow of time itself.
