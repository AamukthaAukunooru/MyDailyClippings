## A Decade-Long Prediction Manifests in a Quantum Material

In a significant stride for condensed matter physics, researchers have successfully created a **two-dimensional topological crystalline insulator**, a quantum material first predicted over a decade ago. This achievement, announced on July 11, 2026, by physicists from the University of Jyväskylä and Aalto University in Finland, marks the first experimental realization of such a material. The breakthrough was led by Associate Professor Kezilbeiek Shawulienu, in collaboration with Professors Peter Liljeroth and Jose Lado from Aalto University.

The material exhibits unusual **conducting edge states**, which can be precisely controlled through mechanical strain. This characteristic makes it an exceptionally promising candidate for the development of future **room-temperature quantum electronics**, potentially overcoming some of the significant hurdles currently faced in quantum computing and advanced device fabrication. Until now, the challenges in developing the precise materials needed had held back its creation. The ability to manipulate these edge states could pave the way for novel quantum devices that operate efficiently without the need for cryogenic cooling, opening new avenues for technological innovation.

## Peering into the Cosmic Dawn: Quasars and a Rapidly Growing Black Hole

Our understanding of the early universe has received a substantial boost with recent astronomical observations. The European Space Agency's (ESA) **Euclid space telescope**, launched in 2023, has discovered an unprecedented 31 new quasars in the early universe, with 14 of these at or above a redshift of 7. The two most ancient quasars identified boast redshifts of 7.69 and 7.77, setting a new record for the earliest quasars ever found. These luminous beacons emerged just over 13 billion light-years away, within the first 670 million years of the universe's existence. As Professor Hennawi noted, "Every step further back in time makes the puzzle more perplexing: How did the Universe produce supermassive black holes so quickly?".

Adding to this cosmic narrative, an international team led by researchers from the Max Planck Institute for Radio Astronomy (MPIfR) made a first-ever discovery while observing **SDSS J110546.07+145202.4**, a spiral galaxy located approximately 1.8 billion light-years from Earth. For eight years, this galaxy has radiated intensely in the radio spectrum due to an exceptionally active supermassive black hole (SMBH) at its core. This particular black hole, though relatively low in mass, is growing at an extraordinary rate by accreting matter from its disk, triggering the powerful jets observed. Co-author Kovi Rose from the Sydney Institute for Astronomy emphasized that such high-energy events provide a wealth of insights into the physical processes in the universe's most extreme environments, offering a crucial window into the early cosmos.

## The Universe's Grandest Movie Begins: The Legacy Survey of Space and Time

A monumental undertaking in astrophysics officially commenced on July 10, 2026, as the **Vera C. Rubin Observatory** in Chile initiated the **Legacy Survey of Space and Time (LSST)**. This ambitious 10-year endeavor is poised to create the most detailed and comprehensive record of the universe's history ever assembled. Utilizing the world's largest digital camera, mounted on the planet's largest survey telescope, the LSST will capture images of the entire southern sky, far surpassing the depth and coverage of previous ground-based observatories.

The project involves thousands of scientists globally, who will analyze the vast dataset to map potentially billions of galaxies, identify asteroids, scrutinize the solar system, and delve into the mysteries of **dark matter**, which constitutes roughly 80% of the universe's mass. Brian Stone, acting director of the National Science Foundation, aptly described the launch, stating, "Today, we begin filming the greatest cosmic movie ever made". Jonathan Blazek, an associate professor of physics at Northeastern University and a leader in the LSST's dark energy project, highlighted the scale, noting that the Rubin Observatory's camera will capture a new image every 40 seconds, revisiting certain cosmic regions up to 800 times over the decade. This unparalleled data collection promises to redefine our cosmological understanding, offering unprecedented detail on objects both near and far.
