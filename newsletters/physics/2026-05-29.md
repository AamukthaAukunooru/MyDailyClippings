## Superconductivity Shatters a Three-Decade Record

In a monumental achievement with far-reaching implications, scientists at the University of Houston have announced a new world record for **superconductivity** at the highest temperature ever achieved under normal pressure conditions. This breakthrough, detailed in the *Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences*, pushes the transition temperature to an astonishing **151 Kelvin** (approximately minus 122 degrees Celsius), surpassing a record that had stood for more than 30 years.

The ability of certain materials to conduct electricity with **zero resistance** is a holy grail in physics and engineering. Reaching higher operating temperatures under ambient pressure has been a primary goal, as it would make superconducting technologies far more practical and affordable. Imagine electrical grids that transmit power without loss, vastly improved energy storage systems, faster electronics, and advancements in fusion energy and medical imaging. The team, led by physicists Ching-Wu Chu and Liangzi Deng from the Texas Center for Superconductivity (TcSUH) and the University of Houston's Department of Physics, utilized a novel material composition to unlock this enhanced performance. As Professor Chu stated, current grids lose about 8% of electricity during transmission, highlighting the immense potential impact of this discovery. This advancement brings us significantly closer to a future where superconductivity could transform our daily lives, moving from specialized, cryogenically intensive applications to more widespread use.

## The LHC Hints at Physics Beyond the Standard Model

The quest to understand the fundamental forces and particles of the universe has taken another intriguing turn at CERN's **Large Hadron Collider (LHC)**. Recent findings from experiments at the LHC suggest the strongest hints yet of physics extending beyond the well-established **Standard Model**. By meticulously studying incredibly rare particle transformations known as "**penguin decays**," researchers have observed behavior that does not fully align with theoretical predictions.

The Standard Model, our reigning theory for describing the fundamental particles and forces, has been incredibly successful for decades, yet we know it is incomplete. It fails to explain phenomena such as gravity, dark matter, and the perplexing asymmetry between matter and antimatter in the universe. The "penguin decays," which involve the transformation of a B-meson into other particles, are particularly sensitive to the influence of hypothetical new particles or forces. The observed discrepancies, if confirmed, could indicate the presence of unknown particles, such as **leptoquarks**, which would unite different types of matter, or heavier analogues of existing Standard Model particles. This exciting development from CERN, following extensive research, suggests that we might be on the cusp of discovering the first real cracks in the Standard Model, potentially reshaping our understanding of the cosmos. While further experimental verification and theoretical refinement are needed, these hints are a powerful motivator for continued exploration at the energy frontier.

## Antihydrogen Precision Illuminates Antimatter Mystery

Another significant step forward in fundamental physics comes from the ALPHA antihydrogen collaboration at CERN, where physicists, with leading contributions from Swansea University, have achieved an unprecedented level of precision in measuring the **ground state hyperfine splitting of antihydrogen**. This measurement is now 100 times more accurate than any previous attempt, providing invaluable insight into the internal structure of antihydrogen, the elusive antimatter counterpart of hydrogen.

Hydrogen, the most abundant atom in the universe, is well understood. Its antimatter twin, antihydrogen, is purported to be its mirror image, but must be synthesized and studied in highly controlled laboratory environments at CERN. The ability to precisely measure its properties is crucial for testing the fundamental symmetry between matter and antimatter, a principle known as **CPT symmetry**. Any measurable difference between hydrogen and antihydrogen could provide a profound clue to the universe's baffling imbalance, where matter vastly outnumbers antimatter. A key factor in this breakthrough was a novel technique pioneered by the Swansea team, which uses laser-cooled Beryllium ions to sympathetically cool the antielectrons, or **positrons**, used to create antihydrogen. This allowed for a massive increase in the number of trapped antihydrogen atoms, exceeding 25,000 for this experiment, enabling the remarkably precise measurement. Professor Niels Madsen from Swansea University's Department of Physics noted that these finer details in antihydrogen could ultimately reveal a difference that "could cast light on the apparent near absence of antimatter in the Universe". This ongoing work at CERN continues to push the boundaries of antimatter research, bringing us closer to solving one of the universe's greatest mysteries.
