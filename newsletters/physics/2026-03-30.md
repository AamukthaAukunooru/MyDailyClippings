## A New Frontier in Superconductivity: 151 Kelvin at Ambient Pressure

The quest for room-temperature superconductors, materials that can conduct electricity with zero resistance without extreme cooling, has long been a holy grail in physics. This week, we've taken a significant stride closer to that goal. Researchers at the University of Houston, led by physicists Ching-Wu Chu and Liangzi Deng, have announced a groundbreaking achievement: superconductivity at a record-setting 151 Kelvin (approximately -122 degrees Celsius or -188 degrees Fahrenheit) under **ambient pressure**. This is the highest temperature ever recorded for a superconductor operating without the need for immense external pressure, a major hurdle for practical applications.

The implications of this breakthrough, published in the *Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences*, are profound. Current power grids lose about 8% of transmitted electricity due to resistance. Imagine a world where that loss is eliminated, dramatically reducing energy waste and costs. Beyond energy transmission, this advance could revolutionize technologies ranging from advanced medical imaging to more efficient electronics and even new forms of propulsion. The team, based at the Texas Center for Superconductivity (TcSUH), utilized a novel material composition to achieve this unprecedented transition temperature. While 151 Kelvin is still far from "room temperature," achieving it at normal atmospheric pressure is a critical step, opening new pathways for material scientists to explore and potentially engineer even higher-temperature superconductors for widespread use.

## Unveiling Water's Hidden Critical Point

Water, the very essence of life, is a remarkably strange substance. Unlike most liquids, it becomes less dense as it freezes, and its density peaks at 4 degrees Celsius. Its high heat capacity and surface tension are also anomalous. For decades, scientists have theorized the existence of a **hidden critical point** in supercooled water, a theoretical juncture where two distinct liquid forms of water, with different molecular bonding structures, merge into a single phase. This week, researchers at Stockholm University have finally confirmed this long-suspected feature, using ultra-fast X-ray lasers to capture water at extreme conditions before it could freeze.

Published in *Science*, the study reveals that this critical point occurs at approximately -63 degrees Celsius and 1000 atmospheres of pressure. Near this point, water becomes incredibly unstable, rapidly fluctuating between its two liquid states. Even under everyday conditions, these fluctuations influence water's behavior, explaining many of its peculiar properties. Robin Tyburski, a researcher in Chemical Physics at Stockholm University, noted that approaching this critical point is "almost like a Black Hole," due to the dramatic slowing of molecular motion. This discovery, made possible by advanced X-ray laser techniques, not only deepens our fundamental understanding of water, but also has significant implications for fields ranging from climate science to biology, as water's unique properties are crucial for virtually all biological processes.

## Solving the 50-Year Mystery of Gamma Cassiopeiae's X-rays

In the realm of astrophysics, a half-century-old puzzle concerning one of the sky's most prominent stars has finally been solved. **Gamma Cassiopeiae**, a bright, naked-eye star in the constellation Cassiopeia, has baffled astronomers since 1976 with its unusually powerful and variable X-ray emissions, far more intense than expected from a typical massive star. This week, an international team of astronomers, led by researchers at the University of Liège, Belgium, and utilizing Japan's **XRISM space telescope**, pinpointed the source of these enigmatic X-rays: a **hidden white dwarf companion**.

The findings, published in *Astronomy & Astrophysics*, indicate that the white dwarf is not merely orbiting Gamma Cassiopeiae, but is actively pulling material from the massive star's ejected disk. This accretion process generates extreme heat and the observed X-rays, which reach temperatures above 100 million degrees. The highly precise observations from XRISM's Resolve instrument allowed scientists to link the X-ray emissions directly to the orbital motion of the white dwarf, rather than Gamma Cassiopeiae itself. This discovery not only resolves a decades-long mystery, but also confirms the existence of a long-predicted type of binary star system, a **Be + white dwarf binary**, which had never been clearly identified before. Yaël Nazé of the University of Liège expressed the culmination of intense effort, stating, "There has been an intense effort to solve the mystery of gamma-Cas across many research groups for many decades. And now, thanks to the high-precision observations of XRISM, we have finally done it."
