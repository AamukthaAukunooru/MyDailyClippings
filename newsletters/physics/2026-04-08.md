## Mu2e Experiment Nears Search for New Physics

A significant milestone has been achieved at the Fermi National Accelerator Laboratory (Fermilab), where researchers from Argonne National Laboratory have successfully commissioned a critical subsystem for the **Mu2e (muon-to-electron conversion) experiment**. This multiyear international collaboration, involving over 30 institutions and 200 scientists, aims to observe an exceptionally rare particle physics process: a muon transforming directly into an electron without emitting any other particles.

The newly commissioned component is the **Cosmic Ray Veto (CRV) detector**, designed by an Argonne team of high-energy physics scientists. Its primary function is to filter out background noise, specifically signals from cosmic-ray muons, which could mimic the rare conversion event Mu2e seeks to detect. According to Argonne physicist Yuri Oksuzian, who played a key role in the CRV's development, "The CRV detector will enable Mu2e to more reliably and accurately detect an event expected to be vanishingly rare according to current particle physics theory."

Observing even a few instances of this muon-to-electron conversion would be a profound discovery, providing "strong evidence of new physics" beyond the established Standard Model. The Standard Model predicts this transition to be so infrequent that its detection would imply the involvement of a new particle or force, fundamentally altering our understanding of how the universe operates. With the CRV detector successfully tested and installed, other Mu2e subsystems are slated for commissioning over the next year, with the experiment expected to commence data collection in 2027. Scientists like Oksuzian, Sam Grant, and Simon Corrodi from Argonne, alongside Ralf Ehrlich from the University of Virginia, are at the forefront of this ambitious endeavor.

## A New Blueprint for Detecting Spacetime Fluctuations

The elusive quest to reconcile **gravity** with **quantum mechanics**, one of physics' most enduring challenges, has received a crucial theoretical boost. Researchers led by the University of Warwick have introduced the first unified approach for identifying tiny, random distortions in the structure of **spacetime** itself, often referred to as "spacetime fluctuations" or "ripples".

These minute variations were initially proposed by physicist John Wheeler and are a predicted outcome in several leading theories of **quantum gravity**. However, the diverse predictions from different theories have historically complicated experimental efforts, leaving scientists uncertain about the specific signals to search for. The new research, published in *Nature Communications*, addresses this by categorizing spacetime fluctuations into three primary types based on their behavior across space and time. For each category, the team has identified clear, measurable patterns that could be detected using highly sensitive instruments.

This breakthrough means that powerful tools like the **Laser Interferometer Gravitational-Wave Observatory (LIGO)**, along with smaller, specialized tabletop experiments such as QUEST and GQuEST being developed in the UK and USA, could begin testing competing quantum gravity theories much sooner than previously anticipated. By providing a concrete framework for experimental detection, this work transforms abstract theoretical concepts into tangible signals, moving us closer to understanding the quantum nature of gravity.

## Memory Device Operates at Extreme Temperatures, Unlocking New AI Potential

In a development that could revolutionize electronics for harsh environments and advanced computing, engineers at the University of Southern California (USC) have created a breakthrough **memory device** capable of operating at temperatures hotter than molten lava. This tiny component maintains its functionality even at a staggering 700°C (1300°F), dramatically exceeding the approximately 200°C limit of conventional electronic devices.

The team, led by Joshua Yang, Arthur B. Freeman Chair Professor at the USC Viterbi School of Engineering, and with Jian Zhao as the study's first author, achieved this by constructing the device from an unusual stack of ultra-durable materials. It utilizes **tungsten** for the top electrode, **hafnium oxide ceramic** in the middle, and **graphene**, a single-atom-thick sheet of carbon, for the bottom layer. Tungsten is known for having the highest melting point of any element, while graphene boasts exceptional strength and heat resistance.

The performance of this novel device is remarkable: it retained data for over 50 hours at 700°C without needing refreshment, endured more than one billion switching cycles at that extreme temperature, and operated efficiently at just 1.5 volts with speeds measured in tens of nanoseconds. Published in *Science* on March 26, 2026, this accidental discovery revealed a powerful new atomic-level mechanism that prevents heat-induced failure. This advancement could pave the way for more robust electronics in applications ranging from space exploration and industrial monitoring to potentially enabling faster and more energy-efficient **AI computations** by overcoming thermal limitations.
