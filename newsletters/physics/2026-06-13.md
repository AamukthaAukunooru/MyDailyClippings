## Paving a One-Way Street for Quantum Signals

In a significant stride towards more robust quantum technologies, scientists at the RIKEN Center for Quantum Computing have unveiled a novel theoretical framework for achieving **one-way quantum synchronization**. This breakthrough, announced on June 12, addresses a persistent challenge in quantum systems, namely their extreme susceptibility to environmental noise and manufacturing imperfections.

The team, including researchers Franco Nori, Adam Miranowicz, and Deng-Gao Lai, proposed a method that combines two distinct quantum effects to create a form of synchronization that allows information to flow in only one direction, much like a one-way street for sound particles, or **phonons**. This **nonreciprocal quantum synchronization** is designed to be remarkably stable, even in the presence of the very real-world flaws that often hinder practical quantum device development.

Traditional quantum systems struggle with decoherence and noise, which can quickly destroy their delicate quantum states. By enabling a one-way flow of information, these researchers are laying the groundwork for more resilient quantum components. Such nonreciprocal devices are already vital in classical microwave and optical systems, directing signals and minimizing unwanted reflections. Extending this capability to the quantum domain could have profound implications for future quantum signal processing and even quantum cloaking technologies. This theoretical advance promises a new foundation for generating "fragile-to-robust nonreciprocal quantum resources," opening pathways for their future practical application.

## A Brain-Inspired Chip Thrives at Near Absolute Zero

The quest for scalable and stable quantum computers has received a significant boost from an unexpected quarter: a brain-inspired chip capable of operating at temperatures just above **absolute zero**. Researchers at the University of Hong Kong (HKU) have engineered a remarkable new type of neuromorphic hardware that functions effectively at chilling temperatures as low as 10 millikelvin (mK).

Unveiled on June 12, this development by Professor Yuhao Zhang and PhD student Xin Yang from HKU's Department of Electrical and Computer Engineering and the Centre for Advanced Semiconductors and Integrated Circuits (CASIC), utilizes a standard **silicon carbide (SiC) transistor** in an innovative way. They demonstrated that a single transistor could mimic the energy-efficient "spiking" activity of biological neurons, firing electrical pulses akin to those in the human brain.

The critical challenge in quantum computing lies in controlling highly sensitive **qubits**, which must be maintained at millikelvin temperatures. Existing classical control electronics generate considerable heat and consume significant power, necessitating their physical separation from the qubits. This new brain-inspired chip, by operating directly within the cryogenic environment, could dramatically reduce the thermal load and power consumption, enabling the development of larger and more integrated quantum computers. The team's work introduces a novel method for controlling **negative differential resistance (NDR)** in SiC MOSFETs, paving the way for more efficient and robust cryogenic electronics, not just for quantum computing, but potentially for future deep-space missions as well.

## Universe's Accelerating Expansion Confirmed, Crisis Averted

Cosmologists can breathe a collective sigh of relief as a new study has robustly reaffirmed that the universe's expansion is indeed accelerating, dispelling recent claims to the contrary. An international team of astrophysicists, including Nobel laureates Professor Adam Riess and Professor Brian Schmidt, definitively refuted a 2025 study that suggested a slowdown in cosmic growth.

Published on June 11, the research, led by the University of Southampton, meticulously re-evaluated data from **Type Ia supernovae**, which serve as cosmic "standard candles" for measuring vast intergalactic distances. The 2025 claims had posited that the brightness of these supernovae might vary with the universe's age, potentially misleading astronomers into believing the cosmos was accelerating when it was, in fact, decelerating.

However, by carefully calibrating the supernovae data and accounting for different host environments and stellar populations, the Southampton-led team found that "the evidence for cosmic acceleration remains remarkably consistent." This powerful confirmation solidifies the prevailing theory that a mysterious force known as **dark energy** is driving the universe apart at an ever-increasing rate. The original discovery of this accelerating expansion, which earned Riess, Schmidt, and Saul Perlmutter the Nobel Prize in Physics in 2011, stands firm, underscoring the validity of nearly three decades of astronomical progress and our fundamental understanding of the cosmos.
