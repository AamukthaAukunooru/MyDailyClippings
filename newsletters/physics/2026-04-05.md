## Electrons Go Four-Dimensional in Moiré Crystals

Our understanding of quantum materials has taken a fascinating turn, with MIT physicists announcing the creation of **3D "moiré crystals"** that effectively simulate **four-dimensional quantum materials**. Published recently in *Nature*, this work from researchers including Pablo Jarillo-Herrero, the Cecil and Ida Green Professor of Physics, and Raymond Ashoori, both at MIT, pushes the boundaries of how we conceive of electron behavior. Typically, electrons in our everyday metals move in three dimensions. However, in these newly engineered moiré crystals, which are formed from two different interfering atomic lattices, electrons exhibit movement that suggests they are "teleporting" into and out of a mysterious fourth spatial dimension.

This isn't to say we've found a portal to another dimension, but rather that the intricate geometry of the moiré superlattice creates an emergent **"4D superspace" lattice** that dictates electron motion in an analogous way. The phenomenon, which involves **quantum tunneling**, allows electrons to behave as if they are navigating this higher-dimensional space. The implications are profound, potentially unlocking new pathways for realizing theoretical predictions of higher-dimensional superconductivity and topological properties in a laboratory setting. Professor Joe Checkelsky's lab at MIT also developed a new, scalable technique for generating these moiré materials, overcoming a significant bottleneck in their fabrication and potentially accelerating research into next-generation electronic applications.

## Quantum Computing Edges Closer with Ultra-Efficient Error Correction

The promise of **quantum computing** often feels just beyond our grasp, largely due to the formidable challenge of **quantum error correction**. However, recent theoretical advancements from a team at the California Institute of Technology (Caltech) and a new startup, Oratomic, suggest that the hardware requirements for building a fault-tolerant quantum computer might be drastically reduced, by as much as 100 times. This breakthrough, detailed in a preprint on arXiv, proposes a design that could bring the number of **qubits** needed to run complex algorithms, like Shor's algorithm for breaking encryption, down from an estimated 1 million to a more achievable 10,000 to 20,000 qubits.

The key lies in what physicist Manuel Endres, a professor at Caltech, describes as "ultra-efficient error correction," specifically tailored for **neutral-atom quantum computers**. Unlike other quantum computing architectures where qubits are static, neutral-atom qubits can be physically moved and connected over longer distances. This flexibility is crucial for their new error correction scheme, which reportedly can create one virtual, fault-tolerant qubit from as few as five physical qubits, a significant improvement over previous estimates requiring hundreds. The immediate launch of Oratomic, a Pasadena-based startup aiming to build these machines, underscores the tangible excitement and potential impact of this theoretical leap, suggesting a functioning quantum computer capable of breaking common encryption could be years, rather than decades, away.

## An "Ancient Immigrant" Star Offers a Glimpse of the Early Universe

From the microscopic to the cosmic, another compelling story comes from the field of astrophysics, where a group of undergraduate students at the University of Chicago has made an extraordinary discovery: one of the oldest stars ever observed, an "ancient immigrant" now drifting within our Milky Way galaxy. What began as a class project, combing through vast datasets from the **Sloan Digital Sky Survey (SDSS)**, led to the identification of an exceptionally **"pristine" star** composed almost entirely of hydrogen and helium. This elemental purity is a strong indicator that the star formed very early in cosmic history, making it a true time capsule from the universe's dawn.

Led by Professor Alex Ji, the deputy Project Scientist for SDSS-V, along with graduate teaching assistants Hillary Andales and Pierre Thibodeaux, the ten students meticulously analyzed thousands of stars before selecting 77 for further observation at the Las Campanas Observatory. The star, designated **SDSS J0715-7334**, revealed an astonishingly low carbon content, suggesting a unique formation pathway influenced by an early sprinkling of cosmic dust, a scenario only observed once before. Tracing its trajectory backward over billions of years, the team determined that this ancient star did not originate in our galaxy, but rather formed in the nearby **Large Magellanic Cloud** before being gravitationally pulled into the Milky Way. This serendipitous discovery by budding astronomers provides invaluable insights into the conditions and processes that shaped the earliest stars and galaxies in our universe.
