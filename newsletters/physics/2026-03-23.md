## A Heavier Cousin Joins the Proton Family

Particle physicists are abuzz with the latest news from CERN, where the **LHCb experiment** at the **Large Hadron Collider (LHC)** has announced the discovery of a new particle, the **Xi-cc-plus** (Ξcc⁺). This exotic baryon is a heavier relative of the familiar proton, distinguished by its composition: two **charm quarks** and one **down quark**. For context, a standard proton consists of two up quarks and one down quark. The presence of two much heavier charm quarks gives the Ξcc⁺ a mass approximately four times that of a proton, making it a truly substantial addition to the particle zoo.

This discovery, presented at the Moriond conference and reported by CERN on March 17, 2026, marks the first new particle identified since the significant upgrades to the LHCb detector were completed in 2023. The enhanced luminosity, allowing for more collisions, and a world-first all-software trigger system, which processes data in real-time without hardware preselection, were crucial in spotting this short-lived particle. According to LHCb Spokesperson Vincenzo Vagnoni, this is only the second time a baryon with two heavy quarks has been observed, with the first being nearly a decade ago.

The Ξcc⁺ is not just another particle; its observation provides a vital testbed for **quantum chromodynamics (QCD)**, the fundamental theory describing the strong force that binds quarks together to form protons, neutrons, and other **hadrons**. Understanding how this strong force operates within such a unique configuration, especially with two heavy quarks, will help theorists refine their models and potentially shed light on more exotic hadronic structures like tetraquarks and pentaquarks. Researchers from institutions like the University of Manchester played a key role in this international collaboration, which involved over 1,000 scientists from more than 20 countries. The detection of the Ξcc⁺, observed through its decay into lighter particles in 2024 collision data, resolves a long-standing question in particle physics, confirming predictions and settling a debate that has spanned over two decades regarding its existence.

## Unveiling a 48-Dimensional Quantum Information Alphabet

In a development that could profoundly impact the future of quantum computing and secure communication, scientists have uncovered a hidden, incredibly complex topological structure within entangled light, reaching a staggering **48 dimensions**. This groundbreaking work, published on March 21, 2026, by researchers at the University of the Witwatersrand in South Africa, in collaboration with Huzhou University, reveals that standard methods for producing entangled photons can contain a vast new "alphabet" for encoding stable quantum information.

The key to this discovery lies in the **orbital angular momentum (OAM)** of light, a property that describes how light twists as it propagates. While previously thought to contribute simpler structures, this team demonstrated that OAM can give rise to significantly richer and higher-dimensional topologies than ever recognized. In their experiments, they found over 17,000 distinct topological signatures, which essentially means an enormous increase in the capacity to store and transmit quantum information per photon. This finding challenges previous assumptions that such complex topology would require multiple properties of light. Instead, it can emerge from a single property: OAM.

This breakthrough comes at a time when the field of **quantum information science** is rapidly accelerating. Indeed, just recently, on March 18, 2026, Canadian computer scientist Gilles Brassard and American physicist Charles Bennett were awarded the prestigious **Turing Award** for their foundational contributions to this very field, including their work on unbreakable quantum communication and demonstrating quantum teleportation. The ability to encode information in such high-dimensional, topologically protected states could offer unprecedented robustness against noise, a major hurdle in building practical quantum technologies. This means more stable **qubits** and potentially far more powerful quantum computers, pushing us closer to a truly secure and quantum-enabled future.

## New Windows into Superconductivity's Secrets

The quest for **room-temperature superconductors**, materials that can conduct electricity with zero resistance without extreme cooling, remains one of condensed matter physics' "holy grails." Recent research from Harvard and MIT has provided crucial new insights into the intricate mechanisms governing these fascinating materials, taking us closer to this transformative goal.

On March 19, 2026, a team of Harvard physicists, led by Professor Norman Yao and Associate Professor Chris Laumann of Boston University, published new findings in *Nature*. They developed a novel method using **quantum sensors** to study materials at high pressure, adapting a device pioneered by Nobel laureate Percy Bridgman. Their work revealed that what appears to be a single sample of a promising superconductor, such as a nickelate, should actually be viewed as a collection of micron-scale regions that exhibit mysteriously uneven superconducting performance. This ability to "sniff around this neighborhood better," as Laumann put it, will allow researchers to engineer more efficient materials by understanding and controlling these local variations.

Concurrently, on March 18, 2026, MIT physicists announced in *Nature* the creation of a powerful new **terahertz microscope** that allowed them to directly observe "hidden quantum jiggling" inside superconductors for the first time. By compressing normally unwieldy terahertz light into a tiny region, they were able to witness electrons moving collectively in a frictionless, wave-like state within a material called **bismuth strontium calcium copper oxide (BSCCO)**. Terahertz radiation, which pulses more than a trillion times per second, is ideally suited to probe the natural vibrations of atoms and electrons. This breakthrough offers an unprecedented window into the fundamental workings of superconductors, potentially driving future advances in high-speed wireless communication and energy-efficient technologies by better understanding how these materials achieve their remarkable properties. These complementary approaches, from understanding microscopic inhomogeneities to directly observing quantum motions, are essential steps on the long road to harnessing superconductivity for widespread practical applications.
