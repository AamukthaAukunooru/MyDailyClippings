## The Emergence of Time in a "Mini Universe"

One of the most profound questions in physics asks if time is a fundamental aspect of reality or an emergent phenomenon. Recent groundbreaking work by scientists at the University of Birmingham, published on July 9, 2026, offers compelling experimental evidence for the latter. Researchers constructed a "mini universe" in the lab using **24,000 ultracold atoms**, cooled to mere billionths of a degree above absolute zero. This isolated quantum system allowed them to observe how the flow of time could emerge naturally from internal changes within the system, without relying on an external clock.

Professor Giovanni Barontini, the lead physicist on this project, demonstrated that the passage of time could be measured purely through the behavior of the quantum system itself. Specifically, the experiment revealed that "time" arose from changes in the disorder, or **entropy**, of the atoms as they moved between distinct bright and dark regions within the isolated setup. This finding is particularly significant because several modern physics theories, such as the **Wheeler-DeWitt equation**, suggest that the universe may not possess an inherent, universal clock. Instead, the familiar progression of time might arise from the intricate relationships and evolution between different parts of a system. The team even found that a version of the **Schrödinger equation**, a cornerstone of quantum mechanics, could be expressed using this entropic time, allowing for predictions of quantum system evolution even without an external clock. This research, published in *Physical Review Research*, opens new avenues for testing theories of **quantum gravity** and exploring the origins of time itself.

## AI Takes on Neutrino Mass: Automating Theoretical Physics

The intersection of artificial intelligence and fundamental physics continues to yield fascinating results. On July 9, 2026, physicists at the University of California, Irvine, unveiled an AI system capable of autonomously designing theoretical physics models. This system, dubbed **Autonomous Model Builder (AMBer)**, represents a significant leap, as model building has traditionally been a task reserved for human theorists. Led by UC Irvine doctoral candidates Victoria Knapp-Pérez and Jake Rudolph, AMBer leverages **reinforcement learning** to explore vast, uncharted territories of particle physics theory.

One of AMBer's initial applications is tackling the enduring mystery of **neutrino mass**. The **Standard Model of particle physics** does not account for the fact that neutrinos, though tiny, possess a non-zero mass, making it one of the field's major outstanding challenges. AMBer constructs theoretical models by selecting mathematical symmetry groups, determining particle inclusions, and assigning their behavior under chosen symmetries. By evaluating its own choices and improving through trial and error, the system efficiently narrows down the immense space of possible theories to identify promising candidates. Knapp-Pérez emphasized that AMBer is designed to assist, not replace, human physicists, acting as a powerful filter to provide a more informed starting point for studying complex neutrino models. This work, published in *Nature Communications Physics*, showcases the growing power of AI to accelerate discovery in even the most abstract domains of science.

## Mapping the Cosmos: The Legacy Survey of Space and Time Begins

For those of us who gaze skyward, the prospect of an unprecedented cosmic census is truly thrilling. The **Legacy Survey of Space and Time (LSST)** officially commenced on July 10, 2026, from the **Vera C. Rubin Observatory** in Chile. This monumental 10-year endeavor aims to create the most detailed and comprehensive record of the universe's history ever assembled. Brian Stone, acting director of the National Science Foundation, aptly described it as "filming the greatest cosmic movie ever made".

The LSST utilizes the world's largest digital camera, mounted on the largest survey telescope on the planet, to capture images of the entire southern sky. Over its decade-long run, the observatory will revisit some regions of the universe up to 800 times, generating an astronomical dataset of unparalleled size. This wealth of imagery will be used by thousands of scientists globally to map billions of galaxies, discover asteroids, analyze our solar system, and, crucially, delve into the secrets of **dark matter** and **dark energy**. Jonathan Blazek, an associate professor of physics at Northeastern University leading the LSST's dark energy project, highlighted that this project will provide more detailed observations of distant objects and how the cosmos evolves. Early data from a pre-launch survey already yielded over 11,000 previously undiscovered asteroids, including 33 near-Earth objects, underscoring the immense potential of this ambitious undertaking.
