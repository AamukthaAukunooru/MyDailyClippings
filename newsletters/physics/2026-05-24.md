## The Sun's Stubborn Signal: A 19-Day Radio Mystery

Our closest star, the Sun, continues to surprise us, and a recent observation has left NASA scientists quite astonished. A solar radio burst, far from the fleeting events we typically observe, persisted for an incredible **19 days**, shattering all previous records. This unusual longevity has captivated researchers, who usually see such bursts last mere hours or, at most, a few days.

The team, utilizing a fleet of spacecraft strategically spread across our solar system, meticulously traced the origin of this mysterious signal. Their investigation pointed to a massive magnetic structure on the Sun's surface, known as a **helmet streamer**. What makes this event particularly intriguing is the hypothesis that the prolonged burst might have been sustained by a series of three **coronal mass ejections (CMEs)**, all erupting from the same solar region. These powerful explosions release vast clouds of charged particles and magnetic energy into space, and their repeated occurrence in conjunction with the radio burst suggests a complex interplay within the Sun's atmosphere. Published in *Astrophysical Journal Letters*, these findings are crucial for enhancing our understanding of these rare, long-duration solar radio bursts and, consequently, improving **space weather forecasting**. Better predictions of such events are vital for protecting our increasingly vulnerable satellite infrastructure from potentially damaging solar activity.

## Probing the Standard Model: New Hints and High Honors

In the intricate world of particle physics, the quest to understand the fundamental building blocks of the universe often involves pushing the boundaries of the **Standard Model**, our most comprehensive theory of nature's fundamental particles and forces. Recent developments offer both a celebration of past precision and intriguing hints of what might lie beyond.

Firstly, the collaborative efforts behind the **Muon g-2 experiment** were recently recognized with a prestigious honor, as several physicists, including those from the University of Michigan, were among the recipients of the **2026 Breakthrough Prize in Fundamental Physics**. This award acknowledges decades of painstaking work by three overlapping collaborations to measure the **anomalous magnetic moment of the muon** with unprecedented precision. Muons, those heavier cousins of electrons, exhibit a slight "wobble" in a magnetic field, and the precise measurement of this wobble provides a critical benchmark for the Standard Model. The experiment achieved an extraordinary precision of **127 parts per billion**, a mind-boggling 30,000 times more precise than the initial g-2 experiment. This incredible accuracy helps scientists rigorously test the Standard Model, probing for any discrepancies that could signal the existence of new particles or forces.

Adding to this excitement are fresh hints emerging from **CERN's Large Hadron Collider (LHC)**, suggesting that certain particle decays may not be behaving entirely as the Standard Model predicts. Specifically, physicists working on the LHCb experiment have detected a highly unusual pattern in rare **B meson decays**. These results, if verified, could represent some of the strongest recent evidence for **physics beyond the Standard Model**. The Standard Model, while incredibly successful, is known to be incomplete, lacking explanations for phenomena like gravity and dark matter. The anomalies observed in B meson decays could be a window into new theories, potentially involving hypothetical particles such as **leptoquarks** or heavier analogues of known Standard Model particles. The continued scrutiny of these rare processes at the LHC is a thrilling endeavor, potentially paving the way for revolutionary discoveries in our understanding of fundamental reality.

## Time Crystals Edge Towards Practicality

From the farthest reaches of space to the most fundamental aspects of matter, our understanding is constantly evolving. In the realm of quantum physics, a peculiar state of matter known as a **time crystal** has taken a significant leap towards real-world applications. Proposed by Nobel Prize-winning physicist Frank Wilczek in 2012, time crystals are quantum systems that exhibit a repeating pattern in time, much like a conventional crystal repeats a pattern in space, but without requiring any external energy input. They essentially "tick" forever in their lowest energy state.

While their existence was experimentally confirmed in 2016, the challenge has been to interact with them in a controlled manner. Now, researchers at **Aalto University's Department of Applied Physics**, led by Academy Research Fellow Jere Mäkinen, have achieved a major milestone by successfully linking a time crystal to an external device for the first time. The team converted a time crystal into an **optomechanical system**, demonstrating that they can actually control its behavior by connecting it to a tiny mechanical oscillator. This breakthrough, published in *Nature Communications*, is not merely a theoretical curiosity. It opens the door to powerful new technologies, with potential applications ranging from highly precise sensors to improved memory systems for **quantum computers**, significantly enhancing their performance. The ability to manipulate these perpetually ticking quantum systems marks a crucial step in harnessing their unique properties for future quantum technologies.
