## Quantum Computing Takes a Giant Leap Forward

In a potentially transformative development for the field of quantum computing, scientists at the California Institute of Technology (Caltech), in collaboration with the Pasadena-based startup Oratomic, have unveiled a theoretical design that could drastically reduce the hardware requirements for building fault-tolerant quantum machines. This breakthrough suggests that the amount of hardware needed could be slashed by approximately 100 times, a significant hurdle overcome in the race to practical quantum computing.

The research, detailed in a preprint on arXiv, focuses on ultra-efficient **quantum error correction** using **neutral-atom qubits**. Traditional quantum error correction methods often demand hundreds of physical qubits to form a single, reliable logical qubit. The new approach, however, promises to reduce this overhead by more than 100-fold. This means that a neutral-atom quantum computer with as few as 10,000 qubits could theoretically be sufficient to execute Peter Shor's algorithm, a computational task that would famously break modern cryptographic schemes.

Among the senior authors of this pivotal work are Caltech's Richard P. Feynman Professor of Theoretical Physics, John Preskill, who noted he has been working on fault-tolerant quantum computing for decades, and expressed that "Now at last we're getting close". Also instrumental were Hsin-Yuan (Robert) Huang, an assistant professor of theoretical physics at Caltech and Oratomic's Chief Technology Officer, and Dolev Bluvstein, a visiting associate in physics at Caltech and Oratomic's Chief Executive Officer. The accelerated timeline implied by these findings underscores the urgency for a global transition to **post-quantum encryption standards**, a shift global guidelines currently target for 2035.

## Unraveling a Fusion Reactor Mystery

The quest for clean, abundant fusion energy just got a significant boost, as physicists at Princeton University have reportedly solved a long-standing mystery within **tokamaks**, the donut-shaped machines designed to harness fusion. For years, experiments revealed an inexplicable imbalance: escaping plasma particles consistently struck one side of the exhaust system, known as the **divertor**, far more than the other. This asymmetry baffled scientists, as existing simulations failed to account for it.

Now, researchers have discovered that the **toroidal rotation** of the plasma, its motion as it circles around the tokamak, plays a crucial role. This rotation, working in conjunction with sideways particle drift, creates the observed imbalance in particle distribution at the divertor plates. The team utilized the **SOLPS-ITER modeling code** to simulate particle behavior under various conditions. Their results, published in *Physical Review Letters*, demonstrated that simulations only accurately matched real-world measurements when plasma rotation was incorporated alongside cross-field drifts.

This newfound understanding is more than just an academic curiosity, it has major implications for the engineering of future fusion reactors. Accurately predicting where heat and particles will concentrate is essential for designing divertors that can withstand the extreme conditions of a fusion environment and operate efficiently. Better predictions mean engineers can build more resilient and effective exhaust systems, bringing us closer to a viable fusion power plant.

## A New Perspective on the Big Bang

Cosmology is abuzz with a fresh theoretical framework that could reshape our understanding of the universe's earliest moments. Physicists from the University of Waterloo and the Perimeter Institute in Canada have proposed a new theory suggesting that the rapid expansion of the universe after its birth, a period known as **inflation**, emerges naturally from a deeper and more complete theory: **quadratic gravity**.

This work, published in *Physical Review Letters*, tackles one of the most significant challenges in modern physics: unifying quantum mechanics and general relativity. While Einstein's theory of general relativity masterfully describes gravity on large scales, it breaks down under the extreme conditions present at the universe's origin. The new approach posits that by treating gravity in a way that remains mathematically consistent at extremely high energies, the universe's explosive early growth arises organically, without the need for additional assumptions often introduced in current Big Bang models.

According to Jerome Quintin, a co-author and theoretical cosmologist, quadratic gravity can be thought of as "Einstein raised to the second power". Crucially, this model is not just theoretical; it makes testable predictions. It forecasts a minimum level of **primordial gravitational waves**, tiny ripples in spacetime generated in the first moments after the Big Bang. Senior author Niayesh Afshordi, a physicist, emphasized that "This work shows that quantum gravity can absolutely be studied and bridged to concrete cosmological scenarios, which come with specific predictions that we can test now and in the future as well". Future generations of gravitational wave detectors may soon provide the observational evidence needed to confirm or refute this bold new vision of cosmic origins.
