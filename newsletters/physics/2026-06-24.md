## Unveiling Quantum Oscillations in an Enigmatic Insulator

A significant stride in condensed matter physics comes from a collaborative effort that has shed new light on the mysterious behavior of the **Kondo insulator YbB12**. Researchers from Tokyo University of Science, The University of Tokyo, and Kobe University have unexpectedly observed magnetic **quantum oscillations** in this material, but only after it transitions into a metallic state. Their findings, published in *Physical Review B* on June 16, 2026, were even selected as an Editors' Suggestion, highlighting their significance.

Kondo insulators are a fascinating class of materials that act as electrical insulators at low temperatures, yet exhibit properties typically associated with metals, such as strong electron correlations. The presence of quantum oscillations, usually a hallmark of free-moving charge carriers in metals, has been a puzzling observation in some insulators. The team, led by Dr. Ryosuke Kurihara of Tokyo University of Science, employed ultrasonic measurements to investigate YbB12. They found no such oscillations when the material was in its insulating phase. However, clear signals emerged once the material underwent a field-induced transition to a metallic state. This suggests that the previously observed quantum oscillations in YbB12 are intrinsically linked to its metallic phase, resolving a long-standing paradox. The researchers propose that the absence of oscillations in the insulating state might be due to weak coupling between proposed fermionic quasiparticles and acoustic phonons, the vibrations that carry sound through the material. Furthermore, the large **cyclotron mass** of these quasiparticles could suppress the oscillatory signal. This work provides crucial insights into the unusual quantum behavior of topological Kondo insulators, paving the way for a more comprehensive understanding of these next-generation quantum materials.

## Pioneering a 3D View of the Pion

In the realm of particle physics, a team of nuclear theorists has achieved an unprecedented look into the heart of one of the universe's most fundamental particles, the **pion**. Utilizing the immense computational power of the Polaris supercomputer at the U.S. Department of Energy's (DOE) Argonne National Laboratory, researchers from Argonne and DOE's Brookhaven National Laboratory have revealed the **3D internal structure** of the pion in exquisite detail.

Pions are subatomic particles crucial for mediating the **strong nuclear force**, the fundamental interaction that binds protons and neutrons together within atomic nuclei. Understanding the pion's internal dynamics is therefore critical to explaining how matter forms at its most fundamental level. The team employed complex **Monte-Carlo simulations of lattice quantum chromodynamics**, a technique pioneered at Brookhaven Lab, to perform first-principles calculations. This allowed them to generate high-resolution images of the **quark structure** within a moving pion, specifically revealing the transverse spatial distributions of quarks carrying different fractions of the pion's momentum. According to Yong Zhao, an Argonne physicist and principal investigator on the project, their results show that the transverse size of the pion decreases as its momentum increases, a pattern also observed in the proton. Crucially, the effective size of the pion was found to be smaller than that of the proton at moderate parallel pion momentum values. This groundbreaking 3D imaging of the pion's quark **generalized parton distribution (GPD)** offers fundamental new insights into the strong force and how quarks are confined within composite particles.

## Quantum Systems Challenge the Arrow of Time

Our everyday experience dictates that time flows in one direction, ever forward. However, at the quantum scale, the rules can be far more flexible. New research from Los Alamos National Laboratory, published on June 22, 2026, in *Physical Review X*, has demonstrated quantum control techniques that can make a quantum system's behavior appear consistent with time flowing backward, or at least suppress its perceived arrow of time.

The microscopic laws governing quantum systems are often reversible, meaning the equations work equally well if time runs forward or backward. In classical physics, observation usually has minimal impact on a system, but in quantum physics, measurement can randomly alter a system's state, contributing to our perception of a unidirectional "arrow of time." The Los Alamos team ingeniously utilized precise measurements and feedback mechanisms to engineer **stochastic trajectories** that mimic time-reversed evolution. They achieved this by developing a **control Hamiltonian**, essentially a programmed sequence of fields and pulses, that could imitate the effects of these measurements. As a compelling demonstration, the researchers used their approach to design a **measurement-powered engine** capable of extracting energy directly from the act of monitoring a quantum system. This breakthrough not only offers a novel avenue for exploring one of physics' most fundamental concepts, the nature of time, but also holds promise for practical applications. The ability to manipulate how energy moves into and out of a quantum system could lead to new methods for preparing quantum states and even developing novel forms of **quantum batteries**, where quantum measurements themselves become a thermodynamic resource.
