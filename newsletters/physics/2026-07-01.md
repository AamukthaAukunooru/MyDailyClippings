## The LHC Powers Down for a Luminous Future

The world of particle physics marked a significant transition this week as the **Large Hadron Collider (LHC)** at CERN, near Geneva, concluded its third data-taking period, Run 3, on June 29, 2026. The colossal accelerator has now entered a planned four-year shutdown, known as Long Shutdown 3 (LS3), to prepare for its next incarnation, the **High-Luminosity LHC (HL-LHC)**. This ambitious upgrade, slated to bring beams back online in 2030, aims to dramatically increase the collider's **luminosity**, a measure of the collision rate, by up to a factor of ten compared to its original design.

The goal of this immense undertaking is to collect unprecedented amounts of data, allowing physicists to delve deeper into the mysteries of the universe. With the HL-LHC, researchers anticipate compiling data on approximately **380 million Higgs bosons** over its operational lifetime, a substantial increase from the 55 million gathered to date. This wealth of information will enable more precise measurements of the Higgs boson's properties and facilitate searches for new physics phenomena that could challenge the Standard Model. The upgrade also promises new avenues for exploring elusive concepts such as **dark matter** and the cosmic asymmetry between matter and antimatter.

The transformation is no small feat, requiring the removal and replacement of 1.2 kilometers of accelerator components, including new superconducting magnets designed to further concentrate particle beams. Markus Zerlauth, the HL-LHC project chief, emphasized the significance of this period, stating, "From Monday, we will be entering a new phase." Stéphane Willocq, ATLAS Spokesperson, added that preparing for the HL-LHC is "among the most ambitious scientific undertakings our collaboration has ever engaged in." While the beams are off, thousands of scientists will continue to analyze the vast datasets already accumulated, laying the groundwork for the discoveries that the HL-LHC promises.

## Rubin Observatory Begins Its Cosmic "Movie"

In a momentous event for astronomy and astrophysics, the **NSF-DOE Vera C. Rubin Observatory** officially commenced its **Legacy Survey of Space and Time (LSST)** on June 30, 2026. Located atop Cerro Pachón in Chile, the observatory is embarking on a revolutionary ten-year mission to create the most comprehensive, cinematic record of the universe in history.

At the heart of this endeavor is Rubin's extraordinary 3200-megapixel camera, the largest digital camera ever built, which is now capturing a new, detailed image approximately every 40 seconds. This unprecedented speed and sensitivity will allow the observatory to detect faint objects and fleeting events with remarkable consistency. The LSST is expected to unlock a treasure trove of discoveries, from pulsating stars and supernova explosions to the fossil record of galaxies. It will also provide crucial clues to the enduring mysteries of **dark energy** and **dark matter**.

Beyond the distant cosmos, Rubin is also poised to become the most powerful solar system discovery machine ever created. In just its first month and a half of early optimization surveys, the observatory has already identified over **11,000 never-before-seen asteroids**, including 33 near-Earth objects and 380 trans-Neptunian objects. U.S. National Science Foundation (NSF) director Brian Stone hailed the occasion, declaring, "Today, we begin filming the greatest cosmic movie ever made." Phil Marshall, Deputy Director of Rubin Operations for SLAC, highlighted the two decades of "hard science, engineering, and more" that led to this point. The resulting dataset, containing billions of objects, will be made available to scientists and the public worldwide, ushering in a new age of cosmic exploration.

## Graphene Reveals Surprising Superconducting States

A groundbreaking study published in *Nature* on June 29, 2026, by researchers at MIT, has revealed unexpected properties in **rhombohedral graphene**, a specific microscopic structure found within natural graphite. The team discovered that this atomically thin material can host **multiple superconducting states**, challenging conventional understanding of superconductivity.

Superconductivity, where electrons pair up and flow with zero resistance, typically occurs below a material's critical temperature and is often suppressed by magnetic fields. However, the MIT researchers observed that in rhombohedral graphene, at a certain electron density and in the presence of a perpendicular magnetic field, superconductivity not only persisted but actually **strengthened**. The transition temperature, for instance, increased from 55 millikelvin to approximately 90 millikelvin, and the material could withstand significantly more current before superconductivity was destroyed.

This enhancement of superconductivity by a magnetic field is highly unusual and opens new avenues for exploring unconventional superconducting mechanisms. While the precise microscopic behavior enabling these multiple and robust superconducting states remains a mystery, the findings point towards a new family of unconventional superconductors within a seemingly simple material. This discovery in condensed matter physics could have profound implications for future energy-efficient technologies and our fundamental understanding of quantum materials.
