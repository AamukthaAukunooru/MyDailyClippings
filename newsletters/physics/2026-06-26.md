## Quantum Computing's Accelerated Genesis

The race for practical quantum computing has taken a monumental leap forward this week with major announcements from the U.S. Department of Energy (DOE) and new advancements in architecture. On June 22, the DOE unveiled its ambitious **Quantum Genesis initiative**, a national effort to develop and deploy the world's first **fault-tolerant, scientifically relevant quantum computing capability** by 2028. This initiative, a foundational element of the broader Genesis Mission, aims to usher in a new era of computational power to accelerate scientific discovery and innovation, as outlined in President Donald Trump's Executive Order, "Ushering the Next Frontier of Quantum Innovation".

According to Michael Kratsios, Assistant to the President and Director of the White House Office of Science and Technology Policy, this effort will strengthen U.S. competitiveness and drive breakthroughs unachievable by classical systems. DOE Under Secretary for Science Darío Gil highlighted the profound impact, stating that advanced quantum computers will allow us to "peer into the fundamental laws of nature with unparalleled precision". The initiative encompasses three priorities, including establishing a National Quantum Supercomputing User Facility for scientists and engineers, identifying key scientific applications in chemistry, materials science, and high-energy physics, and holding a competition to demonstrate fault-tolerant quantum systems by 2028.

Further bolstering the quantum landscape, a Yale-led project, ERASE, secured a new $4 million National Science Foundation (NSF) grant on June 24 for its work on error-correcting quantum computers. The ERASE project, or "Erasure Qubits and Dynamic Circuits for Quantum Advantage," is developing an innovative approach to tackle error correction using "erasure flag" quantum bits that identify errors, making them easier to fix. This grant will propel the project into its second phase, focusing on hardware and software blueprints and expanding the quantum tech workforce in Connecticut, in collaboration with industry partner D-Wave Quantum. Meanwhile, QuEra and Los Alamos National Laboratory introduced a **Transversal STAR Architecture** for scalable quantum simulation, published in *PRX Quantum* on June 25, which aims to reduce the physical qubit overhead for early fault-tolerant quantum simulation, bringing execution speeds closer to the "megaquop" regime, or one million reliable logical operations. These combined efforts signal a concerted push toward making robust, large-scale quantum computing a reality within the next few years.

## Twistronics Honored with Kavli Prize in Nanoscience

In a significant recognition for the field of condensed matter physics, the 2026 **Kavli Prize in Nanoscience** was awarded on June 25 to Eva Y. Andrei, Pablo Jarillo-Herrero, and Allan H. MacDonald for their groundbreaking work establishing **twistronics**. This marks the first time research rooted in two-dimensional materials has received one of the world's most prestigious scientific honors, awarded by the Norwegian Academy of Science and Letters.

Twistronics is a rapidly expanding area of research that exploits the unique properties that emerge when atomically thin materials, such as **graphene**, are stacked and rotated relative to one another. Theoretical predictions by Allan H. MacDonald, combined with experimental breakthroughs by Eva Y. Andrei and Pablo Jarillo-Herrero, revealed that a small "magic-angle" twist between graphene layers could give rise to entirely new quantum phenomena, including correlated electronic states and even **superconductivity**. These discoveries have transformed our understanding of how material properties can be engineered at the atomic scale, opening new frontiers in condensed matter physics. The Graphene Flagship, a European initiative, lauded the award as powerful recognition of the transformative impact of atomically thin materials on science and technology, emphasizing the importance of sustained investment in fundamental research.

## Binary Black Hole Collision Reveals Event Horizon's Edge

In a truly astounding development for astrophysics, scientists have reported the first direct observation of the **event horizon** at the precise moment of a binary black hole collision. On June 25, *Phys.org* reported that two Australian scientists and their colleagues, utilizing the "loudest gravitational wave ever heard," witnessed this elusive boundary right before all light and sound are swallowed by the newly formed black hole.

The **event horizon** is the boundary around a black hole beyond which nothing, not even light, can escape. Directly observing this region during such a cataclysmic event has long been a theoretical aspiration, offering unprecedented insights into the extreme physics governing black holes and the fabric of spacetime itself. This observation, made possible by an exceptionally powerful gravitational wave signal, provides a unique opportunity to test the predictions of Einstein's theory of General Relativity in the most extreme gravitational environments imaginable. While further details on the specific observatory and methodology are eagerly awaited, this announcement represents a monumental step forward in our quest to understand these enigmatic cosmic giants.
