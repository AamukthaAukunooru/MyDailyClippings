## Unraveling the Neutrino's Dance

The universe, as we know it, is built upon a handful of fundamental particles, and among the most enigmatic are neutrinos. These ghostly particles, which interact only weakly with matter, are notoriously difficult to study. Yet, understanding their properties is crucial for a complete picture of particle physics and cosmology. In a significant stride, scientists co-led by the University of California, Irvine, have reported the most precise measurements ever made of two key neutrino oscillation parameters at the Jiangmen Underground Neutrino Observatory (JUNO) in China.

The JUNO detector, a monumental effort over a decade in design and construction, has now delivered its first results after just sixty days of data collection. According to Pedro Ochoa-Ricoux, a UC Irvine particle physicist and lead author of the study featured on the cover of *Nature*, the achieved precision surpasses two decades of previous measurements. The parameters in question relate to **neutrino oscillation**, a quantum mechanical phenomenon where neutrinos change between their three "flavors" (electron, muon, and tau) as they travel. This oscillation is direct evidence that neutrinos possess mass, a concept not included in the original Standard Model of particle physics.

While the exact **neutrino mass ordering**—which neutrino is the heaviest and which is the lightest—remains an open question, the JUNO results are a critical step. Sindhujha Kumaran, a postdoctoral researcher in Ochoa-Ricoux's group, highlighted JUNO's unique capability to detect tiny differences in the oscillation pattern imprinted on the energy spectrum of neutrinos from eight nuclear reactors, which will be key to solving the mass ordering puzzle. This remarkable precision marks a new era for neutrino physics, promising profound implications for our understanding of the universe's fundamental constituents.

## A Supernova's Echo Near the Galactic Core

Turning our gaze to the cosmos, astronomers using NASA's **Chandra X-ray Observatory** have potentially uncovered a new **supernova remnant** nestled in the bustling heart of our Milky Way galaxy. This discovery, detailed in a paper published in *The Astrophysical Journal*, is particularly exciting because, if confirmed, it would be one of the closest supernova remnants ever found to Sagittarius A*, the supermassive black hole at our galaxy's center.

Supernova remnants are the expanding debris fields left behind after a massive star explodes. These cosmic explosions are vital for the universe, as they forge and disperse heavy elements, such as iron, oxygen, and silicon, which are essential building blocks for planets and life itself. The region around the Galactic Center is an exotic environment, densely packed with massive stars, intricate magnetic fields, and thick clouds of gas orbiting rapidly. Identifying a supernova remnant in such a dynamic neighborhood provides invaluable data for understanding the violent processes that sculpt galactic evolution and the cycle of matter in star-forming regions. The X-ray data from Chandra, renowned for its high-resolution imaging of energetic cosmic phenomena, offers a unique window into these extreme conditions, allowing astronomers to piece together the history of stellar deaths and their profound impact on our galactic home.

## Illuminating the Pion's Inner Workings

Finally, a dive into the subatomic realm reveals new insights into the fundamental forces holding matter together. Researchers at the U.S. Department of Energy's (DOE) **Argonne National Laboratory** have utilized a powerful supercomputer to produce unprecedentedly detailed 3D images of the **pion**, a subatomic particle that plays a crucial role in mediating the **strong nuclear force**. This force is responsible for binding protons and neutrons within atomic nuclei, essentially holding all visible matter in the universe together.

Understanding the internal structure of the pion, which is composed of quarks and gluons, is fundamental to comprehending quantum chromodynamics (QCD), the theory of the strong force. By leveraging the immense parallel computing power of Argonne's supercomputers, the team generated high-resolution images revealing the transverse spatial distributions of quarks inside a moving pion. Yong Zhao, an Argonne physicist and principal investigator on the project, explained that these calculations allowed them to determine the pion's **quark generalized parton distribution (GPD)**.

The results, published in the *Journal of High Energy Physics*, showed that the transverse size of the pion decreases as its momentum increases, a pattern also observed in the proton. Intriguingly, the effective size of the pion was found to be smaller than that of the proton at moderate parallel momentum values. These detailed 3D images provide critical data, shedding new light on how quarks are arranged within the pion and offering a deeper understanding of the strong force at its most fundamental level.
