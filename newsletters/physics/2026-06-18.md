## Nanoscale Engineering Boosts Superconductivity

A significant leap in **condensed matter physics** comes from Sweden, where researchers at Chalmers University of Technology and RISE Research Institutes have unveiled a novel method to enhance **superconductivity**. Led by Professor Floriana Lombardi, a leading figure in Quantum Device Physics at Chalmers, the team demonstrated that subtly sculpting the surface beneath an ultrathin superconducting material can dramatically improve its performance. The innovation allows these materials to maintain their zero-resistance electrical flow at previously unattainable higher temperatures and under much stronger magnetic fields.

The breakthrough, detailed in *Nature Communications*, centers on a clever **nanoscale redesign** of the substrate. By treating the substrate in a vacuum at high temperatures, the researchers created an orderly pattern of microscopic ridges and valleys. This precise topographical modification subtly alters the electronic environment at the interface between the substrate and the superconducting layer, effectively guiding the arrangement of atoms in the superconducting film. This, in turn, stabilizes and strengthens the superconducting state. Eric Walhberg of RISE Research Institutes of Sweden, a key contributor to the study, emphasized how these minute changes at the nanoscale can have "decisive effects." The implications are vast, promising a future of more energy-efficient electronics, advanced quantum components, and robust technologies capable of operating in powerful magnetic fields, potentially even bringing us closer to the long-sought goal of **room-temperature superconductivity**.

## Quantum Sensors Target Dark Matter and Gravitational Waves

In a stride towards unraveling the universe's most elusive secrets, a team at Imperial College London, as part of the international **Atom Interferometer Observatory and Network (AION) collaboration**, has successfully demonstrated a prototype quantum sensor capable of overcoming a major hurdle in detecting **dark matter** and **gravitational waves**. Understanding the composition of the universe and identifying new sources of gravitational waves necessitates measuring incredibly faint signals, which are often obscured by experimental noise. The Imperial researchers have now shown, for the first time under realistic conditions, that a key principle behind next-generation quantum detectors can effectively cancel out this background interference.

The experimental setup involves comparing two **long-baseline atom interferometers**, instruments that use lasers to precisely measure the behavior of atoms. By employing two macroscopically separated clouds of ultracold strontium-87 atoms, interrogated by a single ultrastable clock laser, the system can meticulously compare their behavior and effectively eliminate common-mode phase noise. Dr. Richard Hobson, co-lead of the Ultracold Strontium Laboratory at Imperial, highlighted that this demonstrates how "some of the most precise instruments ever built" can be repurposed to open "entirely new windows onto the invisible parts of our Universe." Professor Oliver Buchmueller, Principal Investigator of the AION collaboration, echoed this sentiment, calling the work an "important milestone" towards future large-scale quantum sensors. Scaling this prototype to full-scale facilities, such as CERN or Fermilab, could enable searches for gravitational waves from the early universe and detect the elusive signatures of exotic forms of dark matter, pushing the boundaries of our cosmic understanding.

## The Universe's Accelerated Expansion Reaffirmed

Cosmologists can breathe a collective sigh of relief, as new research has emphatically re-confirmed that the universe's expansion is indeed **accelerating**, driven by the mysterious force known as **dark energy**. This finding, led by the University of Southampton and published in the *Monthly Notices of the Royal Astronomical Society*, directly refutes a 2025 study that had challenged this bedrock principle of modern cosmology.

The original discovery of accelerated expansion, made in the late 1990s using observations of **Type Ia supernovae**, earned Nobel laureates Professor Adam Riess and Professor Brian Schmidt, both of whom were involved in this latest study, the Nobel Prize in Physics in 2011. The 2025 paper had suggested that an "age effect" might bias supernova brightness measurements, making it appear as though the universe was accelerating when it was, in fact, slowing down. However, the new analysis meticulously re-evaluated the largest calibrated supernova samples, finding no evidence for this purported age effect. The Southampton-led team also corrected for crucial factors like the mass of host galaxies and potential errors in stellar age estimation, ultimately confirming the robustness of the original findings. As Professor Riess stated, "Extraordinary claims require especially careful testing," and this latest work ensures that our understanding of cosmic acceleration, and the pervasive influence of dark energy, remains firmly in place.
