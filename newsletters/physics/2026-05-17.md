## Interstellar Dust Confirmed in Antarctic Ice

In a fascinating convergence of astrophysics and geology, an international research team, led by the Helmholtz-Zentrum Dresden-Rossendorf (HZDR), has recently confirmed the continuous accumulation of **iron-60**, a rare radioactive isotope, within Antarctic ice. This discovery provides compelling evidence of our Solar System's ongoing passage through the **Local Interstellar Cloud**, a tenuous region of gas and dust between stars. The findings, published in *Physical Review Letters*, detail how this iron-60 is produced in massive stellar explosions, or supernovae, and subsequently ejected into space.

Dr. Dominik Koll from HZDR's Institute of Ion Beam Physics and Materials Research explained that their initial idea was that the Local Interstellar Cloud could store iron-60 over long periods, allowing Earth to collect this material as it moves through the cloud. While previous analyses of deep-sea sediments up to 30,000 years old also revealed iron-60, the new Antarctic ice samples, dating back 40,000 to 80,000 years, solidify the Local Interstellar Cloud as the probable source. The iron-60 signal varies over tens of thousands of years, a remarkably swift change on cosmic timescales, allowing researchers to rule out other explanations for its influx. This precise dating and analysis mean that for the first time, we have a direct terrestrial "fingerprint" of our galactic neighborhood, offering a unique opportunity to investigate the origins of these interstellar clouds.

## Handheld Superconducting Magnets Promise Revolution

Imagine a superconducting magnet, once the size of a small building, now fitting neatly into the palm of your hand. Scientists at **ETH Zurich** have achieved just this, marking an extraordinary breakthrough with immense potential for fields like **nuclear fusion** and **nuclear magnetic resonance (NMR)**. This "Tony Stark-like" feat involved creating two prototype magnets, neither exceeding 2.5 inches in diameter, capable of generating incredibly strong magnetic fields between **38 and 42 tesla**.

To put this into perspective, the world-record-holding hybrid resistive magnet at the National High Magnetic Field Laboratory in Florida generates 45 tesla but required a $15 million investment, 35 tons of material, and consumes 33 megawatts of power, along with thousands of gallons of water and liters of liquid helium per minute to operate. The ETH Zurich team achieved their compact design by winding flat **REBCO tape** (rare earth barium copper oxide) into disk-shaped coils, or "pancakes," and stacking them. This innovative design concentrates the magnetic field into a small volume while minimizing the length of tape required and crucially, avoids loss of conductivity by eliminating joints, breaks, or insulation between coils. This means less power and cooling are needed. The ability to perform high-field NMR with a tabletop device, as demonstrated by their 38 tesla magnet, could make such advanced analytical techniques widely accessible, accelerating discovery in chemistry, materials science, and medicine.

## DESI Maps 47 Million Galaxies, Hints at Evolving Dark Energy

The cosmos continues to reveal its secrets through ambitious surveys, and the **Dark Energy Spectroscopic Instrument (DESI)** has just delivered a monumental dataset. Since 2021, DESI has been meticulously observing a section of the sky, and its initial results have exceeded expectations, producing a map containing more than **47 million galaxies and quasars**. This stunning map spans 11 billion years of cosmic history, providing an unprecedented look at the large-scale structure of the universe, revealing how galaxies have clustered into vast filaments surrounding immense cosmic voids.

DESI's primary mission is to shed light on **dark energy**, the mysterious force driving the accelerated expansion of the universe for the past 5 billion years. While dark energy is typically assumed to be constant across time and space, several theories based on new physics suggest it may evolve. If this is true, DESI's detailed map across cosmic time would show different patterns than those predicted by a constant dark energy model. Already, some clues within the data hint at this possibility, though further confirmation is needed in the coming years as DESI continues its observations until 2028. This extensive mapping effort allows astronomers to probe the interplay between dark energy, which pushes galaxies apart, and **dark matter**, whose gravitational pull draws them together into the structures we observe. The insights gained from DESI are crucial for refining our cosmological models and understanding the ultimate fate of our universe.
