## Peering into the Strong Force with LHC "Near-Misses"

The Large Hadron Collider (LHC) at CERN is renowned for smashing particles together at nearly the speed of light to probe the fundamental constituents of matter. However, a recent study led by an MIT team has demonstrated a groundbreaking new way to utilize this colossal instrument: by observing "near-misses" rather than direct collisions. Published this week in *Physical Review Letters*, this innovative approach has transformed the LHC into a novel kind of microscope, revealing new behaviors within the **strong force**, which binds quarks and gluons together to form protons and neutrons.

When particles pass incredibly close to each other without directly colliding, their surrounding electromagnetic fields, flattened by their near-light speed, produce extremely high-energy photons. The MIT-led team meticulously sifted through LHC data to identify instances of these "photonuclear interactions," where a photon from one particle lightly "pings" off another. In a significant first, they were able to measure a type of subatomic particle called a **D0 meson** being kicked out during these interactions. What makes this particularly intriguing is that D0 mesons contain a **charm quark**, a rare type of quark not typically found in ordinary nuclear matter. The ability to observe these particles, and the new behavior of the strong force they reveal, offers an unprecedented window into the dynamics of matter at its most fundamental level, without the chaotic aftermath of a full-on collision. This opens up entirely new avenues for particle physicists to explore the universe's most basic forces.

## Quantum Leaps: Laser-Crafted Josephson Junctions Emerge

In a stunning display of quantum engineering, German scientists have achieved a significant breakthrough, successfully recreating a **Josephson junction** using laser technology to manipulate ultracold atoms. This development, highlighted this week, was previously considered an impossible feat without highly precise engineering and costly superconducting materials. A Josephson junction, for the uninitiated, is a crucial quantum device allowing electrical current to tunnel between two superconductors separated by a thin insulating layer. They are foundational components in everything from highly sensitive magnetometers, known as SQUIDs, to the burgeoning field of quantum computing.

The German team employed a technique called **atomtronics**, which involves carefully manipulating the behavior of ultracold atoms to mimic electronic components. By trapping a cloud of atoms in a controlled environment and precisely guiding their movement with lasers, they managed to create a system that exhibited the same quantum effects as a traditional Josephson junction. This innovative "simple laser setup" bypasses many of the manufacturing challenges associated with conventional Josephson junctions. This "unbelievable quantum breakthrough" could usher in a new era for quantum technology, potentially leading to more stable, scalable, and efficient quantum devices, including improved sensors and more powerful quantum computers. It's a testament to the ingenuity of physicists pushing the boundaries of what we thought possible in the quantum realm.

## Water's Hidden Critical Point Unveiled

Even the most ubiquitous substance on Earth, water, continues to hold profound mysteries for physicists. This week, new experiments have finally brought to light a long-predicted, but elusive, feature of supercooled water: a second **critical point**. Reported in the journal *Science* on March 26, this discovery occurs at approximately 210 kelvins (around -63° Celsius) and at a staggering 1,000 times the pressure of Earth's atmosphere at sea level.

Water is already known to have a critical point at high temperatures, where its liquid and gas phases become indistinguishable. However, scientists have long suspected a second critical point exists at lower temperatures, particularly in **supercooled water**, which can remain liquid below its normal freezing point. This hidden feature may provide the key to understanding some of water's most peculiar properties. For instance, unlike most liquids, water reaches its maximum density at about 4° Celsius, then becomes less dense as it cools further, which is why ice floats. Its heat capacity also exhibits unusual behavior. The new experimental confirmation of this second critical point, where two distinct liquid phases of water, one high-density and one low-density, merge into one, offers a profound explanation for these anomalies. This finding deepens our understanding of this essential molecule and its complex phase behavior, with implications across chemistry, biology, and Earth sciences.
