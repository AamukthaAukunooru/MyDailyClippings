## Proving the "Impossible": Atoms in Two Places at Once

Einstein famously scoffed at **quantum entanglement**, calling it "spooky action at a distance." Now, a groundbreaking experiment from the Australian National University (ANU) has pushed the boundaries of this strangeness, demonstrating that massive atoms can indeed exist in two locations simultaneously and exhibit entanglement through their physical motion. This isn't just a philosophical curiosity, it's a significant step toward reconciling quantum mechanics with **General Relativity**, the two pillars of modern physics that notoriously refuse to play nice.

Led by Dr. Sean Hodgman from the ANU Research School of Physics, the team successfully entangled **helium atoms** based on their momentum. They then allowed these atoms to fall through a **Rarity-Tapster interferometer**, a device designed to measure their momentum as they impacted a detector plate. The resulting patterns unequivocally showed that the atoms were entangled, with their final landing spots remaining linked even as they plummeted. Crucially, the moment one atom's momentum was measured, its entangled partner's possible paths instantly collapsed, regardless of its distance.

This experiment, published on April 1, 2026, not only confirms a century-old prediction that matter can occupy multiple positions at once but also provides a novel avenue for exploring the elusive problem of **quantum gravity**. By using massive atoms, which undeniably experience gravity, researchers are now closer to understanding how these two seemingly incompatible descriptions of the universe might eventually be unified.

## Sounding Out Gravity: The Dawn of Phonon Lasers

The laser, a ubiquitous tool in science and technology, has historically been synonymous with light. However, researchers at the University of Rochester and Rochester Institute of Technology are ushering in a new era with the development of a breakthrough **"phonon laser"** that manipulates sound, or more precisely, tiny vibrations at the quantum level. This innovation, announced on March 31, 2026, promises to dramatically enhance our ability to measure motion and forces, potentially even gravity, with unprecedented precision.

Traditional lasers control **photons**, particles of light. In recent decades, however, physicists have expanded this concept to other particles, including **phonons**, which are discrete units of vibration or sound. The challenge has always been to reduce the inherent noise in these systems. The Rochester team, including Nick Vamivakas, the Marie C. Wilson and Joseph C. Wilson Professor of Optical Physics at the University of Rochester, successfully developed a new type of **squeezed phonon laser** capable of precisely controlling these nanoscale vibrations.

By significantly reducing the thermal noise within the phonon laser, the researchers have achieved a level of control that allows for more accurate acceleration measurements than those possible with traditional photon lasers or radio frequency waves. This breakthrough, published in *Nature Communications*, could revolutionize fields from fundamental physics, offering new insights into the nature of gravity and particle motion, to practical applications like navigation. Imagine **quantum compasses** that are far more accurate and "unjammable" than current GPS systems, operating without reliance on satellites. This phonon laser could be a crucial step towards making such concepts a reality.
