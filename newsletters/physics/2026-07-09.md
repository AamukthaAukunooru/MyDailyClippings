## Unveiling the Universe's Secrets with Quantum Sensors

A significant stride in fundamental physics was announced this week by researchers at Imperial College London, who have developed a prototype **quantum sensor** capable of cutting through overwhelming interference to reveal faint, hidden signals. Published in *Nature* on July 7, this breakthrough marks a crucial step toward building next-generation detectors for some of the universe's most profound mysteries: **dark matter** and ancient **gravitational waves**.

The team, part of the UK-wide **Atom Interferometer Observatory and Network (AION)** collaboration, demonstrated a clever noise-canceling technique using two clouds of **ultracold atoms**. In their tabletop experiment at the Imperial Ultracold Strontium Laboratory, they utilized two widely separated clouds of **strontium-87 atoms**, measured with a single ultrastable clock laser. This setup was meticulously designed to mimic the challenging conditions expected in future large-scale detectors, where experimental noise is a significant hurdle. By comparing the signals from these two **long-baseline atom interferometers**, they effectively eliminated interference, allowing meaningful signals to be recovered even when individual measurements were completely swamped.

Dr. Richard Hobson, co-lead of the Ultracold Strontium Laboratory at Imperial, highlighted that this real-world demonstration validates a crucial concept for future quantum detectors, moving beyond idealized laboratory assumptions. The advance could pave the way for instruments sensitive enough to detect gravitational waves from the very early universe, as well as potential signatures of exotic forms of dark matter. The work also points towards ambitious future projects like the **Atom Interferometry CERN Experiment (AICE)**, which would scale these techniques to much greater distances, potentially becoming one of the largest quantum experiments ever constructed. This innovative approach promises to unlock new avenues for exploring the fundamental composition and dynamics of our cosmos.

## Euclid's Glimpse into Cosmic Dawn

In a stunning astronomical revelation, the European Space Agency’s (ESA) **Euclid space telescope**, with contributions from NASA, has more than doubled the number of known **quasars** from the universe’s infancy. A new study, published in *Astronomy & Astrophysics* on July 6, details the discovery of 31 ancient quasars, including the two most distant ever observed. These cosmic titans shone when the universe was just 670 million years old, approximately 5% of its current age, their light traveling some 13 billion light-years to reach Earth.

Quasars are the extraordinarily luminous cores of galaxies, powered by **supermassive black holes** actively accreting vast amounts of gas and dust. The extreme gravitational and frictional forces heat this material to millions of degrees, releasing energy that can outshine entire galaxies. Identifying such ancient quasars is incredibly challenging due to their immense distance, which renders their light faint and difficult to distinguish.

Euclid's wide survey, which will eventually cover over a third of the sky, proved to be a "game-changer" in this pursuit, according to researchers. The mission's ability to efficiently search vast sky areas for fainter light has allowed astronomers to capture a more representative sample of the ancient quasar population, moving beyond just the brightest outliers. The most distant object, designated **EUCL J172902.75+641018.1**, has a redshift of 7.77, while the second, **EUCL J125308.55+705432.3**, has a redshift of 7.69, surpassing the previous record holder from 2021.

These findings offer unprecedented insights into the **epoch of reionization**, a pivotal phase when the first stars and galaxies ionized the neutral hydrogen fog that filled the early universe. By studying these early supermassive black holes and their host galaxies, astronomers hope to unravel the mysteries of how such colossal structures formed so rapidly after the Big Bang and how they influenced cosmic evolution. The Euclid mission, launched in July 2023, is poised to continue mapping billions of galaxies, providing invaluable data for understanding **dark energy** and the universe's large-scale structure.

## Engineering a New Quantum Reality

In a captivating development from the realm of condensed matter physics, scientists at the University of Innsbruck, in collaboration with theoretical partners including Alvise Bastianello of the CNRS and Université Paris-Dauphine, have successfully engineered a never-before-seen quantum state, unveiling a new phase of matter. This groundbreaking work, published in *Physical Review Letters* on July 8, describes the deliberate creation of a **"fractional Fermi sea,"** pushing the boundaries of what was previously understood about quantum systems.

The research team, led by Hanns-Christoph Nägerl's group in the Department of Experimental Physics, focused on **ultracold Cesium atoms** confined to a single dimension. Through a process they termed **quantum engineering**, they repeatedly cycled the atoms between strong repulsive and strong attractive interactions. Rather than simply heating the system, this precise manipulation drove the atoms from their ground state into a highly excited, yet surprisingly organized, non-equilibrium state.

This novel critical phase of matter defies the long-established **Tomonaga-Luttinger liquid theory**, which has been the standard framework for describing one-dimensional quantum systems. The fractional Fermi sea exhibits a "hidden order" that becomes apparent in its correlations, characterized by pronounced **Friedel oscillations** and distinctive decay patterns. Yi Zeng, the study's lead author, explained that this interaction cycle reorganizes the atoms into a new many-body state, offering a controlled method to explore quantum matter beyond conventional equilibrium paradigms. This discovery not only expands our fundamental understanding of quantum mechanics but also demonstrates the profound potential of actively engineering quantum systems to create states that do not naturally occur under ordinary conditions. Such capabilities could lead to advancements in quantum computing, materials science, and our ability to harness quantum phenomena for novel technologies.
