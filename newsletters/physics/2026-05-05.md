## Unlocking New Quantum Realms with "Quadsqueezing"

In a significant stride for quantum technology, physicists at the University of Oxford have achieved the first-ever demonstration of **quadsqueezing**, an elusive fourth-order quantum effect. Published on May 1st in *Nature Physics*, this breakthrough represents a powerful new method to control quantum systems, making previously hidden quantum behaviors both visible and usable.

The team, building on a 2021 theory by Dr. Raghavendra Srinivas and Robert Tyler Sutherland, utilized a single trapped ion and precisely tuned laser fields to generate and control increasingly complex forms of "squeezing". In quantum physics, many physical systems behave like tiny oscillating objects, known as quantum harmonic oscillators. Controlling these oscillations is fundamental for modern quantum technologies, ranging from ultra-precise measurement tools to next-generation quantum computers. While squeezing is a common technique to manipulate these oscillators, the Oxford researchers combined two precisely controlled, non-commuting forces acting on the trapped ion. This ingenious approach allowed the forces to amplify each other, leading to the observation of quadsqueezing. This work not only introduces a novel way to engineer these interactions but also opens new frontiers for advancements in quantum simulation, sensing, and computing, promising more stable and resilient quantum technologies.

## A Subtle Blur in the Fabric of Time

Our fundamental understanding of time itself may require a subtle re-evaluation, according to new research supported by the Foundational Questions Institute (FQXi) and published on May 3rd in *Physical Review Research*. Physicists are grappling with one of quantum mechanics' most profound puzzles, how the fuzzy possibilities of the quantum world coalesce into the definite reality we experience. This latest work suggests that spontaneous **"collapse" processes**, possibly linked to gravity, could subtly blur time itself.

While this effect would be imperceptible to the clocks we use daily, it reveals a hidden limit to how precisely time can ever be measured. This tantalizing finding offers a new avenue for uniting quantum physics with gravity, a long-sought goal in theoretical physics. Nicola Bortolotti, a PhD student at the Enrico Fermi Museum and Research Centre (CREF) in Rome, Italy, emphasized the significance of taking seriously the idea that collapse models may be intrinsically linked to gravity. The research explores alternative explanations to the standard interpretation of quantum measurement, where a quantum system's wavefunction collapses into a single outcome upon observation. By examining these quantum collapse models, the international group of physicists has uncovered surprising consequences for the behavior of time, hinting at tiny fluctuations and offering a possible experimental pathway to test these models against conventional quantum theory.

## When "Darkness" Outruns Light, Without Breaking the Rules

In a captivating experimental confirmation, physicists have shown that dark "holes" within light waves can appear to move faster than light itself, without, crucially, violating Einstein's theory of relativity. This intriguing result, reported on May 4th, validates a long-standing theoretical prediction and was achieved using a special ultrafast electron microscopy setup.

The phenomenon involves what is known as a **phase singularity**, a point within a wave where the wave cancels out and the field goes dark. Researchers directly measured these optical singularities as they formed, moved, and annihilated inside a membrane of hexagonal boron nitride. The key finding was that just before annihilation, some of these dark singularities appeared to accelerate past the speed of light, reaching approximately 1.04 times the speed of light. This doesn't contradict Einstein's cosmic speed limit because darkness, in this context, is neither a particle nor a wave that carries mass, energy, or information. The experimental team essentially created a "movie" of the light wave's geometry, tracking these dark zeroes and measuring their apparent velocity. This experimental proof, while not unexpected by theorists, provides a concrete demonstration of a fascinating aspect of wave mechanics and our nuanced understanding of light.
