The world of physics has been buzzing over the past few days, offering a tantalizing glimpse into both the microscopic quantum realm and the grand cosmic expanse. We're seeing remarkable strides in quantum technologies, from manipulating magnetic waves for future computing to seemingly bending the arrow of time itself. Meanwhile, our telescopes continue to redraw maps of the universe, revealing a larger Milky Way and enigmatic phenomena in the early cosmos. Even our largest particle accelerator, the Large Hadron Collider, is entering a transformative phase, preparing for an unprecedented era of discovery.

## Quantum Leaps Towards a New Computing Paradigm

The quest for practical quantum computing continues at a rapid pace, with several recent developments pushing the boundaries of what's possible. Researchers at the University of Vienna, led by Andrii Chumak, have made a significant breakthrough by dramatically extending the lifetime of **magnons**, tiny magnetic waves, by nearly 100 times, reaching up to 18 microseconds. This makes them promising carriers of quantum information, potentially enabling the creation of ultra-compact quantum computers, possibly as small as a one-cent coin. The team's findings, published in *Science Advances*, revealed that the primary limitation to magnon lifespan isn't a fundamental law of physics, but rather the purity of the material, suggesting future improvements could come from enhanced manufacturing. These long-lived magnons could serve as reliable quantum memory devices and low-loss communication channels, connecting hundreds of **qubits** through a "quantum bus".

Adding to the quantum excitement, scientists at Los Alamos National Laboratory have developed novel **quantum control techniques** that can make a system appear to run backward in time. Published in *Physical Review X*, this research introduces protocols that reshape a system's "arrow of time" by precisely managing quantum measurements. This breakthrough could have profound implications for developing more powerful quantum computers and even quantum batteries, allowing for energy harvesting directly from the measurement process itself. Separately, McGill University researchers have engineered a new quantum device capable of generating precisely controlled bursts of **phonons**, or sound-like particles. This surprising behavior, observed by forcing electrons through an ultra-thin crystal at extremely low temperatures, pushes beyond current theoretical limits and could pave the way for **phonon lasers**, leading to faster communications, improved medical technologies, and advanced sensing systems. These advancements underscore a critical shift in the quantum landscape, with companies like Microsoft accelerating their transition to **post-quantum cryptography** by 2029, acknowledging the increasing threat from future quantum computers to current encryption standards.

## Reshaping Our Understanding of the Cosmos

Our cosmic backyard, the Milky Way, appears to be larger than previously imagined. New data from NASA's **Chandra X-ray Observatory** and ESA's **XMM-Newton Observatory** indicate that at least two of the galaxy's outer spiral arms, specifically the Outer Scutum-Centaurus Arm and the Outer Arm, lie up to 10% farther away than earlier measurements suggested. This re-evaluation, based on examining light reflections off dust clouds in the spiral arms, means our home galaxy spans a greater distance than previous models accounted for. The peer-reviewed results were published in *Astronomy & Astrophysics* on July 1, 2026.

Beyond our galaxy, the **James Webb Space Telescope (JWST)** continues to deliver breathtaking and sometimes perplexing insights. Most recently, NASA released a stunning new image of "infant stars," or **protostars**, in the **FS Tau region**, located approximately 450 light-years away. These young stars are captured in the act of breaking away from their molecular cloud nurseries, a fitting cosmic fireworks display released to celebrate the 250th anniversary of the United States. However, the JWST also continues to present deep cosmic mysteries. Its observations of the early universe have uncovered hundreds of perplexing "little red dots" and seemingly impossibly large black holes for their age, challenging existing astrophysical models and prompting a wave of new theoretical explanations, such as the concept of "black hole stars". In other exoplanetary news, Texas Tech University physicist Michael Fausnaugh was part of an international team that utilized NASA's **Transiting Exoplanet Survey Satellite (TESS)** in an innovative way, combining its observations with gravitational microlensing to confirm a distant "super Jupiter" exoplanet, **Gaia23bra b**, nearly 40,000 light-years from Earth. This demonstrates a powerful new method for detecting planets that more closely resemble those in our own solar system.

## The LHC Powers Down for a High-Luminosity Future

In the realm of particle physics, a monumental undertaking has just begun: the **Large Hadron Collider (LHC)** at CERN has entered its **Long Shutdown 3 (LS3)**. This extensive program of maintenance, consolidation, and upgrades will prepare the world's most powerful particle accelerator for its next phase, the **High-Luminosity LHC (HiLumi LHC)**. On June 27, 2026, the final protons of the LHC's third data-taking period circulated, marking the end of an incredibly successful run.

While no particle beams will circulate during this period, CERN's scientific activity remains intense, with thousands of researchers continuing to analyze vast datasets accumulated during the LHC era. The LS3 is the most extensive intervention on CERN's accelerator complex since the LHC's initial construction, involving the dismantling and replacement of 1.2 km of accelerator components. The HiLumi LHC, scheduled to begin operation in 2030, aims to increase the collider's **luminosity** by a factor of up to ten beyond its original design, leading to between 140 and 200 proton-proton collisions in every bunch crossing, compared to around 60 previously. This dramatic increase in collision intensity will provide unprecedented statistical power, enabling precision studies of the **Higgs boson** and enhancing the potential to uncover phenomena beyond the **Standard Model** of particle physics. The ATLAS and CMS experiments, two of the main detectors at the LHC, will undergo extensive upgrades, effectively becoming renewed detectors to cope with this extreme environment. The accelerator complex is expected to gradually restart from 2028, inaugurating a new era for high-energy physics.
