## Unmasking a Dormant Giant in the Early Universe

Astronomers have achieved a remarkable feat, making the first direct mass measurement of a **dormant black hole** in the early universe, a colossal entity 6 billion times the mass of our Sun. This silent behemoth, located at the center of the galaxy MRG-M0138, existed when the universe was only about 3 billion years old. Unlike their actively feeding counterparts, known as quasars, dormant black holes do not illuminate their surroundings, making them incredibly difficult to detect, especially at such vast distances.

A team led by Dr. Andrew Newman of Carnegie Science in Pasadena, California, leveraged the unparalleled capabilities of the **James Webb Space Telescope (JWST)**, combined with the cosmic magnifying effect of **gravitational lensing**. A massive galaxy cluster situated between MRG-M0138 and Earth acted as a natural telescope, bending and magnifying the distant galaxy's light by approximately 30 times. This enabled researchers to observe the subtle motions of stars near the galaxy's core, whose orbits are influenced by the black hole's immense gravity.

The findings, published in *Science*, offer crucial insights into the co-evolution of black holes and galaxies. Previously, direct mass measurements of dormant black holes this massive were limited to the nearby universe. The discovery suggests that the densest galaxies were sites of rapid black hole growth in the cosmos's infancy, implying that MRG-M0138, though now quiescent, was likely a powerful quasar in its past. This energy release from a growing black hole could have played a role in halting star formation within its host galaxy. Future observatories like the Euclid satellite, Nancy Grace Roman Space Telescope, and the Giant Magellan Telescope are expected to build upon these methods, revealing even more examples of gravitational lensing and allowing for more detailed studies of stellar motions in distant galaxies.

## Juno Illuminates the Origins of Cosmic Rays

NASA's **Juno mission** at Jupiter has provided groundbreaking evidence regarding the formation of **cosmic rays**, high-energy particles that continually bombard Earth and have puzzled scientists for over a century. By observing particles traveling close to the speed of light near Jupiter, Juno has offered new insights into how and where these energetic particles originate.

Cosmic rays can come from various sources, including supernovas and solar eruptions. When solar cosmic rays, also known as **solar energetic particles**, reach Earth, they can cause space weather effects that disrupt satellites and communication systems. Previous NASA missions, such as MMS and THEMIS, demonstrated how electrons become highly energized in Earth's foreshock region, where solar particles first encounter our planet's magnetic field. Scientists hypothesized that a similar process accelerates high-energy particles in foreshocks around other planets and astrophysical systems, a theory now confirmed by Juno's new observations.

While orbiting Jupiter, Juno measured high-speed electrons in the gas giant's foreshock region. These electrons achieved even higher speeds than those observed at Earth, a phenomenon that scales with Jupiter's significantly larger **bow shock**, formed by its magnetic field interacting with the solar particle stream. The results, published in the journal *Nature*, are particularly exciting because this scaling relationship aligns with cosmic rays observed emanating from supernovas across the galaxy. This strongly suggests that the same fundamental process of particle acceleration occurring within our solar system is a universal mechanism at play throughout the cosmos, providing a significant step forward in understanding these enigmatic high-energy particles.

## Quantum Effect Paves the Way for Battery-Free Devices

In a development that could revolutionize electronics, an international research team has discovered a new way to control a powerful **quantum effect** in an advanced material, potentially leading to electronic devices that operate without traditional batteries. Scientists, led by Professor Dongchen Qi from the Queensland University of Technology (QUT) and Professor Xiao Renshaw Wang from Nanyang Technological University in Singapore, investigated the physics behind the **nonlinear Hall effect (NLHE)**.

The NLHE is a sophisticated quantum phenomenon in condensed matter physics where a voltage is generated perpendicular to an applied alternating current, even in the absence of a magnetic field. Crucially, this effect allows for the direct conversion of alternating electrical signals from the environment into the direct current required to power electronic devices, bypassing the need for conventional components like diodes. Imagine sensors or chips drawing power simply from ambient wireless transmissions or other energy sources around them.

The team's experiments revealed that microscopic imperfections and atomic vibrations within the material can be used to precisely control this quantum effect. Furthermore, they found that the NLHE remains stable even at **room temperature**, a critical step towards practical, real-world applications outside of a lab setting. An intriguing aspect of their discovery is that temperature changes can even flip the direction of the generated electrical signal, offering scientists a novel mechanism to tune device performance. This breakthrough, published by the Queensland University of Technology, opens up significant avenues for future energy-harvesting technologies and could fundamentally alter how we power our electronic world.
