## Quantum's Practical Ascent

The prospect of a **quantum internet** took a significant leap forward this week, moving from carefully controlled lab environments into the bustling reality of urban infrastructure. Scientists at **Northwestern University**, led by Professor **Prem Kumar** and graduate student **Gina Talcott**, achieved a remarkable feat, demonstrating that **quantum entanglement**, the spooky connection between particles, can be reliably maintained over standard, busy **fiber-optic cables**. This isn't just a proof-of-concept; it's entanglement coexisting with everyday internet traffic, a crucial step toward building a robust quantum network. Their study, published on July 20 in *Optica Quantum*, successfully sent entangled photons through a 24.4-kilometer fiber-optic cable connecting Evanston and downtown Chicago, preserving entanglement with over 94 percent fidelity, even amidst high-capacity classical data streams. This marks the first demonstration of entanglement distribution between remote nodes over a fiber simultaneously carrying modern commercial telecommunications traffic, suggesting a practical path toward integrating quantum communication into existing telecom infrastructure.

Further along the path of practical quantum advancements, the integration of artificial intelligence with quantum computing is showing immense promise. Researchers have been actively testing **quantum neural networks**, exploring their capacity to reliably fulfill their theoretical potential. As highlighted by Peter Röseler in *APS Physics Magazine* on July 22, implementing these networks on actual **quantum-computing platforms** is essential for determining their viability. This practical hardware testing complements the theoretical progress being made in using neural networks to unlock larger quantum simulations, significantly reducing computational costs. For instance, a collaborative team, including researchers from **ByteDance Seed**, **JAIST**, **Peking University**, and the **Institute of Science Tokyo**, incorporated local pseudopotentials into Neural Network Quantum Monte Carlo calculations, enabling the reliable modeling of systems with up to 268 electrons, a size previously inaccessible to this methodology. These synergistic efforts are paving the way for more efficient and powerful quantum computations, potentially revolutionizing fields from material science to drug discovery.

## Unraveling Fundamental Mysteries

Beyond immediate applications, physicists are continually pushing the boundaries of fundamental understanding. A fascinating discovery reported by ScienceDaily on July 22 details a **strange quantum effect** that dramatically boosts energy transfer. Researchers, led by Professor **Kaifeng Wu's team at the Dalian Institute of Chemical Physics, Chinese Academy of Sciences**, uncovered a **proton shuttle-assisted triplet energy transfer (PS-TET)** mechanism. This process significantly improves how **triplet energy** moves between **ZnSe-based colloidal quantum dots (QDs)** and nearby phenol-pyridine molecular pairs. The mechanism involves a proton briefly shifting its position, helping to coordinate electron movement, and then returning to its original location. This temporary movement significantly enhances both the rate and efficiency of triplet energy transfer, a finding published in *Nature Materials*. This insight into a fundamental quantum process could have implications for areas like solar energy conversion and quantum sensing.

In the realm of quantum simulation, a significant "map" has been laid out for a long-sought **quantum simulator**. As outlined by Ryan Wilkinson in *APS Physics Magazine* on July 21, researchers have identified how **ultracold atoms** in an **optical lattice** could emulate a prominent model of **quantum matter**. This particular model is linked to profound phenomena, including the physics of **black holes** and the enigmatic behavior of **high-temperature superconductors**. Creating such a simulator would provide an invaluable tool for exploring complex quantum systems that are currently intractable with classical computers, offering a window into some of the universe's most perplexing secrets.

Finally, a bold new theoretical study ventures into the grandest questions of existence, proposing a **new quantum gravity theory** that links **entropy**, **dark energy**, and even **life** itself. Published by ScienceDaily on July 21, this framework, dubbed **Gravity from Entropy (GfE)**, offers a possible explanation for how the Universe can grow more complex without violating the **second law of thermodynamics**. Developed by mathematician Professor **Ginestra Bianconi** from **Queen Mary University of London**, and published in *Physical Review D*, this ambitious theory suggests that while the total entropy of the Universe increases as space expands, the entropy per unit volume decreases. That local decline could leave room for the formation of increasingly complex structures, such as galaxies, stars, planets, and ultimately life, amid an overall increase in cosmic entropy. It's a testament to the enduring human quest to find unifying principles that explain the myriad phenomena we observe, from the smallest quantum interactions to the largest cosmic structures.
