## Unveiling the Universe's Scaffolding: A Direct Look at the Cosmic Web

For decades, the **cosmic web**, the vast network of dark matter filaments thought to connect galaxies across the universe, has been a theoretical cornerstone of modern cosmology. Now, for the first time, scientists have captured a direct, detailed image of one of these monumental structures. An international team, spearheaded by researchers from the Max Planck Institute for Astrophysics, spent hundreds of hours collecting observations to reveal a glowing strand stretching an astounding **3 million light-years**. This filament links two actively forming galaxies from a period when the universe was only about 2 billion years old, offering a rare window into its early architecture.

The image provides the sharpest view yet of a cosmic filament, which astronomers believe acts as an intergalactic highway, channeling gas into galaxies and fueling star birth. This breakthrough, led by PhD student Davide Tornotti of the University of Milano-Bicocca, and published in *Nature Astronomy*, not only confirms predictions about the universe's hidden structure but also offers new ways to study the physical properties of gas within these intergalactic filaments. Understanding these structures is crucial, as they are believed to be shaped by **dark matter**, which constitutes roughly 85% of all matter in the universe. This direct observation moves us closer to understanding how galaxies grow and evolve over cosmic time.

## The State of Physics: Consensus and Conundrums

A groundbreaking, extensive survey of physicists worldwide has offered a fascinating snapshot of where the field stands on its most profound questions, revealing both areas of strong agreement and stark divisions. Released by the University of Waterloo and Perimeter Institute, this "largest-ever survey of physicists" highlights a distinct lack of consensus on many of the discipline's central puzzles, from the nature of **dark matter** and **dark energy** to the elusive unification of **quantum mechanics** with **Einstein's theory of gravity**.

Interestingly, while the **Standard Model of Cosmology** (ΛCDM, or Lambda Cold Dark Matter) did not achieve majority support, there were two points of significant consensus. A notable **68% of physicists** agreed that the **Big Bang** does not necessarily mark the beginning of time itself, but rather describes the universe's evolution from a hot, dense state. Furthermore, a narrow majority of **51%** believe that the early universe underwent a rapid period of expansion known as **cosmic inflation**. However, when it comes to the leading explanation for dark matter, a mere 17% favored undiscovered low-mass particles, and only 12% supported modifications to gravity, with 21% suggesting a combination of solutions. On the perennial challenge of **quantum gravity**, **string theory** emerged as the most likely solution for just 19% of physicists, facing stiff competition from **loop quantum gravity** (12%) and the belief that gravity cannot be quantized at all (18%). As Afshordi, who led the study in collaboration with Phil Harper and *Physics Magazine*, noted, this absence of consensus can be a crucial clue, marking areas where new data, sharper theories, or interdisciplinary connections are most needed.

## Power in Your Palm: Miniaturizing Superconducting Magnets

In a remarkable feat of engineering with far-reaching implications, scientists at ETH Zurich have successfully condensed the power of a superconducting magnet the size of a small building into a device that fits in the palm of your hand. This extraordinary breakthrough promises to revolutionize fields such as **nuclear fusion** and **nuclear magnetic resonance (NMR)**, potentially transforming massive, expensive installations into tabletop instruments.

The researchers from ETH Zurich's Department of Chemistry and Applied Biosciences developed two prototypes, neither exceeding **2.5 inches in diameter**, using flat **REBCO tape** (rare earth barium copper oxide) wound into disk-shaped coils. When powered with **1,000-amp currents**, these compact magnets generated magnetic fields between **38 and 42 tesla**. To put this into perspective, the world-record-holding hybrid resistive magnet at the National High Magnetic Field Laboratory in Florida, which produces a 45-tesla field, required a $15 million investment, 35 tons of materials, and consumes 33 megawatts of power along with thousands of gallons of water per minute for cooling. The ETH Zurich team's innovation lies in its joint-free design, which eliminates conductivity loss, thereby negating the need for additional power and cooling to compensate. This miniaturization could make high-field NMR, a crucial technique for examining sub-atomic particles, widely accessible and dramatically accelerate research in various scientific and medical domains.
