Researchers are pushing the boundaries of what's possible, whether it's building more stable qubits, uncovering hidden quantum properties in common metals, or observing cosmic particle accelerators in action.

## Quantum Leaps: Computing, Materials, and the Fabric of Reality

The race for practical quantum computing continues to accelerate, with Microsoft announcing a significant breakthrough in qubit stability. Their new **Majorana 2 quantum chip** boasts a remarkable 1,000-fold increase in qubit reliability, achieving lifetimes of at least 20 seconds, with some extending to a full minute. This dramatic improvement, building on their topological quantum computing approach, brings the company closer to its goal of a scalable quantum computer by 2029, a timeline slashed in half thanks in part to assistance from their agentic AI research platform, Microsoft Discovery. Not to be outdone, IBM has pledged an investment exceeding $10 billion over the next five years to advance its quantum roadmap, aiming for its **IBM Quantum Starling** in 2029, a fault-tolerant system capable of executing 20,000 times more operations than current systems.

Beyond traditional qubit architectures, Monash University scientists have developed a tiny, integrated chip that leverages a unique quantum property of light, the **"valley" degree of freedom**, for information encoding. This **valleytronics** breakthrough, led by Dr. Chi Li, could pave the way for ultra-fast, energy-efficient computing and future quantum technologies by generating, steering, and reading light-based information all within a single device. Meanwhile, physicists at the University of Oxford have expanded our quantum toolkit by demonstrating a new family of **Schrödinger's cat states**. These quantum superpositions are built from highly exotic quantum components, offering fresh avenues for quantum computing, sensing, and error correction.

The quantum realm is also revealing its secrets in more familiar materials. Scientists at Helmholtz-Zentrum Berlin, led by Dr. Jaime Sánchez-Barriga, have discovered unexpected quantum complexity within **cobalt**, a metal long considered well-understood. Their advanced measurements revealed a dense network of **topological electronic states** that remain robust at room temperature, enabling extremely fast electron behavior and offering new paths for next-generation computing and spin-based devices. In a potentially revolutionary development for electronics, researchers from the Queensland University of Technology and Nanyang Technological University have harnessed a powerful quantum effect called the **nonlinear Hall effect (NLHE)**. This phenomenon can convert alternating electrical signals directly into direct current without traditional components, holding promise for battery-free devices powered by ambient energy.

## Cosmic Accelerators and Galactic Winds Unveiled

Our understanding of the cosmos continues to deepen with remarkable observations from space missions. NASA's Juno spacecraft, orbiting Jupiter, has provided compelling new evidence for the origins of **cosmic rays**. Data analysis, published in *Nature* by a team including Dr. Savvas Raptis from the Johns Hopkins University Applied Physics Laboratory, shows Jupiter's **bow shock** accelerating electrons to relativistic energies of at least 1 MeV. This directly confirms a process suspected to be responsible for accelerating high-energy particles in astrophysical systems across the universe. These energetic particles, which can originate from supernovae and solar eruptions, have been a mystery for over a century, and Jupiter's foreshock now offers a crucial local laboratory for their study.

Closer to home, astronomers have finally found direct evidence of powerful winds emanating from **Sagittarius A***, the supermassive black hole at the heart of our own Milky Way galaxy, after a half-century search. Observations using the Atacama Large Millimeter/Submillimeter Array (ALMA), led by astrophysicists Lena Murchikova and Mark Gorski of Northwestern University, revealed a cone-shaped path carved by a hot, gentle wind. This discovery, reported in *Astrophysical Journal Letters*, sheds light on the often-quiet feeding habits of black holes and their impact on galactic environments.

Further expanding our view of galactic evolution, the James Webb Space Telescope (JWST) has made the first direct mass measurement of a **dormant black hole** lurking at the center of a galaxy in the early universe. Led by Carnegie Science's Andrew Newman, this behemoth black hole, 6 billion times the mass of our Sun, is no longer actively consuming matter. However, by detecting the motion of nearby stars affected by its gravity, researchers could determine its mass, offering new clues about how black holes and galaxies co-evolved billions of years ago. This finding, published in *Science*, suggests that the densest galaxies were sites of rapid black hole growth early in cosmic history.
