## Cosmic Rays: A Universal Fading Pattern Uncovered

For over a century, the origin of **cosmic rays**, high-energy particles that bombard Earth from space, has remained one of astrophysics' most enduring mysteries. Now, scientists working with the **Dark Matter Particle Explorer (DAMPE) space telescope** have uncovered a crucial new clue: a universal "spectral softening" that affects all types of cosmic ray nuclei, from protons to heavy iron, at the same energy threshold. This significant finding, reported on May 14, 2026, could finally help pinpoint their galactic accelerators.

The DAMPE collaboration, which includes major contributions from the astrophysics group at the Department of Nuclear and Particle Physics (DPNC) at the University of Geneva, observed that the number of cosmic ray particles begins to drop much faster after reaching a specific energy level. This effect, known as **spectral softening**, occurs uniformly for different nuclei beyond a rigidity of approximately **15 teraelectron-volts (TV)**. Rigidity, a measure of how strongly a particle's path resists being bent by magnetic fields, provides a common metric for these diverse particles. The revelation that this dramatic decline happens at the exact same rigidity for all cosmic ray species, regardless of their mass or charge, strongly suggests a universal mechanism at play across the galaxy. This could be a breakthrough in understanding the extreme astrophysical environments, such as supernova remnants or pulsar wind nebulae, that act as cosmic particle accelerators.

## Superconducting Magnets Shrink to Palm-Size

In a remarkable feat of engineering, researchers at **ETH Zurich** have developed **superconducting magnets** that generate fields comparable to massive, building-sized laboratory installations, yet fit in the palm of your hand. Announced on May 15, 2026, this innovation promises to revolutionize fields from nuclear fusion to medical diagnostics.

The team at ETH Zurich’s Department of Chemistry and Applied Biosciences successfully created two prototype magnets, neither exceeding 2.5 inches in diameter, capable of producing magnetic fields between **38 and 42 tesla**. To put this into perspective, the current world record for a hybrid resistive magnet, located at the National High Magnetic Field Laboratory in Florida, achieves 45 tesla but required a $15 million investment, 35 tons of materials, and consumes 33 megawatts of power, along with thousands of gallons of cooling water per minute. The ETH Zurich breakthrough utilizes **REBCO (rare earth barium copper oxide) tape**, wound into dense, unbroken disk-shaped coils. This design minimizes conductivity loss, eliminating the need for extensive additional power and cooling. The ability to generate such powerful magnetic fields in a compact device could significantly advance **nuclear magnetic resonance (NMR)** spectroscopy, making high-field NMR more widely accessible by transforming large installations into tabletop instruments. It also holds immense potential for the development of more efficient and compact **nuclear fusion reactors**, a long-sought clean energy solution.

## Altermagnetism Unveiled with Giant Magneto-Optical Effect

The burgeoning field of **spintronics**, which seeks to harness the intrinsic spin of electrons in addition to their charge, has received a significant boost with new research into **altermagnetic materials**. On May 15, 2026, researchers in China reported a novel method for probing the magnetic domains within altermagnets and, crucially, observed a **giant magneto-optical Kerr effect (MOKE)** in alpha-phase iron oxide.

Altermagnets, identified as a distinct class of magnets in 2022, defy conventional ferromagnetic and antiferromagnetic classifications. While they exhibit an antiparallel arrangement of neighboring atomic spins, similar to antiferromagnets, the atoms hosting these spins are related by specific symmetries that lead to properties typically associated with ferromagnets, despite having a near-zero net magnetization. The Chinese team’s work provides direct evidence supporting this classification. By using MOKE microscopy, they were able to visualize the altermagnetic domains in **alpha-phase iron oxide (α-Fe2O3)**. The observed giant MOKE signal, which is a change in the polarization of reflected light upon magnetization, is particularly exciting because it is predominantly driven by the material's **Néel vector** (the order parameter for antiferromagnetism), rather than any weak net magnetization. This discovery of a robust magneto-optical response in altermagnets opens new avenues for developing advanced memory and logic devices that could leverage these unique spin properties, propelling the next generation of spintronic technologies.
