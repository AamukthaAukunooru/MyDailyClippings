## Quantum Leaps Threaten Modern Encryption

The quantum computing landscape has been abuzz this week with two significant announcements that collectively bring the era of practical quantum advantage, particularly for cryptanalysis, closer than ever. For decades, **Shor's algorithm** has been a theoretical sword of Damocles hanging over digital security, but the sheer number of **qubits** required to wield it against real-world encryption seemed comfortably distant. That comfort is now rapidly eroding.

A team of quantum physicists at the **California Institute of Technology (Caltech)**, including the esteemed **John Preskill**, has unveiled a theoretical design for a quantum computer that could break common encryption schemes with significantly fewer qubits than previously estimated. Their work suggests that a **neutral-atom quantum computer** with as few as **10,000 atoms** could tackle the widely used RSA encryption in about a century, while **100,000 atoms** could shorten that to a mere three months. For elliptic curve cryptography (ECC), another backbone of online security, an array of **26,000 atoms** might suffice in just a few days. This represents a staggering reduction, with previous estimates for fault-tolerant quantum computers often cited in the millions of qubits. The Caltech team has even formed a new startup, **Oratomic**, in Pasadena to pursue the construction of such a machine, highlighting the perceived immediacy of this threat.

Simultaneously, researchers at **Google**, led by **Craig Gidney**, announced a groundbreaking implementation of Shor's algorithm that is ten times more efficient than prior methods for breaking 256-bit elliptic curve cryptography. Notably, Google chose to disclose this result via a **zero-knowledge proof**, a cryptographic technique that verifies the existence of their efficient circuit without revealing its specifics, an unprecedented move for a scientific discovery of this nature.

These twin advancements, detailed in recent publications and discussions, drastically alter the timeline for when quantum computers could pose a tangible threat to current cryptographic standards. Experts like Scott Aaronson, via his Shtetl-Optimized blog, note that when combined, these breakthroughs suggest that systems like **Bitcoin signatures** could be vulnerable to quantum attack earlier than anticipated, possibly with as few as **25,000 physical qubits**. The message is clear, the urgency to migrate to **post-quantum cryptographic schemes** is no longer a distant concern, but an immediate imperative for global cybersecurity.

## Reimagining the Big Bang with Quadratic Gravity

In the realm of theoretical cosmology, new research is proposing a profound shift in our understanding of the universe's very genesis, potentially resolving one of physics' most enduring paradoxes: the **Big Bang singularity**. For decades, Einstein's theory of general relativity has pointed to an initial point of infinite density and temperature, a singularity where the laws of physics break down.

However, a new theory, dubbed **quadratic gravity**, put forth by physicists at the **University of Waterloo** and the **Perimeter Institute** in Canada, offers a simpler and more elegant picture of the early universe. Published in *Physical Review Letters*, their work suggests that the rapid expansion of the universe in its earliest moments aligns naturally with this revised account of gravity.

Co-author and theoretical cosmologist **Jerome Quintin** describes quadratic gravity as "Einstein raised to the second power," indicating a modification to Einstein's standard theory. Instead of an abrupt, unmanageable singularity, this framework proposes that the early cosmos may have passed through a more controlled, high-energy phase.

Crucially, this new model is not just a theoretical construct, but offers testable predictions. Senior author **Niayesh Afshordi**, a physicist at the University of Waterloo and the Perimeter Institute, highlighted that the theory predicts a minimum level of **gravitational waves** generated during the inflationary period, which could be detected by next-generation observatories. This potential for empirical verification sets it apart from many other quantum gravity models. If confirmed, this framework could fundamentally reshape our understanding of cosmic origins, replacing a theoretical breakdown with a consistent, complete picture of how everything began.

## Unveiling the Solar System's Hidden Residents

Shifting our gaze from the cosmic dawn to our own cosmic backyard, the **NSF–DOE Vera C. Rubin Observatory** has delivered a breathtaking preview of its capabilities, announcing the discovery of over **11,000 new asteroids**. This massive haul, confirmed by the International Astronomical Union's Minor Planet Center (MPC), represents the largest single batch of asteroid discoveries in the past year, showcasing the observatory's transformative potential for solar system science.

The preliminary data, gathered during Rubin's early optimization surveys, includes hundreds of **trans-Neptunian objects (TNOs)**, icy bodies orbiting beyond Neptune, and critically, **33 previously unknown near-Earth asteroids (NEOs)**. Among the TNOs are two particularly intriguing objects, provisionally named **2025 LS2** and **2025 MX348**, which exhibit exceptionally large and elongated orbits, reaching distances up to **1,000 times farther from the Sun than Earth**. These place them among the 30 most distant minor planets ever known.

This early success from the Rubin Observatory, a joint program of NSF NOIRLab and DOE's SLAC National Accelerator Laboratory, demonstrates its unique combination of a large mirror, the world's most powerful astronomical digital camera, and sophisticated software pipelines. As **Mario Jurić**, a University of Washington professor of astronomy and leader of Rubin's solar system team, noted, "What used to take years or decades to discover, Rubin will unearth in months." These discoveries are just the "tip of the iceberg" for the observatory, promising to redefine our inventory of the solar system and open doors to unforeseen discoveries.
