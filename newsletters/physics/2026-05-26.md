## When Atoms Spin Backward: A Quantum Material's Strange Twist

In a truly perplexing development in condensed matter physics, an international team of researchers has directly observed **angular momentum** moving through a crystal lattice, only to find the direction of rotation unexpectedly reversing itself. This bizarre quantum effect, akin to a "1 + 1 = -1" scenario, was reported on May 24, 2026, by scientists from the Helmholtz-Zentrum Dresden-Rossendorf (HZDR), the Fritz Haber Institute of the Max Planck Society, and their collaborators, with findings published in *Nature Physics*.

Using ultra-powerful **terahertz laser pulses**, the researchers initiated tiny atomic rotations within a **quantum material**, specifically **bismuth selenide**. As this angular momentum transferred between distinct lattice vibrations, the scientists observed a complete flip in the direction of rotation. This phenomenon, which resembles an **Umklapp process** known in other areas of condensed matter physics, is attributed to the crystal's underlying symmetry. It’s the first experimental demonstration of such a reversal involving lattice angular momentum, a century-old theoretical prediction that had remained unverified.

This groundbreaking observation provides a fresh perspective on the fundamental origins of magnetism and could pave the way for enhanced control over advanced quantum materials. The ability to manipulate angular momentum at this fundamental level has profound implications for future technologies, potentially leading to new forms of data storage or more efficient quantum devices.

## Classical Computers Challenge "Quantum Supremacy" Claims

The narrative around **quantum computing** often highlights its potential to solve problems deemed impossible for even the most powerful **classical computers**. However, a recent breakthrough by physicists at the Center for Computational Quantum Physics (CCQ) at the Simons Foundation's Flatiron Institute, in collaboration with Boston University, has significantly complicated this picture. As reported on May 21, 2026, in *Science*, this team successfully tackled a daunting quantum physics problem using conventional computers, a problem previously claimed to be solvable only by quantum machines.

The problem involved simulating a quantum system comprising hundreds of interacting **qubits** arranged in complex three-dimensional lattices. A March 2025 paper had asserted that a quantum computer achieved a feat of "quantum supremacy" by solving this intricate problem, which classical methods supposedly could not match. Skeptical of such claims, Joseph Tindall, an associate research scientist at the CCQ and lead author of the new paper, and his colleagues employed cutting-edge mathematical tools based on **tensor networks**.

Remarkably, Tindall performed many of the initial calculations on a personal laptop using the ITensor software library, demonstrating an extraordinary level of efficiency. This achievement does not diminish the long-term promise of quantum computing, but rather underscores the continuous evolution of classical computational methods. It opens new avenues for research into quantum dynamics and the prediction of quantum material properties, suggesting that the line between what classical and quantum computers can achieve is far from fixed.

## Blazars Identified as Potential Source of Universe's Most Energetic Neutrino

In a significant stride for astrophysics, researchers may have identified the cosmic origin of the most energetic **cosmic neutrino** ever detected. This mysterious particle, carrying an astonishing 220 **peta-electronvolts** (PeV) of energy, slammed through the Mediterranean Sea on February 13, 2023, and was recorded by the **KM3NeT/ARCA** observatory. A new study, published on May 24, 2026, in the *Journal of Cosmology and Astroparticle Physics* (JCAP), points to **blazars** as the likely culprits.

Blazars are a type of **active galactic nucleus** powered by **supermassive black holes** that unleash colossal jets of plasma directly towards Earth. These extreme objects are among the universe's most powerful particle accelerators, capable of imparting immense energies to particles. The neutrino's energy was more than ten times greater than previously detected high-energy neutrinos, making its source a pressing mystery for three years.

The KM3NeT detector, still under construction at the time of the neutrino's detection, was operating with only 21 of its planned detection lines, roughly 10% of its final configuration. Despite its partial deployment, it captured a signal unlike anything seen before. As KM3NeT expands, researchers anticipate even more powerful statistical analyses, which could confirm this theory and profoundly reshape our understanding of blazar mechanics and the universe's most energetic phenomena.
