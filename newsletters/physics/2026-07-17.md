## Re-evaluating the Universe's Earliest Giants

For some time now, observations from the **James Webb Space Telescope (JWST)** have presented a puzzling picture of the early universe, detecting black holes that appeared unusually massive, seemingly growing far too quickly after the Big Bang. This week, new research offers a compelling explanation that could resolve this cosmic conundrum. A team led by Alessandro Trinca at the INAF Astronomical Observatory of Rome has proposed a model suggesting that these early black holes might not be as gargantuan as they first seemed. Instead, smaller black holes undergoing **super-Eddington accretion**, where they feed at rates exceeding the theoretical limit, could produce signals that lead astronomers to overestimate their true mass.

Published on June 19 in *Astronomy & Astrophysics*, this study posits that the intense radiation from such rapid feeding could mimic the signatures of much larger objects. The model also accounts for the mystery of why many of these distant black holes appear almost invisible in X-rays, a common characteristic of more typical, less rapidly accreting black holes. By combining super-Eddington accretion physics with detailed simulations of accretion disk spectra, Trinca's team has provided two possible interpretations for 14 X-ray-silent black holes previously studied using standard techniques. This re-evaluation highlights the complexity of interpreting signals from the nascent universe and offers a crucial refinement to our understanding of how these cosmic behemoths came to be.

## Quantum Materials: A New Link Between Light and Magnetism

In the realm of condensed matter physics, a significant review published yesterday, July 16, in *Nature Materials* by researchers at the City College of New York is highlighting exciting progress in **atomically thin quantum materials**. This work, emanating from physicist Vinod M. Menon's Laboratory for Nano and Micro Photonics (LaNMP), focuses on materials just a few atoms thick where light and magnetism are no longer independent but are intimately connected.

The breakthrough lies in the observation that **light-generated excitons**, which are quasi-particles formed when light excites an electron and leaves behind a "hole," can interact directly with the material's magnetic behavior. These excitons can also couple with **magnons**, which are quantized spin waves. This direct interaction opens up unprecedented opportunities to control magnetic states using light alone. Such precise manipulation of light and magnetism at extremely small scales has profound implications. Potential applications include advanced magneto-photonic memory, all-optical logic circuits, adjustable light-emitting devices, and even **quantum transducers** that convert signals between microwave and optical frequencies, a critical capability for future quantum networks. This research brings us a step closer to designing next-generation quantum technologies that leverage these unique light-matter interactions.

## Unmasking Superconductors' Dual Nature

Sometimes, the most profound discoveries come from re-examining what we thought we already understood. That's precisely what a new study from the Hebrew University of Jerusalem has achieved, revealing a hidden complexity in two widely studied ultrathin superconducting materials: **niobium diselenide (NbSe₂)** and **tantalum disulfide (TaS₂)**. For decades, physicists believed these materials behaved like simple superconductors with a single energy gap, a fundamental characteristic describing how electrons pair up to flow without resistance.

However, the research team, led by PhD student Shahar Simon and MSc student Maya Klang, under the guidance of Professors Oded Millo and Hadar Steinberg of the Racah Institute of Physics, used extremely sensitive **tunneling spectroscopy measurements** to probe these materials. Their findings, published in *Physical Review Letters* on June 29 and reported on July 15, demonstrate that these materials are far more sophisticated. They actually contain two distinct, strongly interacting superconducting states that conspire to appear as one. This revelation resolves a long-standing mystery and provides crucial new insight into the nature of superconductivity. Understanding this dual nature is vital for designing better superconducting materials, which are essential components for future technologies such as **quantum computers**, ultra-efficient electronics, and advanced sensors.
