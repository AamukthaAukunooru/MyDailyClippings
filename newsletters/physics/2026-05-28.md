## Cracks in the Standard Model? LHC Hints at New Physics

For decades, the **Standard Model of particle physics** has served as our most successful framework for describing the fundamental particles and forces that govern the universe. Yet, physicists know it is incomplete, unable to explain phenomena like gravity, dark matter, or the universe's matter-antimatter asymmetry. This week, new data from CERN's **Large Hadron Collider (LHC)** has provided some of the strongest hints yet that physics beyond this established model may be just around the corner. On May 26, scientists working with the **LHCb experiment** reported observing anomalous behavior in rare particle transformations known as “penguin decays” of **B mesons**. These decays involve a quark changing flavor, a process sensitive to the influence of undiscovered particles or forces. The observed rates of these decays did not fully align with the precise predictions of the Standard Model, suggesting that something unknown is subtly influencing these transformations. While these results are still being rigorously scrutinized, they echo previous anomalies seen in similar B meson decays, building a compelling case for the existence of new physics. Future experiments and increased data collection at the LHC will be critical in confirming these intriguing deviations and potentially revealing the nature of these elusive new particles, such as **leptoquarks**, which could unify different types of matter.

## Superconductivity Smashes Records at Ambient Pressure

In a monumental stride for materials science, researchers at the University of Houston have shattered a long-standing record in **superconductivity**, bringing the dream of lossless power transmission and revolutionary energy technologies closer to reality. On May 27, the University of Houston announced that its scientists, specifically from the **Texas Center for Superconductivity (TcSUH)** and the physics department, achieved a superconducting transition temperature (**Tc**) of **151 Kelvin** (approximately minus 122 degrees Celsius) under ambient pressure conditions. This remarkable achievement surpasses the previous record by 18 degrees Celsius and represents the highest Tc ever reported for a superconductor operating without the need for extreme pressures since the phenomenon was first discovered in 1911. The breakthrough, led by physicists **Ching-Wu Chu** and **Liangzi Deng**, was detailed in the *Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences*. The team utilized a novel **pressure quenching** technique, initially subjecting the material to high pressure to enhance its superconducting properties, then cooling it to a specific temperature before abruptly removing the pressure. This advancement could pave the way for vastly more efficient electrical grids, vastly improved energy storage systems, faster and more powerful electronics, and even novel technologies for fusion energy and medical imaging, by making superconducting technologies more practical and affordable for widespread use.

## Unraveling Antimatter's Secrets with Unprecedented Precision

The universe presents us with a profound cosmic mystery: the overwhelming dominance of matter over antimatter. According to our current theories, the Big Bang should have produced equal amounts of both, yet antimatter is exceedingly rare. To address this fundamental imbalance, physicists are meticulously comparing the properties of matter and antimatter. On May 27, the **ALPHA antihydrogen collaboration** at CERN, with leading contributions from **Swansea University** physicists including Professor **Niels Madsen**, announced a groundbreaking measurement: they have determined the **ground state hyperfine splitting** of antihydrogen 100 times more accurately than ever before. This measurement provides unprecedented insight into the internal structure of antihydrogen, the elusive antimatter counterpart of hydrogen. The ALPHA team achieved this by pioneering a novel technique involving laser-cooled Beryllium ions to sympathetically cool the antielectrons, or **positrons**, which are then merged with antiprotons to create antihydrogen. This method dramatically increased the number of trapped antihydrogen atoms, allowing for the observed 25,000 trapped atoms, a record for such an experiment, which was crucial for the enhanced precision. Comparing this hyperfine splitting to that of ordinary hydrogen could reveal subtle differences, shedding light on why antimatter seems to be largely absent from our universe.
