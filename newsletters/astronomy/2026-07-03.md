What an exhilarating time to be looking up! As we kick off July, the cosmos continues to present us with both grand challenges and unprecedented opportunities for discovery. From a critical mission to save a venerable space telescope to a stark warning about the future of our night sky, and the dawn of a new era in cosmic surveying, the dynamic interplay between human ingenuity and the vastness of the universe is on full display. These developments remind us of the immense value of our orbital assets, the fragility of our view of the cosmos, and the boundless potential of ambitious new projects to reshape our understanding of everything.

## A Daring Rescue Mission for NASA's Swift Observatory Faces Delay

NASA's Neil Gehrels Swift Observatory, a workhorse in gamma-ray astronomy for over two decades, is facing an unexpected peril: it's slowly but surely falling out of orbit. Launched in 2004, this $250 million space telescope has been instrumental in detecting thousands of gamma-ray bursts, the most powerful explosions in the universe, earning it the moniker "NASA's first responder" for its ability to swiftly pivot and alert other observatories to transient cosmic events. However, increased solar activity in recent years has "puffed up" Earth's upper atmosphere, creating more drag and accelerating Swift's descent from an orbit of about 600 kilometers to roughly 360 kilometers.

To avert its fiery demise and preserve its unique capabilities, NASA contracted Katalyst Space Technologies for a daring rescue mission. Their robotic servicing spacecraft, named LINK, is designed to rendezvous with Swift and boost it to a higher, more stable orbit. This mission is particularly challenging because Swift was not designed for on-orbit servicing, requiring innovative solutions to grapple and re-position the observatory. The launch of LINK aboard a Northrop Grumman Pegasus XL rocket from Kwajalein Atoll was postponed yesterday, Thursday, July 2, due to a launch vehicle issue after the L-1011 aircraft carrying the rocket took off. Teams are currently reviewing data to determine the next launch attempt. The stakes are high, as replacing Swift's capabilities could take years and hundreds of millions of dollars, underscoring the critical importance of this first-of-its-kind commercial servicing mission to extend the life of a vital scientific instrument.

## Satellite Mega-Constellations Pose "Existential Threat" to Astronomy

While we strive to keep our existing eyes on the sky operational, the future of astronomical observation from Earth is facing a growing threat from a different kind of orbital proliferation. New research released on July 1 by the European Southern Observatory (ESO) warns that the staggering 1.7 million satellites companies are planning to launch into Earth's orbit in the coming years will have "devastating consequences for astronomy". These massive constellations, primarily for broadband internet services like SpaceX's Starlink, which recently launched another 24 satellites, bringing its total to over 10,700, are making the night sky significantly brighter.

The ESO study, which is the first to quantify how much these bright satellites will impact astronomical observations, describes this as an "existential threat" to telescopes viewing the universe. The sheer number of these objects, reflecting sunlight back to Earth, will increase the sky's background brightness, effectively creating light pollution on a global scale. This directly hinders our ability to observe faint celestial objects, detect transient phenomena, and conduct long-exposure imaging, impacting everything from exoplanet research to cosmology. To safeguard humanity's ability to properly explore the night sky, the researchers advocate for a maximum limit of 100,000 satellites and insist that they be designed to be dim enough to be invisible to the naked eye. This calls for urgent international dialogue and regulation to balance technological advancement with the preservation of our cosmic window.

## Rubin Observatory Begins Unprecedented Cosmic Survey

Amidst these challenges, a new era of observational astronomy has officially begun with the NSF-DOE Vera C. Rubin Observatory commencing its monumental Legacy Survey of Space and Time (LSST). From its mountaintop perch in Chile, under some of the clearest skies on Earth, the Rubin Observatory will spend the next ten years creating the most comprehensive, cinematic record of the universe in history. This revolutionary survey will relentlessly observe the entire southern sky every few nights, generating an ultra-wide, ultra-high-definition time-lapse of the cosmos.

Each night, the observatory will collect approximately ten terabytes of data and produce as many as seven million alerts of changes in the night sky. This unprecedented data stream, which will include billions of objects and trillions of measurements, promises to revolutionize our understanding of everything from near-Earth asteroids to the most distant galaxies. Scientists will track the subtle movements of billions of stars, detect exploding supernovae, chart the paths of potentially hazardous asteroids, and map the distribution of dark matter and dark energy across vast cosmic scales. The LSST's open data policy, making this wealth of astronomical information accessible globally, opens the door for new kinds of discoveries by both professional scientists and the public, empowering a new generation of cosmic explorers to uncover the universe's dynamic secrets.
