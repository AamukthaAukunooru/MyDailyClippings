## Arrakihs: A Galactic Archaeologist in the Making

The European Space Agency (ESA) is set to embark on a profound journey into cosmic history with the formal adoption of its second 'fast-class' mission, Arrakihs. Approved on June 10-11, 2026, this mission is designed to be a "galactic archaeologist," peering into the faint, sprawling halos of stars and gas that envelop galaxies. Picture a galaxy not just as its bright, spiraling disk, but as a much larger, ethereal sphere of matter, mostly invisible dark matter, laced with diffuse stars and hot gas. These halos are like cosmic fossil records, holding the imprints of a galaxy's formation and evolution, including the remnants of smaller galaxies it may have absorbed over billions of years.

Arrakihs, whose name stands for "Analysis of Resolved Remnants of Accreted galaxies as a Key Instrument for Halo Surveys," is planned for launch by the end of 2030. It will investigate at least 80 galaxies with masses similar to our own Milky Way, a sample size large enough to provide robust statistics on typical galaxy formation and help scientists understand if our home galaxy is unique. By mapping these faint stellar streams within the halos, Arrakihs will help us piece together past mergers and gain insights into the role of dark matter, which acts as the gravitational glue for these colossal structures. This mission, rapidly developed as part of ESA's Cosmic Vision program, underscores Europe's long-term commitment to space science. In further good news for ongoing science, ESA also endorsed extensions for 13 of its currently operating science missions, ensuring their continued pursuit of groundbreaking discoveries.

## The Urgent Mission to Save NASA's Swift Observatory

In a testament to the value of our orbital assets and the ingenuity of space technology, NASA is fast-tracking a critical mission to save its Neil Gehrels Swift Observatory. This vital space telescope, launched in 2004, has been instrumental in detecting and studying explosive events across the Universe, such as gamma-ray bursts and supernova explosions. However, its orbit has been steadily decaying, dropping from an initial 600 kilometers (373 miles) to approximately 400 kilometers (249 miles), threatening to bring its mission to an untimely end later this year as it falls too close to Earth.

Enter the LINK robotic servicer, developed by Katalyst Space Technologies. This pioneering commercial spacecraft is poised for launch no earlier than June, marking a monumental first: the robotic capture and servicing of an uncrewed government satellite that was never designed for docking. The mission involves the LINK spacecraft rendezvousing with Swift and then boosting it into a higher, more stable orbit, extending its operational lifetime by several years. NASA will host a media teleconference on June 17 to preview this ambitious undertaking. This mission highlights the growing importance of in-orbit servicing capabilities, not just for extending the lives of valuable scientific instruments, but also for demonstrating new ways to manage and sustain our presence in space.

## Artemis III: Practicing for the Moon in Earth Orbit

The journey back to the Moon continues to evolve, with NASA announcing the crew for the Artemis III mission on June 9, 2026. However, the mission profile for Artemis III has been updated: instead of directly landing on the lunar surface, this crucial flight will now focus on an Earth-orbiting test. The four selected astronauts will conduct critical rendezvous and docking operations with the Human Landing Systems (HLS) being developed by commercial partners Blue Origin and SpaceX.

This strategic shift is designed to de-risk future lunar landing missions by thoroughly testing the complex interfaces and operational procedures between NASA's Orion spacecraft and the commercial landers in the relatively safer environment of Earth orbit. The mission will be an "incredibly exciting, complicated, and highly coordinated multi-launch campaign," involving multiple rockets and demonstrating how these diverse systems can operate together seamlessly. While some may yearn for an immediate lunar landing, this methodical approach is vital for ensuring the safety and success of returning humans to the Moon and establishing a sustainable presence there. It's a testament to the complexity of deep space exploration and the careful, incremental steps required to achieve such ambitious goals.
