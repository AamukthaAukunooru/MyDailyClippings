## Euclid Unveils the Universe's Earliest Quasars

The European Space Agency's Euclid space telescope has achieved a remarkable feat, discovering 31 ancient quasars from a period when the universe was just 670 to 800 million years old, effectively more than doubling the number of such distant objects previously known. Among these, one, dubbed EUCL J172902.75+641018.1, now holds the record as the most distant quasar ever reported, with a redshift of 7.77, placing it over 13 billion light-years away.

Quasars are incredibly luminous galactic cores, powered by supermassive black holes actively devouring surrounding matter. They can shine with the light of a trillion suns, making them visible across vast cosmic distances. Discovering so many of these early quasars is akin to finding a treasure trove of cosmic fossils. Before Euclid, astronomers had primarily detected only the brightest, rarest outliers of the ancient quasar population. Euclid's wide field of view and sensitive instruments allow it to capture much fainter light, revealing a more representative sample of these early, energetic objects.

This discovery is crucial because it helps us understand how supermassive black holes formed and grew so rapidly in the young universe, a puzzle that has long baffled astrophysicists. The presence of such massive black holes so early in cosmic history challenges existing models of galaxy and black hole evolution. By studying these quasars, scientists can gain insights into the "epoch of reionisation," a pivotal period when the universe's opaque, neutral hydrogen gas was ionized by the first stars and quasars, allowing light to travel freely. This is a monumental step in charting the universe's formative years and the intertwined growth of galaxies and their central black holes.

## The First Witnessed Birth of a Magnetar

In a groundbreaking observation, astronomers have for the first time directly witnessed the birth of a magnetar, an extraordinarily magnetic and rapidly spinning type of neutron star. This monumental event, detected through a distinctive "chirp" in the light of a distant supernova, confirms a theory proposed 16 years ago that these exotic objects can power some of the brightest stellar explosions ever seen, known as superluminous supernovae.

Superluminous supernovae are stellar explosions that shine 10 or more times brighter than typical supernovae, and their extreme luminosity has long been a mystery. The observation revealed that a newborn magnetar, a remnant of a massive star's collapse, injects immense energy into the expanding supernova debris, keeping the explosion intensely bright for an extended period. Magnetars, though only about 10 miles across, can spin over a thousand times per second, generating magnetic fields trillions of times stronger than the Sun's. This rotational energy and immense magnetism are what powers the prolonged brilliance.

What makes this discovery even more profound is that the "chirp" signal, a unique feature in the exploding star's light, could only be explained using Einstein's theory of general relativity. This is the first time general relativity has been directly applied to explain the mechanics of a supernova in this way. This observation not only validates a long-standing theoretical prediction but also offers a new tool for understanding the most energetic phenomena in the universe and the extreme physics governing these compact, powerful objects.

## Roman Space Telescope Gears Up for Launch

Looking ahead, NASA's Nancy Grace Roman Space Telescope is currently in Florida, undergoing final preparations for its highly anticipated launch in August. This next-generation observatory is poised to revolutionize our understanding of the cosmos, with a field of view at least 100 times larger than that of the venerable Hubble Space Telescope.

The Roman Space Telescope, named after NASA's first chief of astronomy, Nancy Grace Roman, is designed to tackle some of the biggest questions in astrophysics, including the mysteries of dark matter and dark energy, which together make up about 95% of the universe's mass-energy content. By surveying vast swathes of the sky, Roman will create expansive maps of the universe, searching for subtle distortions in the light from distant galaxies caused by dark matter, and precisely measuring the expansion history of the universe to probe the nature of dark energy.

Beyond its cosmology goals, Roman will also feature a technological demonstration, the Roman Coronagraph Instrument, which will enable it to directly image exoplanets, worlds orbiting other stars. This capability could provide unprecedented direct views of planetary systems far beyond our own, offering insights into their atmospheres and potential habitability. As Roman prepares for its epic journey, the scientific community eagerly anticipates the deluge of data it will provide, promising to reshape our cosmic perspective and potentially uncover entirely new phenomena.
