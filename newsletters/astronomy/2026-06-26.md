## Euclid Peers into the Milky Way's Dazzling Heart

Imagine capturing an image so vast and detailed that it reveals over 60 million individual stars in the crowded core of our own galaxy. That's precisely what the European Space Agency's Euclid telescope has accomplished, delivering the largest and most intricate visible light image ever taken of the Milky Way's center. Launched in 2023 with the primary mission of mapping the universe's dark forces, Euclid has proven itself to be a versatile instrument, its camera sensitive enough to differentiate individual stars even in the dense galactic bulge.

This glittering mosaic, taken after astronomers pointed Euclid at the Milky Way's center for 26 hours, is more than just a pretty picture. It marks the dawn of a new era in exoplanet discovery. Scientists anticipate that this data will significantly boost the number of known exoplanets, potentially adding more than 100,000 to the current count of around 6,000. The image will also be crucial for verifying observations from other telescopes, like the Roman Space Telescope, which will search for exoplanets by detecting the slight dimming of starlight as planets transit, or pass in front of, their host stars. Euclid's detailed view will help distinguish true transiting planets from other phenomena, such as binary star systems. This incredible capability, initially unforeseen for Euclid, highlights the serendipitous nature of scientific exploration and the boundless potential of our space-faring instruments.

## "Super-Puff" Planets: Lighter Than Candy Floss

Just when we thought we had a grasp on planetary diversity, the universe throws us another curveball. Researchers from the University of Oxford, in an international collaboration, have announced the discovery of two of the lowest-density giant planets ever detected: "super-puff" planets so extraordinarily light they are less dense than candy floss. These gas giants, named TOI-791 b and TOI-791 c, orbit an F7-type dwarf star located approximately 1,110 light-years from Earth in the southern constellation Volans.

The existence of these "super-puff" planets, roughly the size of Jupiter but with only a tiny fraction of its mass, presents a fascinating challenge to our models of planetary formation. One leading theory suggests these worlds possess enormous atmospheres rich in hydrogen and helium, which make up a significant portion of their total mass. The discovery was made possible by continuous, uninterrupted observations during the Antarctic winter, which allowed astronomers to capture the planets' exceptionally long transits—each lasting over 11 hours—in a single observation. These are the longest continuous planetary transits ever observed in their entirety from the ground. This remarkable finding underscores the incredible variety of worlds out there and pushes the boundaries of what we thought possible for planetary structures.

## A Backward Orbit: Clues to a Hidden Giant

Our journey through the latest discoveries continues with another exoplanet that's challenging our assumptions: TOI-1710 b, a Neptune-sized world that orbits its star in the opposite direction of the star's rotation. Published today in *The Astrophysical Journal Letters*, this rare retrograde orbit is providing astronomers with compelling evidence for the existence of an unseen giant planet shaping its entire system.

For decades, the prevailing theory has been that planets orbit in the same direction as their host star's rotation, a natural outcome of their formation within a swirling disk of gas and dust. While some massive "hot Jupiters" have been found with tilted or even backward orbits, such behavior in a smaller, "warm Neptune" like TOI-1710 b is far less common and harder to explain. The planet's orbital plane is tilted by nearly 180 degrees, effectively circling its star in reverse. This dramatic misalignment suggests powerful gravitational interactions long after the system's initial formation. While a distant companion star in the TOI-1710 system was initially considered, detailed analysis indicates it's too far to cause such a drastic orbital flip. This leaves astronomers postulating the presence of a hidden, massive planet within the system, whose gravitational tugs over eons could have "kicked" TOI-1710 b into its peculiar backward dance. This discovery is a powerful reminder that planetary systems are dynamic, evolving environments, often with gravitational dramas playing out over billions of years, leaving subtle, yet profound, fingerprints for us to decipher.
