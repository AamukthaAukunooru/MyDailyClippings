## Unveiling the Universe's Grand Design with Webb

The James Webb Space Telescope (JWST) continues to redefine our cosmic perspective, and its latest achievement is nothing short of extraordinary: the largest-ever 3D map of the universe's "cosmic web". Released on July 6, this unprecedented survey reconstructs the very "skeleton" of the cosmos, revealing how galaxies have evolved since the universe's infancy, some 13 billion years ago.

Imagine the universe not as a uniform void, but as a vast, interconnected network, much like a neural system on a cosmic scale. This is the cosmic web, a colossal scaffolding of gas filaments, dark matter, and voids that dictates the large-scale organization of all matter. It's within these filaments that countless galaxy clusters, and even clusters of clusters, reside. Webb's new map, built from a catalog of 164,000 galaxies, traces this structure in exquisite detail, extending back to an era when the universe was only a few hundred million years old, a time previously almost unreachable. This monumental undertaking allows astronomers to witness, with startling clarity, the formation and evolution of galaxies within this cosmic framework. Understanding the cosmic web is fundamental to grasping how our universe came to be structured the way it is, offering profound insights into the forces that have shaped everything we see.

## Hubble Peeks into the Milky Way's Ancient Heart

While Webb gazes into the distant past, our venerable Hubble Space Telescope offers a stunning look at a relic much closer to home, yet almost as old as the universe itself. NASA released on July 5 a spectacular red, white, and blue image of NGC 6426, one of the Milky Way's oldest star clusters. This globular cluster, a dense, spherical swarm of stars, is estimated to be around 13 billion years old, meaning it formed not long after the Big Bang, which occurred approximately 13.7 billion years ago.

Located in the outer halo of our galaxy, NGC 6426 is a living fossil of the early universe. Its stars exhibit "low metallicity," meaning they contain very small amounts of elements heavier than hydrogen and helium. This composition mirrors the primordial universe, where heavier elements, like carbon and oxygen essential for planets and life, were just beginning to be forged inside massive stars through nuclear fusion. Scientists have even found evidence of two distinct stellar populations within the cluster, suggesting that earlier generations of massive stars ended their lives in powerful supernova explosions, scattering newly created heavy elements and enriching the gas for subsequent star formation. This glimpse into NGC 6426 provides invaluable clues about the conditions and processes of star formation in the universe's infancy, laying the groundwork for the eventual emergence of planets and, ultimately, life itself.

## SpaceX's Latest Orbital Delivery, Including a Nuclear First

Closer to home, the dynamism of space exploration was on full display with SpaceX's Transporter-17 rideshare mission. Early on July 7, a Falcon 9 rocket successfully launched 81 payloads into orbit from California's Vandenberg Space Force Base, with its first stage booster achieving its 11th successful landing on a drone ship in the Pacific Ocean.

While such multi-satellite launches are becoming a regular sight, this mission marked a significant milestone: the deployment of the first commercially designed and operated spacecraft to harness nuclear energy in orbit. This development underscores the accelerating pace of innovation in commercial space. A rideshare mission like Transporter-17, carrying dozens of satellites for various clients, demonstrates the growing accessibility of space for a wide range of applications, from Earth observation to communications and scientific research. The introduction of nuclear power in a commercial satellite, while still in its early stages, could pave the way for longer-duration missions, more powerful scientific instruments, and greater autonomy for spacecraft, pushing the boundaries of what's possible in Earth orbit and beyond. It’s a testament to how rapidly technology is evolving, opening new frontiers even in the seemingly routine business of launching satellites.
