## A Cosmic Blockbuster Begins: The Legacy Survey of Space and Time

Imagine a movie of the entire southern sky, playing out over a decade, revealing the universe's most subtle changes and deepest secrets. That's precisely what has begun with the official launch of the Legacy Survey of Space and Time (LSST) at the Vera C. Rubin Observatory in Chile. After nearly two decades of development, this ambitious 10-year endeavor will create the most comprehensive and detailed record of the universe's history ever assembled.

At the heart of this project is the world's largest digital camera, paired with the largest survey telescope on the planet. This powerful combination is already capturing new images every 40 seconds, with some regions of the sky to be observed up to 800 times over the decade. The sheer scale of the data collected will be immense, forming the foundation for what scientists are calling the "greatest cosmic movie ever made." Thousands of scientists worldwide will utilize these images to map billions of galaxies, identify countless asteroids, analyze our solar system, and delve into the mysteries of dark matter, the elusive substance making up 80% of the universe. Even in a pre-launch survey, the observatory managed to find over 11,000 previously undiscovered asteroids, including 33 near-Earth objects, in just a month and a half. This survey promises to change our understanding of the cosmos by seeing more objects, further away, and in greater detail than ever before.

## The Reusability Race Heats Up: Japan and China Make Strides

The global push for more cost-effective and sustainable space travel saw significant developments this week, with both Japan and China announcing successful test flights of experimental reusable rocket technology. The ability to reuse rocket components, particularly the first stage, dramatically reduces the cost of launching payloads into space, a game-changer pioneered by companies like SpaceX.

Japan's experimental reusable rocket, the RV-X, conducted its first test flight on Saturday. The rocket successfully lifted off, hovered, moved horizontally for about 16 meters (52 feet), and then safely landed during its less than one-minute flight at the Japan Aerospace Exploration Agency's (JAXA) Noshiro Testing Center. Rising 11 meters (36 feet) in the air, the RV-X maintained its upright position throughout the maneuver. This successful test is a crucial step for Japan in developing a lower-cost successor to its current H3 series of single-use rockets, aiming to bolster its competitiveness in the global space market.

Just one day prior, China also made history by successfully recovering the first stage of its Long March-10B rocket after a liftoff. This marks China's first successful recovery of a rocket's first stage, which returned to a platform in the sea. These advancements from both Japan and China underscore a global trend towards making space access more affordable and frequent. As more nations and private entities master reusable rocket technology, we can anticipate an acceleration in scientific research, commercial ventures, and human exploration beyond Earth.

## Echoes from the Dawn of Time: Ancient Quasars Revealed

Astronomers have unveiled a wave of ancient quasars, including two of the most distant ever observed, offering an extraordinary window into the universe's infancy. These luminous galactic cores, powered by supermassive black holes, are like cosmic lighthouses, shining from a time when the universe was only about 670 million years old.

The European Space Agency's Euclid mission, working in conjunction with the W. M. Keck Observatory in Hawaii, confirmed 21 of 31 identified ancient quasars. Quasars represent a brief yet incredibly energetic phase in a galaxy's life, where vast amounts of material spiral into a central supermassive black hole, releasing immense energy that can make the galaxy's core one of the brightest persistent sources in the universe. Studying these ancient objects is crucial for understanding how the first supermassive black holes and galaxies formed and grew so rapidly, a significant mystery in astrophysics. The challenge lies in their elusiveness, as few galaxies had grown large enough at that early stage, and their light has traveled for over 13 billion years, arriving faint and stretched into infrared wavelengths. These discoveries provide invaluable "celestial archives" that allow us to piece together the conditions of the early cosmos and the evolutionary paths of galactic behemoths.
