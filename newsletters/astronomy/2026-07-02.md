## A Daring Rescue Mission for NASA's Swift Telescope

Today, July 2nd, marks a truly unprecedented moment in space history: a private spacecraft is set to launch on a daring mission to save NASA's Neil Gehrels Swift Observatory from an impending fiery demise in Earth's atmosphere. The mission, aptly named "Swift Boost," will see Katalyst Space's robotic servicing spacecraft, LINK, launched aboard a Northrop Grumman Pegasus XL rocket from Kwajalein Atoll in the Marshall Islands. This isn't just another satellite launch; it's a robotic lifeline, a first-of-its-kind attempt to grab and boost an uncrewed U.S. government satellite back into a safe, higher orbit.

The Swift telescope, launched in 2004, has been an incredibly successful workhorse, providing invaluable data on gamma-ray bursts, the most powerful explosions in the universe, and other high-energy cosmic events. However, increased solar activity starting in 2024 caused Earth's atmosphere to expand, creating more drag on low-orbiting objects like Swift. Scientists realized early last year that Swift was losing altitude faster than anticipated and would reenter and break up by mid-2026 if no action was taken. The LINK spacecraft will spend several weeks assessing Swift's condition and identifying optimal grapple points. Once secured, its gentle ion thrusters will slowly raise the pair's orbit over several months, ensuring Swift can continue its vital observations. This mission highlights not only the ingenuity of space engineering but also the growing importance of on-orbit servicing to extend the lives of valuable scientific assets.

## Unraveling a Giant Planet's Fiery Survival

In a discovery that provides a stunning glimpse into the distant future of planetary systems, including our own, astronomers have used the James Webb Space Telescope (JWST) to shed light on how a gas giant exoplanet, WD1856b, survived the violent death of its star. Discovered in 2020, this Jupiter-sized world orbits a dead star, a white dwarf, at an astonishingly close distance, completing a full revolution in just 1.4 days. This proximity initially baffled scientists, as a gas giant at such a range should have been engulfed and destroyed when its star swelled into a red giant phase, a process our own Sun will undergo in about five billion years.

New observations from the JWST, published in *Nature*, allowed an international team, including astrophysicists from Northwestern University and Cornell, to analyze the planet's atmosphere, mass, and temperature for the first time. They found WD1856b to be significantly hotter than expected, roughly 127 degrees Celsius (260 degrees Fahrenheit), which is about 240 degrees hotter than can be explained by the white dwarf's light alone. By combining these measurements with models of how giant planets cool, researchers reconstructed its journey. The findings suggest that WD1856b initially orbited its star from a much safer distance, only migrating inwards towards its dead companion billions of years *after* the star had already collapsed into a white dwarf. This "post-death migration" offers a compelling explanation for its survival and provides crucial data for understanding the long-term evolution of planetary systems and the ultimate fate of gas giants like Jupiter in our solar system.

## A New Window for Exoplanet Detection

Our quest to discover worlds beyond our solar system has just received a significant boost with a new method combining existing tools to find previously elusive exoplanets. A "super Jupiter" exoplanet, named Gaia23bra b, located nearly 40,000 light-years from Earth, has been confirmed using an innovative approach that leverages NASA's Transiting Exoplanet Survey Satellite (TESS) in conjunction with gravitational microlensing.

Gravitational microlensing is a phenomenon where the gravity of a foreground star acts like a cosmic magnifying glass, bending and brightening the light from a more distant background star. If the foreground star has a planet, that planet's gravity can cause an additional, brief flicker in the light, revealing its presence. While microlensing has been a known technique, accounting for less than 5% of all known exoplanets, combining it with TESS data is a novel development. The exoplanet Gaia23bra b, which orbits an orange dwarf star about 80% the mass of our Sun, was initially flagged by the European Space Agency's now-retired Gaia space telescope in 2023. Researchers then revisited archived TESS data, which continuously images vast swathes of the sky every few minutes, and found that it had also captured the microlensing event. This new synergy demonstrates the immense potential of repurposing existing observatory data with advanced analytical techniques to discover planets, particularly those that are further from their host stars and more akin to the gas giants in our own solar system, which are often difficult to detect with traditional transit methods. This opens a powerful new avenue for expanding our census of exoplanets and understanding the diversity of planetary architectures across the galaxy.
