## Touching the Edge of Forever: Black Hole Event Horizons Revealed

For the first time, scientists believe they have detected the elusive "fingerprints" of a black hole's event horizon – that infamous point of no return from which nothing, not even light, can escape. Published on June 24, 2026, this groundbreaking discovery was made by meticulously analyzing gravitational waves, the ripples in spacetime generated when two black holes violently collide and merge. Specifically, researchers focused on the final burst of waves, known as "direct waves," from the powerful GW250114 merger event detected by the LIGO observatory in January 2025.

The concept of an event horizon has long been a theoretical cornerstone of Einstein's general relativity, defining the boundary of a black hole's immense gravitational pull. Yet, directly observing this boundary has been an insurmountable challenge due to its very nature – it literally swallows all information. By isolating these direct waves, scientists were able to extract information from closer to an event horizon than ever before, revealing how black holes twist space, a phenomenon known as "frame dragging". The signal observed behaved like a rapidly fading oscillation, with its frequency linked to the rotation of the newly formed black hole and its fading rate tied to the object's intense gravity. While preliminary, these findings offer a tantalizing new avenue for studying these extreme phenomena and could help test the predictions of general relativity with unprecedented precision. This isn't just a win for theoretical physics; it's a thrilling step towards "touching" a region once confined to science fiction.

## Cosmic Cotton Candy: The Mystery of Super-Puff Exoplanets

Imagine a planet the size of Jupiter, yet so incredibly light it's less dense than cotton candy. This week, an international team led by the University of Oxford announced the discovery of two such "super-puff" exoplanets, TOI-791 b and TOI-791 c, orbiting a dwarf star approximately 1,110 light-years from Earth. Published on June 25, 2026, in *Monthly Notices of the Royal Astronomical Society*, this discovery challenges our understanding of how planets form and evolve.

These gas giants, detected by NASA's Transiting Exoplanet Survey Satellite (TESS), are indeed baffling. TOI-791 b is roughly Jupiter's size but only 3% of its mass, giving it a density of a mere 0.038 grams per cubic centimeter. Its sibling, TOI-791 c, is even larger than Jupiter but boasts just 5.9% of its mass, with a density of 0.047 grams per cubic centimeter. To put this into perspective, Jupiter's average density is about 1.33 grams per cubic centimeter, and even cotton candy typically has a density of around 0.05 grams per cubic centimeter.

Astronomers measured these planets' densities by combining observations of their size, determined by how much starlight they block during transit, with their mass, calculated from subtle gravitational tugs on each other over eight years of observation. The existence of such incredibly diffuse worlds suggests they possess enormous, hydrogen and helium-rich atmospheres that account for a significant fraction of their total mass. One leading theory posits that these gaseous envelopes accumulated rapidly in the cold outer regions of their protoplanetary disk, far from their host star. Understanding these "cosmic cotton candy" planets is crucial for refining our models of planet formation, especially for giant worlds that exist outside our own solar system.

## Euclid Unveils Our Galactic Heart in Unprecedented Detail

On June 24, 2026, the European Space Agency's (ESA) Euclid space telescope delivered a truly breathtaking gift: the largest and most detailed image ever taken of the visible light pouring from the center of the Milky Way. This ultra-wide view, capturing an astonishing 60 million stars, marks the beginning of a new age of discovery for understanding our home galaxy.

The sheer scale of this image is immense, offering an unparalleled perspective on the complex distribution of matter within the galactic core. The Milky Way's center is a bustling, crowded region, obscured by vast swathes of dust and gas in visible light. However, Euclid's advanced instruments, designed to observe in visible and near-infrared wavelengths, have pierced through much of this veil, revealing countless stars, star-forming regions, and intricate structures that were previously hidden or only dimly resolved.

This new image is not just a pretty picture; it's a treasure trove of data that will help astronomers map the distribution of matter, including the elusive dark matter, and better understand the processes that govern galactic evolution. By studying such a high-resolution view of our own galaxy's heart, scientists can gain crucial insights into the formation and dynamics of spiral galaxies across the universe. It's a powerful reminder that even in our familiar cosmic neighborhood, there are still profound secrets waiting to be illuminated.
