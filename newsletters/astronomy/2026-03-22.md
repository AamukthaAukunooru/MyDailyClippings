This week in astronomy brings extraordinary images from JWST, a historic crew announcement for lunar exploration, and the discovery of a potentially habitable world tantalizingly close to home.

## JWST Peers Into a Planetary Nursery

The James Webb Space Telescope has produced its most detailed images yet of a protoplanetary disk surrounding the young star HL Tauri, located 450 light-years away in the Taurus molecular cloud. The new mid-infrared observations reveal **concentric ring structures** at a resolution previously unachievable, showing gaps that correspond almost precisely to the orbital resonances predicted for forming planets.

The images confirm theoretical models of **gap-clearing by proto-planets** and reveal a surprising asymmetry in one of the outer rings, suggesting an as-yet unseen protoplanet with significant mass perturbing the disk. This is the closest observational confirmation of planet formation in action that astronomers have ever achieved.

## Artemis III Crew Unveiled

NASA has announced the four-person crew for Artemis III, the mission that will return humans to the lunar surface for the first time since Apollo 17 in 1972. The crew includes **Commander Reid Wiseman**, Pilot Victor Glover, Mission Specialist Christina Koch — who will become the first woman to set foot on the Moon — and Mission Specialist Jeremy Hansen of the Canadian Space Agency, marking Canada's first lunar surface mission.

The mission is currently targeting a **South Pole landing** near the Shackleton Crater, where permanently shadowed regions are believed to contain significant water ice deposits that could support future sustained presence on the Moon.

## An Exoplanet Next Door

Using the European Southern Observatory's ESPRESSO spectrograph, astronomers have confirmed the existence of a rocky exoplanet orbiting within the habitable zone of Epsilon Indi A, a sun-like star just 11.8 light-years from Earth. The planet, designated Epsilon Indi Ab, has a minimum mass of **1.4 Earth masses** and receives approximately 85% of the solar flux that Earth receives from our Sun.

At this proximity, it represents one of the most accessible potentially habitable worlds yet discovered. While direct atmospheric characterization lies beyond current capabilities, it is an ideal target for next-generation telescopes such as the Extremely Large Telescope currently under construction in Chile.
