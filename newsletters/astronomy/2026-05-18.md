## A Close Call with Asteroid 2026 JH2

Today, May 18, 2026, marks the closest approach of a recently discovered asteroid, 2026 JH2, to Earth. First spotted just days ago on May 10 by the Mount Lemmon Survey in Arizona, this celestial wanderer is estimated to be between 15 and 35 meters (approximately 50 to 115 feet) wide – comparable in size to the Chelyabinsk meteorite that famously streaked across Russian skies in 2013. While its size might sound intimidating, astronomers have assured us there is no risk of collision.

At its closest, 2026 JH2 will pass by Earth at a distance of about 90,000 kilometers (56,000 miles). To put that into perspective, this is roughly a quarter of the average distance between Earth and our Moon, and even closer than many of our geostationary communications satellites. Such close passes are invaluable for scientists, offering a rare opportunity to study a near-Earth object (NEO) at close range without the immense cost and time of launching a dedicated mission. Observations from this flyby can provide crucial data on the asteroid's composition, rotation, and orbital dynamics, helping us refine our understanding of these cosmic rocks and improve our planetary defense strategies. For those with a small telescope, 2026 JH2 is expected to reach a magnitude of +11.5, making it visible as it traverses the constellation Leo.

## Reshaping the Realm of Extreme Gravity

In a truly mind-bending development, new research published on May 16 suggests that the traditional boundary between black holes and neutron stars, long held sacrosanct under Einstein's general theory of relativity, may vanish under a new gravity theory. This groundbreaking study, conducted by a joint research team from the National Institute for Mathematical Sciences (NIMS) and the Korea Astronomy and Space Science Institute, applied the "Horava-Lifshitz (HL) gravity theory" to extreme celestial objects.

Under HL gravity conditions, ultra-dense objects like neutron stars could theoretically become significantly heavier than previously thought. More strikingly, the "compactness gap" – the theoretical range of densities separating neutron stars from black holes – might disappear as the density of these objects becomes extremely high. This has profound implications, potentially explaining "objects difficult to distinguish as either black holes or neutron stars" that have been observed by gravitational wave detectors like LIGO-Virgo-KAGRA. If confirmed by future observations, this theory could open entirely new avenues for understanding the universe's most enigmatic compact objects, the nature of gravity itself in extreme environments, and even shed light on the elusive dark matter. It's a tantalizing glimpse into a universe that might be even stranger and more interconnected than we currently imagine.

## Psyche's Martian Slingshot

Our robotic emissaries continue their epic journeys across the solar system, and this past week saw a crucial milestone for NASA's Psyche mission. On May 15, 2026, the Psyche spacecraft successfully performed a gravity assist maneuver at Mars, passing just 4,500 kilometers (2,800 miles) from the Red Planet's surface. This intricate celestial ballet is a common, yet always impressive, technique in space exploration.

Launched in 2023, Psyche is destined for a truly unique target: 16 Psyche, a rare metal-rich asteroid located in the main asteroid belt between Mars and Jupiter. Scientists believe this asteroid could be the exposed nickel-iron core of an early planetesimal, a building block of our solar system. By using Mars's immense gravitational pull as a "slingshot," the spacecraft gained a vital boost in speed and adjusted its trajectory, setting it on course for its arrival at 16 Psyche in 2029. This maneuver is not just about fuel efficiency; it's about precisely shaping a spacecraft's path through the vastness of space. The Psyche mission promises to offer unprecedented insights into planetary formation and the hidden interiors of rocky worlds, and this successful Mars flyby brings us one step closer to unlocking those secrets.
