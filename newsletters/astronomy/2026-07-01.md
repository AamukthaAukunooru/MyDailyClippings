## A Daring Rescue Mission for the Swift Telescope

Today marks a critical moment for space exploration as NASA launches a rescue mission to save the Neil Gehrels Swift Observatory, a venerable space telescope, from falling back to Earth. The mission, dubbed Swift Boost, is set to deploy a private spacecraft, LINK, built by Arizona-based Katalyst Space Technologies, to grapple the Swift observatory and tow it into a stable orbit. The launch, initially delayed by unfavorable weather, is scheduled for no earlier than 5:43 a.m. EDT (0943 GMT) today, July 1.

Swift, launched in 2004, has been instrumental in studying gamma-ray bursts, the most powerful explosions in the universe, and observing in X-ray and ultraviolet wavelengths. Its orbit has been gradually decaying, threatening its continued scientific operations. The LINK satellite will be launched aboard an air-launched Northrop Grumman Pegasus XL rocket, released from the L-1011 Stargazer jet at about 39,000 feet (12,000 meters). This is a historic flight for more than just Swift, as it will be the final launch of the Pegasus rocket, marking the end of an era for this unique air-launch system. Successfully boosting Swift will demonstrate innovative commercial capabilities for in-space servicing, extending the life of a valuable scientific asset and ensuring its continued contributions to our understanding of the high-energy universe.

## Rubin Observatory Begins "Greatest Cosmic Movie Ever"

A new era of astronomy officially began on June 30, as the Vera C. Rubin Observatory in Chile commenced its landmark 10-year Legacy Survey of Space and Time (LSST). This ambitious project aims to create the most comprehensive, cinematic record of the universe in history, observing the entire southern sky every few nights. U.S. National Science Foundation (NSF) director Brian Stone aptly stated, "Today, we begin filming the greatest cosmic movie ever made".

The Rubin Observatory's Simonyi Survey Telescope is equipped with the world's largest digital camera, a staggering 3,200-megapixel instrument, capable of capturing images that are a whopping 1.7 gigapixels in size. This unprecedented data stream is expected to revolutionize our view of the universe, providing crucial insights into some of astronomy's most profound mysteries. Over the next decade, the LSST will illuminate pulsating stars, supernova explosions, the fossil record of galaxies, and provide clues to the enigmatic nature of dark energy and dark matter. It will also compile a detailed census of our own solar system, including millions of asteroids and comets, having already discovered over 11,000 never-before-seen asteroids during early optimization surveys. The sheer volume and depth of this data will allow researchers worldwide to participate in cutting-edge science, opening new windows on the universe.

## A New Earth-like World Discovered Just 25 Light-Years Away

The ongoing quest for exoplanets, particularly those that might harbor life, received another exciting update this week. Astronomers at the University of California, Irvine, announced the discovery of a new Earth-like exoplanet, dubbed GJ 3378b, orbiting a star approximately 25 light-years from our solar system. While 25 light-years may sound like a vast distance, in the context of our Milky Way galaxy, which spans about 100,000 light-years, GJ 3378b is considered one of our "closest cosmic neighbors".

This newly found world is roughly twice the size of Earth and resides within its host star's habitable zone, often referred to as the "Goldilocks region". This means it receives just the right amount of stellar radiation for liquid water to potentially exist on its surface, a key ingredient for life as we know it. According to lead author Paul Robertson, the super-Earth receives about 90 percent of the radiation Earth gets from our Sun, placing it squarely in this "sweet spot". A lingering mystery, however, is the nature of the planet's atmosphere. GJ 3378b sits near what researchers call the "cosmic shoreline," a region where intense stellar radiation could strip away a planet's atmosphere, similar to what scientists believe happened to Mars in our own solar system. This discovery adds to the growing catalog of potentially habitable worlds and further fuels our fascination with the possibility of life beyond our home planet.
