## Science Returns to Earth Aboard SpaceX Dragon

Today marks a crucial moment for research conducted aboard the International Space Station (ISS) as SpaceX's Cargo Dragon spacecraft, supporting the 34th Commercial Resupply Services (CRS-34) mission for NASA, is scheduled to undock from the orbiting laboratory. After a month-long stay, the capsule, designated Cargo Dragon 2 capsule C209 on its sixth flight, will bring back thousands of pounds of invaluable scientific research samples to Earth. Splashdown is anticipated off the California coast tomorrow morning, June 17.

The Dragon, which originally launched on May 15 and docked with the ISS on May 17, delivered nearly 6,500 pounds of supplies and equipment to the Expedition 74 crew. Its return cargo, however, is where the true scientific harvest lies. Among the precious samples are bioprinted organ and cartilage tissue, materials developed for DNA-inspired cancer treatments, and crucial data from cryogenic fuel storage experiments. An ocular imaging device, used to monitor crew members' eye health, is also making the journey home. These experiments, conducted in the unique microgravity environment of the ISS, offer insights that cannot be replicated on Earth, promising breakthroughs in medicine, materials science, and our understanding of long-duration spaceflight. The successful undocking and return underscore the vital role of the ISS as a platform for scientific advancement and a stepping stone for future human exploration. NASA is providing live coverage of the undocking, which began at 11:45 a.m. EDT today.

## Peering into the Universe's Infancy: Early Galaxies Revealed

In a significant leap for cosmology, an international research team has achieved the first direct detection of star-forming gas in galaxies as they existed just 700 to 800 million years after the Big Bang. Published on June 15, this groundbreaking study utilized the Atacama Large Millimeter/submillimeter Array (ALMA) to detect the [O I] 145 µm emission line in four typical star-forming galaxies. This emission line is a direct tracer of neutral gas, which is the raw material for star formation.

Previously, telescopes like the James Webb Space Telescope (JWST) and Hubble Space Telescope (HST) could observe stars and hot gas in these distant, early galaxies, but directly tracing the neutral gas component remained a challenge. The ALMA observations have overcome this, allowing researchers to model the physical conditions within this neutral gas. What they found paints a fascinating picture: these early galaxies were compact and incredibly dense sites of star formation, with gas densities comparable to the most vigorously star-forming systems known, called starburst galaxies. However, the intensity of the radiation field was moderately lower than in starburst galaxies. This discovery provides crucial details about how galaxies formed and grew during the universe's first billion years, a period when the cosmos was rapidly evolving from a hot, dense plasma to the structured universe we observe today. Understanding these foundational processes is key to unlocking the full story of cosmic evolution.

## Astrobotic Unveils Griffin-1 Lander for Future Lunar Outposts

Looking ahead to humanity's return to the Moon, Astrobotic, a Pittsburgh-based company, unveiled its Griffin-1 lunar lander on June 15. This lander is a critical component of NASA's Commercial Lunar Payload Services (CLPS) program and is contracted to deliver several research and technology demonstrations to the lunar surface, including the FLIP (Flex Lunar Innovation Platform) rover from Astrolab.

The Griffin-1 mission, targeting a late 2026 launch aboard a SpaceX Falcon Heavy rocket, represents a significant step towards establishing a permanent human presence on the Moon. Astrobotic CEO John Thornton highlighted the Griffin-1 as the "first infrastructure-class lander going to the surface of the moon," emphasizing its role in building a lunar base. This mission is part of NASA's broader efforts, under the Artemis program, to not just visit the Moon, but to stay. Unlike the Apollo missions, which were focused on short-duration visits, Artemis aims for long-term habitation and sustainable exploration. The Griffin-1's ability to carry substantial payloads, including a rover, is essential for constructing and supplying future lunar outposts. The integration of payloads is expected to conclude this week, followed by environmental testing at NASA's Jet Propulsion Laboratory. This ongoing development showcases the collaborative spirit between NASA and commercial partners, accelerating our journey back to and eventually, beyond, the Moon.
