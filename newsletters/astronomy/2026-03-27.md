## Witnessing Worlds in the Making

Imagine looking back in time to see our own Solar System forming, billions of years ago. That's precisely the kind of insight a team of astronomers has offered us with the groundbreaking discovery of a second planet actively forming around the young star WISPIT 2. Detected using the European Southern Observatory's (ESO) Very Large Telescope in Chile, this newly confirmed gas giant, named WISPIT 2c, is estimated to be a mere 5 million years old and boasts a mass about ten times that of Jupiter.

WISPIT 2c orbits its host star four times closer than its sibling, WISPIT 2b, which was discovered just last year by the same research team. The star system, located approximately 437 light-years away in the constellation of the Eagle, is only the second known young, multi-planet system where planets are still embedded within their multi-ringed dust disk. Observing such a system provides an unparalleled "best look into our own past," as lead author Chloe Lawlor noted. The presence of distinct gaps and rings in the disk further suggests that even more planets might be taking shape, sculpted by the gravitational dance of these nascent worlds. This direct observation of planet formation in action is crucial, offering empirical data to refine our theories on how planets, including our own, come to be.

## Unmasking Gamma Cassiopeiae's Secret

For over five decades, the brilliant, naked-eye star Gamma Cassiopeiae has puzzled astronomers with its unusually powerful and extreme X-ray emissions. This star, a prominent fixture in the constellation Cassiopeia, produces X-rays forty times stronger and hotter than expected from a typical massive star. The mystery has finally been unraveled thanks to the highly precise observations from Japan's XRISM space telescope. Scientists have pinpointed the source: a hidden white dwarf companion star.

A white dwarf is the dense, super-compact remnant of a star like our Sun, after it has exhausted its nuclear fuel. In the case of Gamma Cassiopeiae, this diminutive companion is actively pulling in material from its massive, rapidly spinning partner, creating immense heat and generating the observed X-rays. This discovery not only solves a long-standing astronomical enigma but also confirms the existence of a specific type of binary star system that had been predicted but never definitively observed. The plasma responsible for these intense X-rays reaches temperatures exceeding 100 million degrees Celsius, and XRISM's Resolve instrument was able to show that the velocity changes of this hot plasma followed the orbital motion of the white dwarf, providing the direct evidence needed. It's a testament to how new observational capabilities can crack even the most stubborn cosmic cold cases.

## Charting Humanity's Next Steps: Moon to Mars

The ambition for human exploration beyond Earth orbit continues to gather momentum, with significant developments shaping the future of our presence in space. NASA's Artemis II mission, set to carry astronauts on a circumlunar journey, is now targeted for launch no earlier than Wednesday, April 1, with pre-launch events for the crew commencing today at Kennedy Space Center. This mission represents a critical step in humanity's return to the Moon, paving the way for future lunar landings.

In a strategic shift, NASA is reportedly shelving the planned Gateway lunar-orbiting station in favor of establishing a permanent base directly on the Moon's surface. This pivot underscores a commitment to sustained lunar presence, leveraging commercial landers to deliver more and heavier payloads to the surface. Looking further afield, NASA has also announced plans for a nuclear-powered mission to Mars by 2028. This revolutionary nuclear propulsion technology holds the promise of dramatically reducing transit times to the Red Planet, mitigating health risks for astronauts, and improving mission efficiency compared to traditional chemical or solar power. These bold steps, from immediate lunar missions to advanced interplanetary travel, signal a new era in space exploration, moving us closer to becoming a truly multi-planetary species.
