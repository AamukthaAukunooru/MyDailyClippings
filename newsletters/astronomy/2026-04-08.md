What a week it's been for our cosmic pursuits! From humanity's latest journey around our celestial neighbor to the astounding discovery of a star that harks back to the universe's infancy, and even the dramatic end of a much-anticipated comet, the cosmos continues to surprise and inspire. These recent developments remind us of the boundless wonders awaiting our exploration and the profound insights we gain into our origins.

## Artemis II Makes History Around the Moon

In a monumental leap for human space exploration, NASA's Artemis II crew successfully completed their lunar flyby on Monday, April 6th, marking humanity's first return to the Moon since the Apollo era. The four-person crew aboard the Orion capsule — Americans Reid Wiseman, Victor Glover, Christina Koch, and Canadian Jeremy Hansen — not only made a close approach to the Moon but also surpassed the distance record for human spaceflight set by Apollo 13 in April 1970.

The Orion capsule, named "Integrity," came as close as 4,066 miles (6,543 kilometers) to the Moon's surface, whipping past it at speeds up to 3,139 mph (5,052 kph) relative to the Moon. During their six-hour flyby, the astronauts were treated to magnificent views of the Moon's far side, a perspective few humans have ever witnessed. Canadian astronaut Jeremy Hansen expressed his awe, remarking, "It is blowing my mind what you can see with the naked eye from the moon right now. It is just unbelievable." The crew even managed to capture both the Moon and Earth in the same shot, a breathtaking testament to their incredible journey.

This mission is more than just a record-breaking flight; it's a critical step towards landing astronauts near the Moon's south pole within the next two years as part of the broader Artemis program. The crew is now on a four-day return journey, with a splashdown in the Pacific expected on Friday, April 10th. This ambitious endeavor not only pushes the boundaries of human endurance and technology but also reignites our collective dream of venturing further into the solar system, setting the stage for a sustained human presence on the Moon and, eventually, Mars.

## A Glimpse into the Early Universe: The Most Pristine Star

Astronomers have made an extraordinary discovery, identifying the most chemically pristine star ever observed, named SDSS J0715-7334. Announced on April 7th, this ancient star offers an unprecedented window into the universe's earliest moments. What makes this star so unique is its incredibly low "metal" content – in astronomical terms, "metals" refer to all elements heavier than hydrogen and helium. SDSS J0715-7334 contains less than 0.005 percent of the Sun's metal content, possessing only half the heavy elements of the previous record holder and notably low levels of iron and carbon. It is also 40 times more metal-poor than the most iron-poor star previously identified.

This extreme lack of heavier elements strongly suggests that SDSS J0715-7334 formed very early in cosmic history, before successive generations of stars had time to forge and disperse these elements through supernovae. Located approximately 80,000 light-years from Earth, observations combining data from the Sloan Digital Sky Survey-V (SDSS-V) and the European Space Agency's Gaia mission indicate that this star likely formed outside the Milky Way before being drawn into our galaxy.

The discovery of such a primordial star is akin to finding a fossil from the dawn of time. It provides invaluable insights into the composition of the universe shortly after the Big Bang and the processes of star and galaxy formation in those nascent stages. By studying stars like SDSS J0715-7334, scientists can test and refine models of cosmic evolution, helping us understand how the universe transitioned from a simple soup of hydrogen and helium to the complex, element-rich cosmos we inhabit today.

## Comet MAPS' Fiery Demise

The celestial spectacle many skywatchers had eagerly anticipated, Comet C/2026 A1 (MAPS), appears to have met a dramatic end. After sweeping exceedingly close to the Sun on April 4th, the sungrazing comet likely disintegrated due to the intense solar heat and gravitational forces.

Comet MAPS, first spotted in January, belonged to the Kreutz sungrazer family, known for their perilous close encounters with our star. It made a hairpin turn around the Sun, passing just 99,400 miles (160,000 km) from the Sun's visible surface – a scorching distance far closer than Earth's orbit. While some comets survive such fiery trials, observations from the SOHO spacecraft's LASCO C3 coronagraph showed no reappearance of the comet on its expected trajectory. Instead, only a fan of debris was observed heading in the opposite direction, strongly indicating its breakup.

The disintegration of Comet MAPS, while disappointing for those hoping for a "Great Comet," provides valuable data for scientists studying the composition and resilience of these icy wanderers. Comets are essentially time capsules from the early solar system, and their interactions with the Sun offer clues about the conditions and materials that existed billions of years ago. The intense solar radiation and tidal forces experienced during a perihelion passage can rip apart a comet, but the exact mechanisms and conditions for survival or destruction are still areas of active research. This event serves as a powerful reminder of the dynamic and often destructive forces at play in our solar system.
