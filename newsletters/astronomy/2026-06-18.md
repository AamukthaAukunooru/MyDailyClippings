## A Cosmic Anomaly: The Third Galaxy Devoid of Dark Matter

In a discovery that sends ripples through our understanding of cosmic architecture, astronomers have identified a third galaxy, dubbed DF9, that appears to contain little to no dark matter. This remarkable finding, announced on June 16, strengthens the case that such "dark matter-deficient" galaxies are not isolated quirks but potentially products of a rare, violent process. Dark matter, an enigmatic substance that doesn't interact with light, is thought to make up about 27% of the universe's mass, providing the gravitational glue that holds most galaxies together. Almost every galaxy we observe is dominated by it, making these exceptions profoundly significant.

DF9 was found alongside two other unusual galaxies, DF2 and DF4, forming a strange linear structure across space. Researchers believe this trio may have been forged during a high-speed, cataclysmic collision between galaxies, a rare event that could have stripped away the gas (ordinary matter) from its surrounding dark matter halo. Imagine two massive cosmic dancers colliding, and in the chaos, one sheds its invisible, gravitational cloak. The scale of this event is immense, involving entire galaxies, each spanning tens of thousands of light-years. This discovery provides some of the clearest evidence yet that dark matter behaves as a physical substance, rather than merely an effect of an alternative theory of gravity, particularly at the dwarf-galaxy scale where these theories are often debated. It offers a powerful new avenue for studying one of the universe's greatest mysteries and reshaping our understanding of galaxy formation.

## Homecoming: SpaceX Dragon Delivers Vital Science from Orbit

Back in our own celestial neighborhood, the SpaceX Dragon cargo spacecraft successfully completed its 34th commercial resupply services mission to the International Space Station (ISS), splashing down off the coast of California on June 17. This unpiloted freighter returned with a staggering nearly 6,500 pounds of crew cargo and science experiments, marking one of the most research-packed returns in the program's history.

The cargo includes a fascinating array of investigations that could have profound implications. Among the returning samples are bioprinted organ and cartilage tissue, offering insights into human health and disease in microgravity. Data on improving cryogenic fuel storage, crucial for future deep-space missions to the Moon and Mars, also made the journey back. Furthermore, DNA-inspired materials aimed at developing new cancer treatments, an ocular imaging device for monitoring astronaut eye health, and components for the station's life support systems were safely retrieved. The sheer volume and diversity of these experiments underscore the ISS's role as a vital orbiting laboratory. Each pound of returned research represents countless hours of dedicated work by scientists and astronauts, with the potential to shape not only the future of space exploration but also to yield breakthroughs that benefit life here on Earth.

## Martian Ballet: Dust Devils Dance Across Mamers Valles

Meanwhile, our robotic scout, the European Space Agency's Mars Express, has delivered a captivating glimpse into the dynamic Martian atmosphere. On June 17, the mission captured images of dozens of active dust devils swirling across the Mamers Valles region of Mars. These tornado-like whirlwinds, though familiar phenomena in arid regions on Earth, are far grander in scale on the Red Planet.

Dust devils form when the Martian surface warms under the Sun, causing the air directly above it to heat up and rise, creating swirling columns that lift dust high into the atmosphere. On Mars, these ephemeral structures can tower up to eight kilometers (about 5 miles) high, traverse kilometers of terrain, and reach impressive speeds of 45 meters per second (over 100 mph). Mamers Valles itself is a vast, labyrinthine system of canyons and valleys, stretching for around 1000 kilometers (over 600 miles) and reaching depths of 1.2 kilometers (0.75 miles). The presence of so many dust devils in this region offers valuable insights into the planet's atmospheric dynamics, wind patterns, and surface-atmosphere interactions. Studying these Martian whirlwinds helps scientists understand how dust is transported across the planet, influencing its climate and even the visibility for future human missions. It's a vivid reminder that even on a seemingly desolate world, nature's forces are constantly at play, shaping the landscape in spectacular, if dusty, ways.
