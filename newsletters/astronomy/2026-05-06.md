## Unveiling Tatooine-like Worlds: A Bounty of Circumbinary Planets

Imagine a sunset not with one star dipping below the horizon, but two, painting the sky with a double glow. Such "Tatooine-like" worlds, officially known as circumbinary planets, have long captured our imagination, but they remain a rarity in our observational catalog. Until now. Astronomers have recently identified 27 new potential planets orbiting around two stars, more than doubling the previously known count of roughly 18 circumbinary planets. These fascinating candidates, ranging from Neptune-sized to ten times the mass of Jupiter, were spotted using data from NASA's Transiting Exoplanet Survey Satellite (TESS).

The conventional method for finding exoplanets, called the transit method, involves observing a dip in a star's brightness as a planet passes in front of it. While effective for single-star systems, this becomes incredibly complex with two stars. The new discovery leveraged a clever technique focusing on subtle changes in the *timing* of the stars' mutual eclipses as they orbit each other. The presence of an unseen planet can subtly perturb these stellar dance rhythms, revealing its existence. These newly identified worlds are incredibly distant, ranging from 650 to 18,000 light-years from Earth. This explosion in potential circumbinary planets is significant because more than half of the stars in our galaxy exist in binary or multiple star systems. Understanding how planets form and persist in such dynamically challenging environments broadens our understanding of planetary formation and the sheer diversity of worlds that might exist, including those potentially harboring life in truly extreme conditions.

## JWST Peers Directly at an Exoplanet's Rocky Face

For the first time ever, the James Webb Space Telescope (JWST) has directly analyzed the surface of an exoplanet, offering an unprecedented glimpse beneath any potential atmospheric veil. The target of this groundbreaking observation was LHS 3844 b, a "super-Earth" approximately 30% larger than our home planet and located about 50 light-years away. Unlike most exoplanet studies that focus on analyzing atmospheric compositions, JWST's Mid-Infrared Instrument (MIRI) was used to measure the heat emitted directly from the planet's surface during its secondary eclipses—when the planet passes behind its star.

The findings paint a picture of a dark, airless, rocky world, strikingly similar to Mercury in our own solar system. LHS 3844 b is tidally locked, meaning one side perpetually faces its star, reaching scorching temperatures of around 725 degrees Celsius (1,340 degrees Fahrenheit), while the other remains in perpetual darkness. Crucially, the observations showed no evidence of plate tectonics or a significant atmosphere, which are thought to be key ingredients for long-term habitability on Earth. This direct interpretation of a distant planet's geology marks a monumental leap in exoplanet characterization. It demonstrates JWST's incredible sensitivity and capability to move beyond just detecting exoplanets and their atmospheres, allowing us to begin deciphering the geological processes shaping worlds far beyond our solar system. This is a critical step in understanding the true diversity of planetary environments and what factors truly contribute to a planet's potential for life.

## Forging a Faster Path to Mars with Advanced Thrusters

Our ambitions to send humans to Mars are grand, but the journey itself presents immense challenges, particularly regarding travel time and efficiency. Exciting news from NASA brings us closer to overcoming these hurdles, as a powerful new electromagnetic thruster has successfully completed initial tests. This innovative propulsion system, which runs on lithium metal vapor, achieved a remarkable 120 kilowatts of power during testing at NASA's Jet Propulsion Laboratory, marking the highest power level for an electric propulsion system of this type in the United States in years.

Unlike traditional chemical rockets that burn propellant, this electromagnetic thruster uses electromagnetic fields to accelerate ions, providing a more sustained and efficient push. This "electric propulsion" could dramatically reshape how we travel across the solar system. Current missions to Mars typically take between seven and ten months for a one-way trip, with astronauts needing to wait for optimal planetary alignment for their return, stretching a round trip to nearly three years. While this new thruster is still in development, the goal is to scale it up to between 500 kilowatts and one megawatt in the coming years, and eventually to four megawatts. Paired with a nuclear power source, such advanced engines could significantly reduce launch mass and carry heavier payloads, including the vital supplies needed for human crews on deep-space missions. This successful test is a crucial step toward developing the technology that could slash travel times to Mars, making human exploration of the Red Planet a swifter and more feasible reality.
