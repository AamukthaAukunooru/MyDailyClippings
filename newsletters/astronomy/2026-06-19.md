Hello, curious minds!

Today's "My Daily Clippings" brings us on a journey from the very dawn of the universe to surprising discoveries in our cosmic neighborhood. The sheer power of our scientific instruments continues to peel back layers of cosmic history and reveal the incredible diversity of worlds beyond our own. From peering back 13 billion years to uncover a galactic "cosmic fossil" to finding unexpected salty clouds on a distant, cool world, and even observing a delightful wobble in a nearby asteroid, the universe is constantly reminding us of its boundless wonders and intricate mechanisms.

## Unveiling a Cosmic Fossil: The Most Primitive Galaxy Yet

Imagine gazing back in time, almost to the very beginning. That's precisely what the James Webb Space Telescope (JWST) has allowed astronomers to do, identifying LAP1-B, the most chemically primitive galaxy ever observed. This incredible "cosmic fossil" dates back to just 800 million years after the Big Bang, meaning the light we're seeing today left it 13 billion years ago. For context, our universe is estimated to be about 13.8 billion years old.

What makes LAP1-B so special is its incredibly sparse composition of "metals"—a term astronomers use for any element heavier than hydrogen and helium. The early universe was primarily a soup of these two lightest elements. Heavier elements, like the carbon and oxygen essential for life, were forged much later within the hearts of the first stars and scattered into space when those stars exploded. The apparent lack of oxygen in LAP1-B's spectrum suggests it's an extremely metal-poor system, just as theories predict for the earliest galaxies. To capture this faint, ancient glow, researchers employed a spectacular trick of cosmic physics: gravitational lensing. They used a closer galaxy cluster, MACS J0416, as a colossal magnifying glass, which warped spacetime and magnified LAP1-B's light by a factor of 100. This discovery provides crucial insights into Population III stars, the very first generation of stars, and helps us understand how the universe began to enrich itself with the building blocks of everything we see today. It's a direct window into the forging of the elements that eventually led to planets, and even us.

## The "Pink Planet" Hides a Salty Secret

Moving closer to home, yet still far, far away, new observations from the James Webb Space Telescope have unveiled a remarkable atmospheric phenomenon on the enigmatic "Pink Planet," formally known as GJ504b. This planetary-mass companion, located 57 light-years from Earth, has long intrigued astronomers due to its faint, rosy haze and unusually cold temperature for a directly imaged exoplanet, hovering around a balmy 550 degrees Fahrenheit.

For over a decade, its atmosphere remained a mystery, too dim to be fully analyzed by ground-based telescopes. But JWST's incredible infrared vision pierced through the haze, revealing an atmosphere filled with exotic chemistry, including, to everyone's surprise, clouds made of salt. This marks some of the first direct evidence for salt clouds in the atmosphere of a cold object, a phenomenon theorized over 15 years ago but never before confirmed. The composition of these clouds, likely composed of elements like sodium and potassium chloride, is unlike anything seen in our own solar system, where clouds are typically made of water ice, ammonia ice, or methane. This discovery is a significant step in understanding the diverse atmospheric processes on exoplanets, especially those that are colder and fainter, pushing the boundaries of what we thought possible for planetary atmospheres. It challenges our assumptions about what ingredients can form clouds and provides new avenues for studying the complex meteorology of worlds far beyond our reach.

## Lucy Mission's First Taste: A Wobbling "Peanut" Asteroid

Our cosmic journey now brings us back to our solar system, with an exciting update from NASA's Lucy mission. This pioneering spacecraft, on its way to explore the Trojan asteroids near Jupiter, made a crucial test flyby of the main belt asteroid Donaldjohanson on April 20, 2025. The results, just now being fully analyzed, are already fascinating.

Lucy's observations confirmed that Donaldjohanson, a small asteroid only half a mile in diameter, has an elongated, "peanut" shape, with two lobes connected by a narrower neck. But the biggest surprise came from its rotation: Donaldjohanson isn't spinning steadily but rather wobbles! It rotates end-over-end once every 10.5 Earth days while simultaneously wobbling around its horizontal axis once every 26.5 days. This complex, dual-axis rotation is a delightful quirk that gives us a deeper understanding of how these ancient space rocks behave. Furthermore, Lucy detected iron-rich clay minerals on the asteroid's surface. The presence of these clays is a strong indicator that liquid water was present on Donaldjohanson in the distant past. These findings suggest the asteroid likely formed from fragments of a larger, carbon- and water-rich asteroid that broke apart after a collision about 155 million years ago. This "test run" for Lucy's primary mission provides invaluable data, giving us a tantalizing preview of the diversity and history locked within the asteroids that hold clues to the early solar system.
