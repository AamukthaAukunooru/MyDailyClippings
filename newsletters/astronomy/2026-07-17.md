## First Atmosphere Detected on a Habitable, Rocky Exoplanet

For the first time ever, astronomers have detected evidence of an atmosphere on a rocky, Earth-like planet orbiting within the habitable zone of its host star. This groundbreaking finding, announced yesterday, focuses on exoplanet LHS 1140 b, located approximately 48 light-years away. It's a true milestone in the search for life beyond our solar system, moving us beyond merely finding potentially habitable worlds to actually characterizing their environments.

LHS 1140 b orbits a cool red dwarf star, and its position within the star's habitable zone means temperatures could allow for the existence of liquid water on its surface, a key ingredient for life as we know it. While astronomers have discovered thousands of exoplanets, including many rocky worlds in habitable zones, confirming the presence of an atmosphere has remained a significant challenge.

Using the Warm Infrared Echelle, or WINERED, spectrograph at the Magellan Observatory in Chile, a team of scientists, including Harvard University's Collin Cherubim and Carnegie astronomers, detected helium escaping from LHS 1140 b. This detection provides compelling evidence that the planet possesses an atmosphere that has likely persisted for over three billion years. As lead author Collin Cherubim emphasized, an atmosphere is crucial for a planet to support life, and this is the first time such a discovery has been made for a rocky planet in another star's habitable zone. This finding represents a critical step in understanding the conditions necessary for life and offers a tantalizing glimpse into the diverse array of planetary environments awaiting our further exploration.

## Securing Our Orbital Future and Pioneering New Technologies

In other significant developments, the International Space Station (ISS) program received a vital commitment this week, and NASA is pushing forward with innovative technologies for future deep-space missions. In meetings held at the Baikonur Cosmodrome, Roscosmos Director General Dmitry Bakanov agreed that Russia would support ISS operations through 2030, aligning with the commitments of other international partners. This extension is crucial for the continued scientific research and international collaboration aboard the orbiting laboratory, ensuring its operations for several more years. Additionally, the agreement includes the continuation of "seat-swaps," where Russian cosmonauts fly on NASA missions and NASA astronauts on Soyuz spacecraft, without direct exchange of funds, fostering ongoing cooperation.

Meanwhile, NASA is actively laying the groundwork for ambitious missions to the Moon and Mars, announcing the selection of 41 commercial technology projects from 37 American companies. These collaborations, part of the agency's 2025 Announcement of Collaboration Opportunity (ACO), aim to accelerate innovations in critical areas like space transportation, planetary surface operations, and the infrastructure needed for long-term lunar exploration. These technologies range from power systems designed to operate in the Moon's permanently shadowed regions to wireless power transfer and advanced payload attachment systems for spacecraft. By partnering with commercial industry, NASA seeks to rapidly develop essential capabilities while simultaneously fostering a robust commercial space economy. The agency also recently selected SpaceX to provide laser communications for the upcoming Artemis III mission, which will offer unprecedented live imagery from the Orion spacecraft as astronauts prepare to return to the Moon.

## The Busy Lanes of Low Earth Orbit

The skies above us remain a bustling highway, with recent launches continuing to shape our access to and understanding of space. Just yesterday, a SpaceX Falcon 9 rocket successfully launched 21 satellites for the U.S. military's advanced new "Tranche 1 Transport Layer" (T1TL) constellation from Vandenberg Space Force Base in California. This mission is part of an ongoing effort to build a resilient and advanced data transport network in low Earth orbit.

Adding to the excitement, SpaceX's colossal Starship megarocket was slated for its 13th test flight just hours after the Falcon 9 launch. Starship, the largest and most powerful launch vehicle ever constructed, is central to SpaceX's long-term ambitions for lunar and Martian exploration, as well as deploying its next-generation Starlink satellites. These frequent launches highlight the increasing pace of space activities, driven by both national security interests and the burgeoning commercial space sector, pushing the boundaries of what's possible in spaceflight.
