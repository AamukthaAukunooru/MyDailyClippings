## Artemis II Lifts Off, Charting Humanity's Return to the Moon

Yesterday, April 1, 2026, at 6:24 p.m. ET, the world watched as NASA's Artemis II mission launched from Kennedy Space Center in Florida, carrying four astronauts on a historic journey around the Moon. This momentous event marks the first time humans have ventured beyond low Earth orbit in over half a century, since Apollo 17 in 1972. The mighty Space Launch System (SLS) rocket, the most powerful rocket ever built, propelled the Orion spacecraft and its crew – Commander Reid Wiseman, Pilot Victor Glover, Mission Specialist Christina Koch, and Canadian Space Agency astronaut Jeremy Hansen – into space.

This 10-day mission is not a lunar landing, but a critical test flight designed to validate the Orion spacecraft's life-support systems, deep-space navigation, and manual piloting techniques essential for future lunar landings. The crew will slingshot around Earth before heading towards the Moon, reaching a distance of approximately 250,000 miles (400,000 km) from our planet. They are set to travel farther into space than any humans have before, offering them a unique perspective of the lunar far side, including views of the coveted lunar south pole, a target for future Artemis landings. The mission is a crucial stepping stone in NASA's ambitious Artemis program, which aims to establish a sustained human presence on the Moon and pave the way for eventual missions to Mars. The National Science Foundation's Green Bank Telescope (GBT) is even playing a role, providing enhanced tracking and communication support for the Orion spacecraft during its journey. The launch was met with widespread anticipation and relief, as ground crews overcame minor battery and range issues to ensure a smooth liftoff.

## Unveiling a Century-Old Stellar Secret with SPHEREx

Beyond our solar system, a century-old mystery surrounding the nova GK Persei has just yielded new clues, thanks to observations from NASA's newest space telescope, SPHEREx. More than 125 years after its spectacular outburst, astronomers have discovered that the ancient nova is enveloped by a previously unknown cloud of molecular hydrogen, stretching approximately 8-by-5 light-years across. This molecular hydrogen, the most abundant molecule in the universe, traces the full extent of a bipolar, two-lobed nebula that has long puzzled scientists.

The nova GK Persei, located in the constellation Perseus, exploded in 1901, becoming one of the brightest stars in the night sky. While the shell of debris from that explosion is still visible and spans about 1 light-year, the much larger bipolar nebula surrounding it has baffled researchers. Some theorized it was an ancient planetary nebula from an earlier stage of the star's life, while others remained unconvinced. This new discovery, published in *The Astrophysical Journal Letters*, suggests a direct connection between the molecular hydrogen and the nebula, offering vital insights into how the nova outburst interacts with its surrounding environment and shedding light on the evolution of classical novae. This finding reminds us that even well-studied celestial objects can hold profound secrets, waiting for the right instrument and keen minds to uncover them.

## Perseverance Rover Unearths Ancient Martian River Delta

Closer to home, the Mars 2020 Perseverance rover has continued its groundbreaking work in the Jezero Crater, revealing evidence for a river delta that existed on Mars as early as 4.2 billion years ago. This discovery, based on ground-penetrating radar (GPR) data collected by the rover, significantly pushes back the timeline for flowing water on the Red Planet, extending the period of potential habitability for Jezero.

The surface of Mars is replete with traces of a watery past, including ancient river channels, deltas, and paleolakes. However, the Perseverance rover's detailed analysis of the Margin unit within the Jezero crater has uncovered a well-preserved paleolandscape with distinct river and deltaic features. These findings suggest an environment capable of preserving biosignatures, further fueling the excitement in the search for past life on Mars. The ability of the rover's RIMFAX instrument to distinguish between different geological regions provides an unprecedented look into the layered history of this intriguing Martian landscape. This ongoing exploration by Perseverance offers a tantalizing glimpse into a Mars that was once far more dynamic and potentially life-sustaining than the arid world we see today.
