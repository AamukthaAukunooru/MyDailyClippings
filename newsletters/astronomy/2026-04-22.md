## The Lyrids' Fiery Dance

Tonight, and into the predawn hours of tomorrow, our planet is passing through a stream of ancient cosmic debris, giving rise to the annual Lyrid meteor shower. Active from April 14 to 30, the shower is peaking right around April 22nd and 23rd, offering a splendid display for those gazing skyward. These "shooting stars" are not stars at all, but tiny fragments, mostly no larger than grains of sand, shed by Comet C/1861 G1 Thatcher over centuries. As Earth intercepts this trail, these particles slam into our atmosphere at speeds of approximately 177,000 kilometers per hour (110,000 miles per hour), burning up from the friction and creating luminous streaks across the night sky.

The Lyrids are one of the oldest recorded meteor showers, with observations dating back over 2,600 years, making them a recurring spectacle deeply embedded in human history. While not as prolific as some other showers, the Lyrids typically produce around 10-20 meteors per hour during their peak under dark skies, and are known for their fast and sometimes bright trails. This year, conditions are particularly favorable for Northern Hemisphere observers, with the Moon being a waxing crescent, providing minimal interference to the celestial show. To catch a glimpse, look towards the constellation Lyra, near its bright star Vega, in the eastern sky after 10 p.m. local time and through the predawn hours. It's a humbling reminder of our journey through the vastness of space, constantly interacting with the remnants of distant comets.

## Artemis II Returns, Paving the Way for Lunar Future

Just a short while ago, on April 11, 2026, humanity took another monumental leap forward as the Artemis II mission successfully concluded its historic journey around the Moon, with its Orion re-entry capsule, "Integrity," splashing down safely in the Pacific Ocean. This mission, which launched on April 1st, carried four astronauts—NASA astronauts Reid Wiseman, Victor Glover, Christina Koch, and Canadian Space Agency astronaut Jeremy Hansen—on a nearly 10-day lunar flyby, marking the first time humans have traveled beyond low-Earth orbit in over 50 years.

The crew not only orbited our nearest celestial neighbor but also broke the distance record for human spaceflight on April 6th, surpassing the Apollo 13 mission by traveling more than 406,000 kilometers (252,000 miles) from Earth. During their journey, they captured breathtaking images, including a poignant "Earthset" as our home planet dipped below the lunar horizon. The success of Artemis II is a critical step in NASA's ambitious plan to return humans to the Moon, with the subsequent Artemis III mission aiming for a lunar landing, and ultimately, to pave the way for human exploration of Mars. The recent rollout of the core stage for the Artemis III Space Launch System rocket underscores the rapid progress being made toward establishing a sustained human presence beyond Earth.

## Unveiling More Worlds: Exoplanet Updates

Our relentless quest to understand planets beyond our solar system continues to yield fascinating results. The past few weeks have brought exciting updates in exoplanet research, expanding our catalog of distant worlds. As of April 16, 2026, the exoplanet archive was updated with new additions, including WISPIT 2 c and CWISEP J193518.59-154620.3 b. WISPIT 2 c is particularly notable as it provides another valuable opportunity to observe the formation of a young planetary system, potentially offering insights into how our own solar system came to be.

Beyond individual discoveries, scientists are also categorizing new types of planets. A recent study, published in *Nature Astronomy* in mid-March, identified a new class of "molten planet" exemplified by L 98-59 d. This exoplanet, about 1.6 times the size of Earth and orbiting a small red star 35 light-years away, appears to harbor vast amounts of sulfur deep within a permanent ocean of magma. Observations from the James Webb Space Telescope and ground-based observatories revealed its unusually low density and significant hydrogen sulfide in its atmosphere, suggesting a unique evolutionary path where chemical exchanges between its molten interior and atmosphere have shaped its present state. These pungent, magma-ocean worlds could be more common than we once thought, further diversifying the incredible array of planetary architectures we are discovering across the galaxy. Each new world we find, whether a young system in formation or a bizarre molten sphere, refines our understanding of planetary evolution and the conditions necessary for life, reminding us of the boundless wonders awaiting discovery in the cosmos.
