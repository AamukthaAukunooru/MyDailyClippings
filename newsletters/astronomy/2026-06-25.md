## Unveiling the Universe's Ancient Past with Comet 3I/ATLAS

Imagine an object nearly as old as the universe itself, hurtling through the cosmos for billions of years before making a fleeting visit to our solar neighborhood. That's the story emerging from new observations of interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS by the James Webb Space Telescope (JWST). Scientists have determined that this enigmatic icy wanderer is an astounding 10 to 12 billion years old, making it almost three times older than our own solar system. This incredible age suggests 3I/ATLAS formed in the very early universe, a relic from a time of intense star formation.

What makes this discovery even more fascinating is the comet's unique chemical fingerprint. JWST's Near-Infrared Spectrograph (NIRSpec) revealed an elemental composition unlike any solar system body we've studied. Specifically, the water in 3I/ATLAS is significantly enriched in deuterium, a heavier isotope of hydrogen, at a level more than an order of magnitude higher than in known comets from our own cosmic backyard. Its carbon isotope ratios are also unusual, exceeding typical values found in our solar system, nearby interstellar clouds, and protoplanetary disks. These extreme isotopic signatures point to a formation environment at incredibly cold temperatures, likely below 30 Kelvin (around -243 degrees Celsius), and in a relatively metal-poor region. Studying 3I/ATLAS offers a direct glimpse into the conditions of planetesimal formation around other, ancient stars, providing invaluable insights into the diversity of exoplanet systems and how our own solar system might be unique. This comet is truly a preserved fragment of an ancient planetary system, carrying secrets from the dawn of galactic history.

## Euclid's Dazzling Galactic Heart and the Hunt for Exoplanets

The European Space Agency's (ESA) Euclid telescope has delivered a truly spectacular image, capturing the largest and most detailed picture ever taken of the visible light emanating from the heart of our Milky Way galaxy. This breathtaking mosaic reveals over 60 million stars clustered together in the galactic bulge, a region previously shrouded in dust and difficult to resolve. Euclid, launched in 2023 with the primary goal of mapping the cosmos in 3D to understand dark energy and dark matter, has proven to be an unexpected powerhouse for stellar and planetary discovery.

The telescope's exceptional sensitivity allows it to distinguish individual stars even in this incredibly crowded environment. This capability is not just visually stunning; it holds profound implications for the search for exoplanets. Researchers anticipate that this new data will kickstart a "new age of exoplanet discovery," potentially increasing the number of known exoplanets from thousands to over 100,000 across our galaxy. By precisely measuring the subtle dimming of starlight as planets transit, or pass in front of, their host stars, Euclid's observations will help astronomers confirm these distant worlds and differentiate them from other celestial phenomena like binary star systems. This deep dive into our galaxy's core could revolutionize our understanding of how common planets are in different stellar environments, and how planetary systems form and evolve in the dense heart of a galaxy.

## ISS Spacewalk Preparations: A Robotic Arm's Vital Repair

Closer to home, the International Space Station (ISS) is a hub of activity as astronauts prepare for a crucial spacewalk on June 30. NASA astronauts Chris Williams and Jessica Meir are gearing up to venture outside the orbital outpost to replace a malfunctioned wrist joint on the Canadarm2 robotic arm. This sophisticated 17.6-meter-long robotic arm, a vital piece of equipment for the ISS, has been in operation for over 25 years, assisting with station assembly, cargo transfers, and spacewalk support.

The spacewalk, expected to last about six hours and 40 minutes, will see Williams and Meir meticulously work in the vacuum of space to perform this delicate repair. Preparations have been extensive, with the crew reviewing robotics activities, configuring tools, and inspecting equipment such as tethers, pistol grip tools, and cameras. This isn't Meir and Williams' first spacewalk together; they previously worked on installing a solar array modification kit in March 2026. Beyond the spacewalk, the Expedition 74 crew continues to conduct ongoing biomedical research and advanced technology studies, including investigating how microgravity affects the human circulatory and respiratory systems, and testing artificial intelligence tools to enhance crew efficiency. These human endeavors are essential for maintaining the station and paving the way for future long-duration missions deeper into space.
