Today's AI landscape is shaped by two major capability announcements and the first real-world enforcement of the EU AI Act.

## GPT-5: Multimodal Reasoning Takes Center Stage

OpenAI's GPT-5 launch marks a genuine architectural shift rather than an incremental update. The model natively interleaves vision, audio, and text during its chain-of-thought process — meaning it can reason across modalities simultaneously rather than processing each channel separately.

Early benchmarks show significant improvements on tasks requiring **spatial reasoning**, scientific diagram interpretation, and code generation from visual specifications. The model scores 87% on the MMMU benchmark and sets a new state-of-the-art on GPQA Diamond (graduate-level scientific QA) with 72% accuracy.

Notably, OpenAI has released a reduced-scale version, GPT-5 Mini, at pricing substantially below GPT-4o — a move clearly aimed at maintaining developer adoption against Anthropic's Claude 3.7 and Google's Gemini 2.5 Ultra.

## AlphaFold Expands Into Drug Discovery

Google DeepMind has announced AlphaFold 3's extension to small molecule binding prediction, closing a critical gap in computational drug design. Previous versions excelled at predicting protein structures but struggled with accurately modeling how drug-like small molecules interact with those structures.

The new system, trained on a proprietary dataset of **crystallographic binding data**, reportedly achieves accuracy competitive with expensive physics-based simulation methods at a fraction of the computational cost. Three pharmaceutical partners have already used it to identify novel lead compounds for previously undruggable targets.

## EU AI Act: First Enforcement Actions

The European AI Office has issued its first formal enforcement notices under the EU AI Act, targeting two companies operating high-risk AI systems in hiring and credit scoring without required transparency documentation. Fines of €2.3M and €1.8M respectively represent a signal that enforcement is moving beyond the grace period.

Legal analysts note that the **prohibited practices** provisions — covering real-time biometric surveillance and subliminal manipulation — have yet to be tested, but regulators have confirmed active investigations in at least four cases.
