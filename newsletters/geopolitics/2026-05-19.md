## Middle East: Strait of Hormuz, Iran, and Regional Stability

The delicate situation between the **United States** and **Iran** remains a central focus, marked by ongoing negotiations and a fragile ceasefire that was initially brokered by **Pakistan** on April 8 and later extended by **US President Donald Trump**. **US Energy Secretary Chris Wright** indicated that a diplomatic agreement with **Iran** could emerge "in the next few days," while also warning that the **American** military might intervene if **Iran** continues to disrupt traffic through the strategic **Strait of Hormuz**. **Wright** noted that the Strait is expected to reopen "sometime this summer at the latest". **President Trump** himself commented that **Iran** has "an interest in reaching an agreement" regarding its nuclear program and the ongoing conflict.

In response to the situation, **Iran** has announced the establishment of a new **Persian Gulf Strait Authority** to manage operations in the **Strait of Hormuz**. **First Vice President Mohammad Reza Aref** stated that **Iran** would no longer permit "enemy" military equipment to transit the waterway, citing **Tehran's** sovereign rights. This plan may include the collection of tolls or "fees" for "specialized services," and aims to restrict commercial vessels to countries that have obtained permission. **Iran's Foreign Minister Abbas Araghchi** reportedly affirmed that while **Tehran** does not trust the **United States**, it remains open to continuing negotiations.

Further underscoring the regional dynamics, the **USS Gerald R. Ford** aircraft carrier has returned home after an extended 11-month deployment that included operations in the "Iran war" and support for the mission that led to the capture of former **Venezuelan President Nicolas Maduro**. Meanwhile, **Iraq's** new Prime Minister, **Ali al-Zaidi**, officially assumed office in **Baghdad** following parliamentary approval. The **Israel-Palestine** conflict also saw continued activity, with **Israeli** forces conducting attacks in **Gaza** and **Lebanon**, resulting in casualties, despite a ceasefire agreement being extended. **Hamas** commanders were reportedly killed in **Gaza**. Separately, tens of thousands of pro-Palestinian demonstrators marched across **Europe** to commemorate the 78th anniversary of the **Nakba**.

## Eastern Europe and Shifting Alliances

The conflict in **Ukraine** continues, with **Russia** claiming to have taken control of two more settlements. In a significant diplomatic move, **Russian President Vladimir Putin** is scheduled to visit **China** from May 19-20 at the invitation of **Chinese President Xi Jinping**. This visit is anticipated to further deepen ties between the two nations amid **Moscow's** ongoing isolation over **Ukraine**.

In a broader European context, all 46 member states of the **Council of Europe** adopted the non-binding **Chişinău Declaration** on Friday. This declaration urges the **European Court of Human Rights** and domestic courts to give fuller effect to subsidiarity and the "margin of appreciation" in migration cases.

## Global Economic Geopolitics

The economic dimension of international relations is increasingly prominent, with "geoeconomic confrontation" identified as the most pressing global threat in the short term for 2026, according to a **World Economic Forum** report. The report highlights the growing trend of weaponizing economic tools, such as tariffs, sanctions, investment restrictions, and supply chain controls, as extensions of geopolitical strategy.

In a recent development reflecting these trends, **US President Donald Trump** concluded a three-day visit to **China**. Following the visit, **Beijing** confirmed the procurement of aircraft from **Washington**, and both sides agreed to reduce tariffs on products of "equal scale." **China's Ministry of Commerce** spokesperson also stated that **Beijing** and **Washington** have agreed to establish a **Trade Council** to discuss issues including tariff reductions, investments, and bilateral trade. The **White House** further detailed that **China** agreed to purchase at least $17 billion per year in **US** agricultural products for 2026, 2027, and 2028. This comes as analysts warn that prolonged disruptions in global oil refining, partly due to conflicts involving **Iran** and **Ukraine**, could increase transportation costs and economic pressure worldwide.
